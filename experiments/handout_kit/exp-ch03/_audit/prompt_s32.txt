## Chain rule — statement (manuscript p.11)

**Chain rule.** Suppose `g(x)` is differentiable at `x₀`, and `f(y)` is differentiable
at `y = g(x₀)`. Then `P(x) = f(g(x))` is differentiable at `x₀`, and

`P′(x₀) = f′(g(x₀))·g′(x₀)`.

> (記號正規化：手稿在**陳述與 Def 1／Def 2** 把固定點寫成大寫 `X₀`，在**證明**裡寫小寫 `x₀`／`x`；
> 同一點，seed 一律用 `x₀`／`x`（與 §3.1 及全章一致）——純大小寫差異、無數學影響。)

## Chain rule — proof (manuscript pp.15–20, remainder form)

> 手稿在證明裡以 `x` 代表那個固定點（即陳述中的 `x₀`）；下標 `m₁,m₂` 為兩段斜率、
> `R₁,R₂,R₃` 為三段餘項。逐格對掃描核過。

**Setup (p.15).** Start from `P(x+h) = f(g(x+h))`. Apply **Def 2 to `g` at `x`**:

`g(x+h) = g(x) + m₁h + R₁(h)`,  where `m₁ = g′(x)` and `lim_{h→0} R₁(h)/h = 0`.

So `P(x+h) = f( g(x) + m₁h + R₁(h) )`.

**Apply Def 2 to `f` at `g(x)` (p.16).** With the increment `m₁h + R₁(h)` playing the
role of `h`:

`P(x+h) = f(g(x)) + m₂·[ m₁h + R₁(h) ] + R₂( m₁h + R₁(h) )`,

where `m₂ = f′(g(x))` and `lim_{y→0} R₂(y)/y = 0`.

**Collect terms (p.16).** Regroup the linear part and the leftover:

`P(x+h) = f(g(x)) + m₁m₂·h + [ m₂R₁(h) + R₂(m₁h + R₁(h)) ]`

`        = P(x) + ( g′(x)·f′(g(x)) )·h + R₃(h)`,

where `R₃(h) = m₂R₁(h) + R₂(m₁h + R₁(h))`.
> (手稿在中括號 `[ m₂R₁(h) + R₂(…) ]` 旁有紅字 `m₃` 標注，隨即正式命名為 `R₃(h)`；
> 即此括號＝餘項 `R₃`。`m₁m₂ = g′(x)f′(g(x))`，`f(g(x)) = P(x)`。**[請查核]** 手稿係數
> 順序寫成 `(g′(x)f′(g(x)))h`，與陳述 `f′(g(x))g′(x)` 同值（交換律），④ 保留手稿順序或統一即可。)

So `P(x+h) − P(x) = (g′(x)f′(g(x)))h + R₃(h)`. By **Def 2**, `P` is differentiable at `x`
with `P′(x) = g′(x)f′(g(x))` **provided** `lim_{h→0} R₃(h)/h = 0` — which is what remains
to prove.

**Goal (p.17):** prove `lim_{h→0} R₃(h)/h = 0`, where `R₃(h) = m₂R₁(h) + R₂(m₁h + R₁(h))`.

**Step ① — the easy facts (p.17).** By differentiability of `g` at `x`:

`lim_{h→0} R₁(h)/h = 0`,  and hence  `lim_{h→0} ( m₁h + R₁(h) ) = 0`.

> (`m₂R₁(h)/h = m₂·(R₁(h)/h) → m₂·0 = 0`，`m₂` 為常數。**[請查核]** 手稿把 ε-δ 火力
> 集中在較難的 `R₂(m₁h+R₁(h))/h` 項；`m₂R₁(h)/h → 0` 這一項手稿**未明寫**、由 ① 的
> `R₁(h)/h→0` 隱含。④ 可一句點明（標 `expansion:formula`），不算改手稿。)

**Step ② — the composed remainder term (pp.17–19), ε–δ.**

- **(i)** Since `lim_{y→0} R₂(y)/y = 0`: for given `ε > 0`, there exists `δ > 0` such that
  `| R₂(y)/y | < ε`  for  `0 < |y| < δ`.
- **(ii)** Since `lim_{h→0} ( m₁h + R₁(h) ) = 0`: there exists `α > 0` such that
  `| m₁h + R₁(h) | < δ`  for  `0 < |h| < α`.

Then for `0 < |h| < α`, the manuscript splits into two cases (writing `m₁h+R₁(h)` out **in full**,
no shorthand):

`| R₂(m₁h+R₁(h)) | / |h| = 0`  if `m₁h+R₁(h) = 0`;

`| R₂(m₁h+R₁(h)) | / |h| = ( |m₁h+R₁(h)|/|h| )·( |R₂(m₁h+R₁(h))|/|m₁h+R₁(h)| )`  if `m₁h+R₁(h) ≠ 0`.

From here the manuscript jumps **directly** to (p.19, opening line):

`| R₂(m₁h + R₁(h)) | / |h|  ≤  ( |m₁| + |R₁(h)|/|h| )·ε`.

> **[④ expansion]** 手稿從上方乘積式**直接**寫出此下界，略去兩個標準補步（seed **不**靜默補、忠於手稿跳步）：
> (a) 第二情形 `0 < |m₁h+R₁(h)| < δ`（由 (ii)），故由 (i) 得 `|R₂(m₁h+R₁(h))|/|m₁h+R₁(h)| < ε`；
> (b) `|m₁h+R₁(h)|/|h| = |m₁ + R₁(h)/h| ≤ |m₁| + |R₁(h)|/|h|`（三角不等式）。④ 補這兩步時標 `expansion:formula`。

Since `lim_{h→0} R₁(h)/h = 0`, there exists `α₁ ∈ (0, α)` such that `|R₁(h)|/|h| < 1` for
`0 < |h| < α₁`. Therefore for `|h| ∈ (0, α₁)`:

`| R₂(m₁h + R₁(h)) | / |h|  <  ( |m₁| + 1 )·ε`.

**Conclude (p.20).** 手稿由 `| R₂(m₁h+R₁(h)) | / |h| < (|m₁|+1)ε`（`ε` 任意）**直接**收尾:

`lim_{h→0} R₃(h)/h = 0`.

This completes the proof of the chain rule.

> (seed 補充組裝說明，**非手稿明文**——手稿在 p.20 直接寫出結論：

  <header class="sec-head">
    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">3.2</span>The Chain Rule</h2>
  </header>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — load-bearing intuition: rates multiply because local linear approximations compose; this carries the statement, the Leibniz mnemonic, and the proof strategy at once -->
  <p>Section 3.1 gave us the derivatives of the trigonometric functions, but only in their bare form. We can differentiate \(\sin x\), yet nothing so far reaches \(\sin(x^{2})\) or \(\sqrt{1 + x^{2}}\), where one function is fed <em>into</em> another. Chapter 2 supplied rules for sums, products, and quotients — but not for <em>composition</em>, the operation of applying one function to the output of another. The chain rule fills exactly this gap, and it is the rule that finally completes the differentiation toolkit.</p>

  <p>The idea behind it fits in a single image. Near a point, a differentiable function behaves like its tangent line: it takes a small change in its input and scales it by its slope, up to an error that shrinks faster than the change itself. Now chain two such functions together. If \(g\) scales a small increment \(h\) by \(g'(x)\), and \(f\) then scales <em>that</em> by \(f'(g(x))\), the two magnifications stack — the composite scales \(h\) by the product \(f'(g(x))\,g'(x)\). That product is the chain rule, and the proof at the end of this section is nothing more than the careful check that the two error terms, once chained, are still negligible.</p>

  <p>The manuscript this chapter follows develops the chain rule alongside two results that already belong to Chapter 2 — the product rule (§2.5) and the fact that a differentiable function is continuous (§2.3, Theorem 2.1). We take both as established and use them freely, without re-proving them here.</p>

  <section class="env env-theorem">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Theorem</span><span class="env-num">3.3</span><span class="env-name">The chain rule</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Let \(g\) be differentiable at \(x_{0}\), and let \(f\) be differentiable at \(g(x_{0})\). Then the composition \(P = f \circ g\), given by \(P(x) = f(g(x))\), is differentiable at \(x_{0}\), and</p>
      <p>\[ P'(x_{0}) = f'(g(x_{0}))\, g'(x_{0}). \]</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:figure — chain rule as composed mapping (ROADMAP key figure; registered in exp-ch03/figures.js as "composed-mapping") -->
  <figure class="figure" data-fig="composed-mapping">
    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure 3.2</span> The chain rule as a composed mapping. A small input increment \(h\) at \(x_{0}\) is carried by \(g\) to an increment of about \(g'(x_{0})\,h\) at \(u_{0} = g(x_{0})\), and then by \(f\) to about \(f'(g(x_{0}))\,g'(x_{0})\,h\) at \(y_{0} = f(g(x_{0}))\). The two local slopes multiply — which is why the composite's derivative is their product.</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — the Leibniz form (the working notation), with the mnemonic-not-proof caveat folded in (ROADMAP pitfall) -->
  <section class="env env-remark">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Remark</span><span class="env-num">3.2</span><span class="env-name">The Leibniz form</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Writing \(u = g(x)\) for the inner variable and \(y = f(u)\) for the output, the chain rule takes the memorable Leibniz shape</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}. \]</p>
      <p>This is the form most often used in practice, and it reads as “the rate of \(y\) per \(x\) equals the rate of \(y\) per \(u\) times the rate of \(u\) per \(x\).” The apparent cancellation of \(du\) is a useful memory aid, but it is <em>not</em> a proof: \(\dfrac{dy}{du}\) and \(\dfrac{du}{dx}\) are limits, not fractions with a common factor to strike out. What actually makes the rule true is the argument given below, where the controlled error terms — not a literal \(du/du = 1\) — do the work.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:strategy — chain-rule decomposition (ROADMAP-assigned; the chapter's first Strategy box) -->
  <section class="env env-strategy">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Strategy</span><span class="env-num">3.1</span><span class="env-name">Differentiating a composition</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>To differentiate a composite expression, work from the outside in.</p>
      <ol class="steps">
        <li>Identify the <em>outermost</em> operation — the last thing you would carry out if you evaluated the expression at a number. Call it the outer function \(f\).</li>
        <li>Whatever that operation is applied to is the inner function \(u = g(x)\).</li>
        <li>Differentiate the outer function at the inner one, forming \(f'(g(x))\) — treat the inside as a single block and leave it untouched for now.</li>
        <li>Multiply by the derivative of the inside: the answer is \(f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\).</li>
        <li>If the inside is itself a composition, repeat steps 1–4 on it. Every layer contributes one slope factor, and the factors multiply.</li>
      </ol>
      <p>For \(\sqrt{1 + x^{2}}\), the last operation is the square root, so \(f(u) = \sqrt{u}\) and \(g(x) = 1 + x^{2}\); for \(\sin(x^{2})\), the outer function is \(\sin\) and the inner is \(x^{2}\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Why the rule is true</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — bridge: the rule is usable; to prove it we need the remainder form, which packages differentiability as a linear approximation that composes -->
  <p>The rule is already usable — the rest of this section earns it. To prove that the two slopes really do multiply, we need to handle both functions' linear approximations at once, and for that it helps to repackage what “differentiable” means. Chapter 2 defined \(f\) to be differentiable at \(x_{0}\) when the difference quotient \(\dfrac{f(x_{0}+h) - f(x_{0})}{h}\) has a limit as \(h \to 0\) (§2.2); that limit is \(f'(x_{0})\). The following form says the same thing, but as a statement about approximation by a straight line.</p>

  <section class="env env-definition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Definition</span><span class="env-num">3.1</span><span class="env-name">Differentiability, remainder form</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>A function \(f\) is <em>differentiable</em> at \(x_{0}\) if there is a number \(m\) and a function \(R\) such that</p>
      <p>\[ f(x_{0} + h) = f(x_{0}) + m h + R(h), \qquad \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{R(h)}{h} = 0. \]</p>
      <p>The number \(m\) is the derivative \(f'(x_{0})\).</p>
      <p class="informal">Informally, \(f(x_{0}+h)\) equals its tangent-line value \(f(x_{0}) + m h\) plus an error \(R(h)\) that dies away faster than \(h\) does — the tangent line is a good fit to first order.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — frame the equivalence: the remainder form is not a new notion of derivative, just a repackaging of §2.2 -->
  <p>This is not a new notion of derivative, only a new way of writing the old one.</p>

  <section class="env env-proposition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proposition</span><span class="env-num">3.3</span><span class="env-name">Equivalence of the two forms</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>The limit form of differentiability (§2.2) and the remainder form (Definition 3.1) are equivalent: \(f\) satisfies one at \(x_{0}\) if and only if it satisfies the other, and the number \(m\) is the same in both — namely \(f'(x_{0})\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:formula — the manuscript states only "it is easy to see that Def 1 and Def 2 are equivalent"; we supply the faithful short bidirectional argument -->
  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Suppose first that the limit form holds, with \(\lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{f(x_{0}+h) - f(x_{0})}{h} = m\). Define \(R(h) = f(x_{0}+h) - f(x_{0}) - m h\). Then</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{R(h)}{h} = \frac{f(x_{0}+h) - f(x_{0})}{h} - m \;\longrightarrow\; m - m = 0, \]</p>
      <p>so the remainder form holds with this same \(m\). Conversely, suppose the remainder form holds: \(f(x_{0}+h) = f(x_{0}) + m h + R(h)\) with \(R(h)/h \to 0\). Dividing by \(h\),</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{f(x_{0}+h) - f(x_{0})}{h} = m + \frac{R(h)}{h} \;\longrightarrow\; m + 0 = m, \]</p>
      <p>so the limit form holds, again with the same \(m\). The two forms therefore describe the same property and the same derivative.<span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — orient the proof before the symbols: write both functions in remainder form, substitute, watch the linear parts multiply, collect the rest into one remainder -->
  <p>With the remainder form available, the chain rule comes out by substitution. We expand \(g\) near \(x_{0}\) and \(f\) near \(g(x_{0})\), each as a linear part plus a small remainder; feeding one into the other, the linear parts multiply to give \(g'(x_{0})f'(g(x_{0}))\,h\), and everything left over gathers into a single remainder. The whole proof is then the one estimate that this leftover still vanishes faster than \(h\).</p>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span><span class="env-name">of Theorem 3.3</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>We use the remainder form of differentiability (Definition 3.1), which by Proposition 3.3 is available for any differentiable function. Because \(g\) is differentiable at \(x_{0}\),</p>
      <p>\[ g(x_{0} + h) = g(x_{0}) + m_{1} h + R_{1}(h), \qquad m_{1} = g'(x_{0}), \quad \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{R_{1}(h)}{h} = 0. \]</p>
      <p>Because \(f\) is differentiable at \(g(x_{0})\), we may apply the remainder form to \(f\) with the increment \(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)\) playing the role of \(h\):</p>
      <p>\[ P(x_{0}+h) = f\bigl(g(x_{0}+h)\bigr) = f(g(x_{0})) + m_{2}\bigl[m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)\bigr] + R_{2}\bigl(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)\bigr), \]</p>
      <p>where \(m_{2} = f'(g(x_{0}))\) and \(\lim_{y \to 0} R_{2}(y)/y = 0\). Collecting the term linear in \(h\) and bundling the rest into a single remainder \(R_{3}\),</p>
      <p>\[ P(x_{0}+h) = P(x_{0}) + \bigl(g'(x_{0})\,f'(g(x_{0}))\bigr) h + R_{3}(h), \qquad R_{3}(h) = m_{2} R_{1}(h) + R_{2}\bigl(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)\bigr), \]</p>
      <p>using \(f(g(x_{0})) = P(x_{0})\) and \(m_{1} m_{2} = g'(x_{0})f'(g(x_{0})) = f'(g(x_{0}))g'(x_{0})\), the same product in either order. By the remainder form, this says precisely that \(P\) is differentiable at \(x_{0}\) with \(P'(x_{0}) = f'(g(x_{0}))g'(x_{0})\) — <em>provided</em> we show</p>
      <p>\[ \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{R_{3}(h)}{h} = 0. \]</p>
      <!-- expansion:formula — the manuscript leaves the splitting and the easy term m₂R₁(h)/h → 0 implicit; we make it explicit -->
      <p>Split the quotient into its two pieces, \(\dfrac{R_{3}(h)}{h} = m_{2}\dfrac{R_{1}(h)}{h} + \dfrac{R_{2}(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h))}{h}\). The first piece is easy: \(m_{2}\) is a constant and \(R_{1}(h)/h \to 0\), so \(m_{2} R_{1}(h)/h \to 0\). We also note for later that \(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) \to 0\) as \(h \to 0\), since \(m_{1} h \to 0\) and \(R_{1}(h) \to 0\) (the latter because \(R_{1}(h)/h \to 0\)).</p>
      <p>The second piece, \(R_{2}(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h))/h\), is the delicate one. Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\).</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Since \(R_{2}(y)/y \to 0\), there is a \(\delta > 0\) with \(\left\lvert R_{2}(y)/y \right\rvert < \varepsilon\) whenever \(0 < \lvert y \rvert < \delta\).</li>
        <li>Since \(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) \to 0\), there is an \(\alpha > 0\) with \(\lvert m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) \rvert < \delta\) whenever \(0 < \lvert h \rvert < \alpha\).</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Now fix \(h\) with \(0 < \lvert h \rvert < \alpha\). If \(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) = 0\), then \(R_{2}(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)) = R_{2}(0) = 0\) — the remainder relation \(f(g(x_{0}) + y) = f(g(x_{0})) + m_{2} y + R_{2}(y)\) at \(y = 0\) forces \(R_{2}(0) = 0\) — and the quotient is \(0\). Otherwise \(0 < \lvert m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) \rvert < \delta\), and we factor</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{\lvert R_{2}(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)) \rvert}{\lvert h \rvert} = \frac{\lvert m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) \rvert}{\lvert h \rvert} \cdot \frac{\lvert R_{2}(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)) \rvert}{\lvert m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) \rvert}. \]</p>
      <!-- expansion:formula — the manuscript jumps straight to the next bound; we supply the two standard steps (the ε bound from the first bullet, and the triangle inequality) it skips -->
      <p>By the first bullet the second factor is less than \(\varepsilon\); and by the triangle inequality the first factor satisfies \(\dfrac{\lvert m_{1} h + R_{1}(h) \rvert}{\lvert h \rvert} = \left\lvert m_{1} + \dfrac{R_{1}(h)}{h} \right\rvert \le \lvert m_{1} \rvert + \dfrac{\lvert R_{1}(h) \rvert}{\lvert h \rvert}\). Hence, in either case,</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{\lvert R_{2}(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)) \rvert}{\lvert h \rvert} \le \left( \lvert m_{1} \rvert + \frac{\lvert R_{1}(h) \rvert}{\lvert h \rvert} \right)\varepsilon. \]</p>
      <p>Finally, since \(R_{1}(h)/h \to 0\), there is an \(\alpha_{1} \in (0, \alpha)\) with \(\lvert R_{1}(h) \rvert/\lvert h \rvert < 1\) for \(0 < \lvert h \rvert < \alpha_{1}\). For such \(h\),</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{\lvert R_{2}(m_{1} h + R_{1}(h)) \rvert}{\lvert h \rvert} < \bigl( \lvert m_{1} \rvert + 1 \bigr)\varepsilon. \]</p>
      <p>As \(\varepsilon > 0\) was arbitrary and \(\lvert m_{1} \rvert + 1\) is a fixed constant, this second piece tends to \(0\) as well. Combining the two pieces, \(R_{3}(h)/h \to 0\), and the chain rule is proved.<span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Using the rule</h3>

  <!-- expansion:example — single composition; pays off √(1+x²) and sin(x²) promised in the chapter opener; part (b) stages the inner-derivative pitfall -->
  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">3.4</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Differentiate (a) \(\sqrt{1 + x^{2}}\) and (b) \(\sin(x^{2})\).</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>For (a), the outermost operation is the square root, so \(f(u) = \sqrt{u} = u^{1/2}\) and \(g(x) = 1 + x^{2}\). By the power rule (Chapter 2), \(f'(u) = \tfrac12 u^{-1/2}\), and \(g'(x) = 2x\). By the chain rule,</p>
        <p>\[ \frac{d}{dx}\sqrt{1 + x^{2}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{1 + x^{2}}}\cdot 2x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 + x^{2}}}. \]</p>
        <p>For (b), the outer function is sine and the inner is \(x^{2}\), so with \(f'(u) = \cos u\) (Theorem 3.1) and \(g'(x) = 2x\),</p>
        <p>\[ \frac{d}{dx}\sin(x^{2}) = \cos(x^{2})\cdot 2x = 2x\cos(x^{2}). \]</p>
        <p>The factor \(2x\) is the inner derivative; dropping it and writing just \(\cos(x^{2})\) is the single most common chain-rule error, which the Caution below isolates.<span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <!-- expansion:caution — the chapter's #1 pitfall (ROADMAP): forgetting the inner derivative -->
  <section class="env env-caution">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Caution</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>The most common mistake with the chain rule is to differentiate the outer function and forget the inner derivative — writing \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin(g(x)) = \cos(g(x))\) instead of the correct \(\cos(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\). The inner factor \(g'(x)\) — the \(2x\) in \(\sin(x^{2})\) above — is never optional.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:example — a three-layer nested composition (ROADMAP core skill f(g(h(x)))); shows Strategy 3.1 iterated, slopes multiplying down the chain -->
  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">3.5</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Differentiate \(\sqrt{1 + \sin^{2} x}\).</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>This is a composition three layers deep: the square root of \(1 + \sin^{2} x\), in which \(\sin^{2} x = (\sin x)^{2}\) is itself a composition. Peeling from the outside in, the outer function is \(\sqrt{\,\cdot\,}\) applied to \(u = 1 + \sin^{2} x\); inside, \(\sin^{2} x\) is the square of \(v = \sin x\). Differentiating one layer at a time,</p>
        <p>\[ \begin{aligned}
          \frac{d}{dx}\sqrt{1 + \sin^{2} x}
            &= \frac{1}{2\sqrt{1 + \sin^{2} x}}\cdot \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(1 + \sin^{2} x\bigr) \\[2pt]
            &= \frac{1}{2\sqrt{1 + \sin^{2} x}}\cdot 2\sin x\cdot \cos x \\[2pt]
            &= \frac{\sin x \cos x}{\sqrt{1 + \sin^{2} x}}.
        \end{aligned} \]</p>
        <p>Each layer contributes exactly one factor: \(\tfrac12 u^{-1/2}\) from the square root, \(2\sin x\) from squaring, and \(\cos x\) from the sine. Their product is the derivative — the slopes multiply down the chain, just as Figure 3.2 pictures.<span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <!-- expansion:summary — forward-ref to §3.3: the chain rule unlocks inverse-function and logarithmic derivatives (D7: named, none worked here) -->
  <p>With the chain rule in hand the differentiation toolkit is essentially complete: any function assembled by composing the elementary functions of Chapters 2 and 3 can now be differentiated mechanically. In the next section the rule does something new — it becomes the key that unlocks derivatives we could not otherwise reach, those of the inverse functions \(\ln x\), \(\arcsin x\), and \(\arctan x\), and, through logarithmic differentiation, of expressions such as \(x^{x}\).</p>
