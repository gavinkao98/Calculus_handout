## Differentiability

In general, for `f(x)`, the limit `lim_{h→0} (f(a + h) − f(a))/h` may or may not exist. Hence the definition.

### Definition (differentiable)

`f` is differentiable at `a` if `f'(a)` exists. When we say `f` is differentiable on an interval `(a, b)`, it means `f` is differentiable at every point of the interval.

## Example (non-differentiability)

An `f` not differentiable at a point: `f(x) = |x|`, looking at its derivative at `x = 0`.
`lim_{h→0} (|0 + h| − |0|)/h = lim_{h→0} |h|/h`, where `lim_{h→0⁺} = 1` and `lim_{h→0⁻} = −1` — so the limit does not exist.

[Figure: V-shaped graph of `|x|`.]

## Differentiable implies continuous

A basic but important result on the relationship of continuity and differentiability.

### Theorem

If `f` is differentiable at `a`, then `f` is continuous at `a`.
(Converse `⇐` not true, as `f(x) = |x|` at `x = 0` shows.)

**Proof.** Goal: prove `lim_{x→a} f(x) = f(a)`, i.e. `lim_{x→a} (f(x) − f(a)) = 0`.
Write `f(x) − f(a) = ( (f(x) − f(a))/(x − a) ) · (x − a)`.
Both `lim_{x→a} (f(x) − f(a))/(x − a)` and `lim_{x→a} (x − a)` exist, so
`lim_{x→a} (f(x) − f(a)) = lim_{x→a} (f(x) − f(a))/(x − a) · lim_{x→a} (x − a) = f'(a) · 0 = 0`.
(By assumption `f'(a) = lim_{x→a} (f(x) − f(a))/(x − a)`.) Hence `lim_{x→a} (f(x) − f(a)) = 0`. ∎

## Higher derivatives

Start with `f(x)`; its derivative `f'(x)` is again a function. So we can ask whether `f'` is differentiable; if it is, `(f')' = f''`, the second derivative. Similarly we continue to `f'''`, …, `f^(n)(x)` — the derivative taken `n` times.

  <header class="sec-head">
    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">2.3</span>Differentiability, Continuity, and Higher Derivatives</h2>
  </header>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — motivation: §2.2 assumed the limit always exists; it need not -->
  <p>Throughout §2.2 we freely computed \(f'(x)\) by taking the limit of the difference quotient — but we never stopped to ask whether that limit <em>exists</em>. For the polynomial, root, and rational functions we worked with, it always did, and we might be tempted to believe that every function has a derivative at every point of its domain. This section shows that the temptation is wrong: at certain points the limit can fail, and recognising those failures is an essential part of understanding what the derivative means. Along the way we prove the first theorem of the chapter — differentiability guarantees continuity — and extend the derivative idea to second, third, and higher orders.</p>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">When does the derivative exist?</h3>

  <section class="env env-definition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Definition</span><span class="env-num">2.3</span><span class="env-name">Differentiable</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>A function \(f\) is <em>differentiable at \(a\)</em> if the limit
      \[ f'(a) \;=\; \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} \]
      exists (as a finite number). We say \(f\) is <em>differentiable on an open interval \((a, b)\)</em> if it is differentiable at every point of that interval.</p>
      <p class="informal">Informally, differentiability at a point means the graph has a single, well-defined tangent direction there — no sharp corner, no vertical spike, and no break.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <p>The definition asks exactly what we have been computing: can the difference quotient settle on a single value as \(h \to 0\)? When it can, the function is differentiable and the derivative equals that value. When it cannot — because the left-hand and right-hand limits disagree, or because the quotient blows up — the function is <em>not</em> differentiable at that point. The simplest failure mode is a corner.</p>

  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">2.11</span><span class="env-name">A corner: \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) at \(x = 0\)</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Show that \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) is not differentiable at \(x = 0\).</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>We form the difference quotient at \(a = 0\):</p>
        <p>\[ \frac{f(0 + h) - f(0)}{h} \;=\; \frac{\lvert h \rvert - 0}{h} \;=\; \frac{\lvert h \rvert}{h}. \]</p>
        <p>Now examine the one-sided limits separately. For \(h > 0\) we have \(\lvert h \rvert = h\), so</p>
        <p>\[ \lim_{h \to 0^{+}} \frac{\lvert h \rvert}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^{+}} \frac{h}{h} = 1. \]</p>
        <p>For \(h < 0\) we have \(\lvert h \rvert = -h\), so</p>
        <p>\[ \lim_{h \to 0^{-}} \frac{\lvert h \rvert}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^{-}} \frac{-h}{h} = -1. \]</p>
        <p>Since the right-hand limit (\(1\)) and the left-hand limit (\(-1\)) are not equal, the two-sided limit \(\lim_{h \to 0} \lvert h \rvert / h\) does not exist. By Definition 2.3, \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) is <em>not</em> differentiable at \(x = 0\).<span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <!-- expansion:figure — |x| corner with left/right secant slopes (ROADMAP key figure) -->
  <figure class="figure" data-fig="abs-corner">
    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure 2.3</span> The graph of \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) near the origin, with secant lines from each side.</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <p>Geometrically, the graph of \(\lvert x \rvert\) forms a V-shape at the origin (Figure 2.3). Approaching from the right, the secant lines all have slope \(+1\); approaching from the left, they all have slope \(-1\). The two families converge to different directions, so there is no single tangent line — exactly the geometric meaning of non-differentiability.</p>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Differentiability and continuity</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — set up the theorem: differentiable is a stronger condition than continuous -->
  <p>We now ask: what does differentiability <em>give</em> us beyond just having a tangent line? The answer is a clean logical relationship with continuity.</p>

  <section class="env env-theorem">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Theorem</span><span class="env-num">2.1</span><span class="env-name">Differentiable implies continuous</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>If \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\), then \(f\) is continuous at \(a\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>We must show \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\), which is equivalent to showing \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \bigl[f(x) - f(a)\bigr] = 0\).</p>
      <p>For \(x \ne a\), write</p>
      <p>\[ f(x) - f(a) \;=\; \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \;\cdot\; (x - a). \]</p>
      <p>As \(x \to a\), both factors on the right have limits that exist:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>\(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} = f'(a)\) (by hypothesis, since \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\));</li>
        <li>\(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} (x - a) = 0\).</li>
      </ul>
      <p>By the product rule for limits,</p>
      <p>\[ \lim_{x \to a} \bigl[f(x) - f(a)\bigr] = f'(a) \cdot 0 = 0. \]</p>
      <p>Hence \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\), so \(f\) is continuous at \(a\).<span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-caution">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Caution</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>The converse of Theorem 2.1 is <em>false</em>: continuity does <em>not</em> imply differentiability. The function \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) is continuous at \(x = 0\) (the left and right limits both equal \(f(0) = 0\)), yet Example 2.11 shows it is not differentiable there. Differentiability is a strictly stronger condition than continuity.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Higher derivatives</h3>

  <p>If \(f\) is differentiable, then its derivative \(f'\) is itself a function — and we can ask whether \(f'\) is differentiable. When it is, the derivative of \(f'\) is called the <em>second derivative</em> of \(f\), written \(f''\). Repeating the process gives the third derivative \(f'''\), the fourth derivative \(f^{(4)}\), and in general the \(n\)-th derivative \(f^{(n)}\):</p>

  <p>\[ f,\quad f',\quad f'',\quad f''',\quad f^{(4)},\quad \ldots,\quad f^{(n)},\quad \ldots \]</p>

  <p>Each is obtained by differentiating the previous one. By convention, \(f^{(0)} = f\) and \(f^{(1)} = f'\).</p>

  <section class="env env-remark">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Remark</span><span class="env-num">2.5</span><span class="env-name">Higher-derivative notation</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>In Leibniz notation the second derivative of \(y = f(x)\) is written</p>
      <p>\[ f''(x) \;=\; \frac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}} \;=\; \frac{d^{2}f}{dx^{2}} \;=\; \frac{d^{2}}{dx^{2}}\,f(x), \]</p>
      <p>and in general the \(n\)-th derivative is</p>
      <p>\[ f^{(n)}(x) \;=\; \frac{d^{n}y}{dx^{n}}. \]</p>
      <p>The superscript in \(d^{n}y/dx^{n}\) is <em>not</em> an exponent — it records how many times the differentiation operator \(d/dx\) has been applied. Prime notation \(f'', f'''\) is convenient for low orders; for \(n \ge 4\) the parenthesised form \(f^{(n)}\) avoids a forest of primes.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:example — higher-derivative computation using §2.2 result (approved in brief; new type) -->
  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">2.12</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Find \(f''(x)\) and \(f'''(x)\) for \(f(x) = x^{2} + 4x - 2\).</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>In Example 2.6 we found \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\). To get the second derivative, we differentiate \(f'\) from the definition:</p>
        <p>\[ \begin{aligned}
          f''(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f'(x + h) - f'(x)}{h} \\[2pt]
                 &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\bigl[2(x + h) + 4\bigr] - \bigl[2x + 4\bigr]}{h} \\[2pt]
                 &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2h}{h} = 2.
        \end{aligned} \]</p>
        <p>The second derivative is the constant \(2\) — the slope of the linear function \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\). Differentiating once more,</p>
        <p>\[ f'''(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f''(x + h) - f''(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2 - 2}{h} = 0. \]</p>
        <p>Every subsequent derivative is likewise \(0\): once we reach a constant function, further differentiation produces zero. In Leibniz notation, \(\dfrac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}} = 2\) and \(\dfrac{d^{3}y}{dx^{3}} = 0\).<span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>
