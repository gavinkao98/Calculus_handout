## Product and quotient rules

Recall `d/dx (f ± g) = d/dx f ± d/dx g`. We now learn `d/dx (fg)` and `d/dx (f/g)`.

## Product rule

The derivative of `fg`, i.e. `(fg)'`, is **not** `f'·g'`.

**Counterexample:** `f(x) = x`, `g(x) = x²` ⇒ `fg = x³`, `(fg)' = 3x²`. But `f'(x) = 1`, `g'(x) = 2x`, so `f'·g' = 2x ≠ (fg)'`.

Let us find the correct formula for `(fg)'`.

**Proof.** Use the definition: `( f(x+h)g(x+h) − f(x)g(x) ) / h`. A useful trick:
`f(x+h)g(x+h) − f(x)g(x)`
`= f(x+h)g(x+h) − f(x)g(x+h) + f(x)g(x+h) − f(x)g(x)`
`= (f(x+h) − f(x))·g(x+h) + f(x)·(g(x+h) − g(x))`.
Thus
`[…]/h = ( (f(x+h) − f(x))·g(x+h) )/h + f(x)·( (g(x+h) − g(x))/h )`.
We have learnt: if `lim h(x)` and `lim s(x)` both exist, then `lim h(x)s(x) = lim h(x) · lim s(x)`. Hence
`lim_{h→0} ( (f(x+h) − f(x))/h )·g(x+h) = lim_{h→0} (f(x+h) − f(x))/h · lim_{h→0} g(x+h) = f'(x)·g(x)`,
`lim_{h→0} f(x)·( (g(x+h) − g(x))/h ) = f(x)·g'(x)`.
Therefore
`(f(x)g(x))' = f'(x)·g(x) + f(x)·g'(x)`, i.e. `d/dx (fg) = (d/dx f)·g + f·(d/dx g)`.

### Example

`h(x) = x·e^x` ⇒ `h'(x) = e^x + x·e^x = e^x·(1 + x)`.

## The quotient rule

For `f(x)/g(x)`:
`f(x+Δx)/g(x+Δx) − f(x)/g(x) = ( f(x+Δx)·g(x) − g(x+Δx)·f(x) ) / ( g(x+Δx)·g(x) )`.
Divide by `Δx`. On the numerator, add and subtract `f(x)g(x)`:
`f(x+Δx)·g(x) − g(x+Δx)·f(x) = f(x+Δx)g(x) − f(x)g(x) + f(x)g(x) − g(x+Δx)f(x)`
`= (f(x+Δx) − f(x))·g(x) − (g(x+Δx) − g(x))·f(x)`.
So the difference quotient is
`( (f(x+Δx) − f(x))/Δx )·( g(x) / (g(x+Δx)·g(x)) ) − ( (g(x+Δx) − g(x))/Δx )·( f(x) / (g(x+Δx)·g(x)) )`.
Let `Δx → 0`:
`= f'(x)·g(x)/g(x)² − g'(x)·f(x)/g(x)²`.
Thus `d/dx (f/g) = ( f'(x)·g(x) − f(x)·g'(x) ) / g(x)²`.

  <!-- expansion:intuition — open by recalling §2.4's clean sum/difference behaviour, then flag that products and quotients are the unfinished business -->
  <p>In §2.4 we learned that differentiation behaves beautifully with respect to addition: the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives, and the same holds for differences, \((f \pm g)' = f' \pm g'\) (Theorem 2.4). It is tempting to hope that products and quotients are just as docile — that the derivative of a product is the product of the derivatives. This section shows that they are <em>not</em>, finds the correct rules, and so completes the toolkit of basic differentiation rules begun in §2.4. We treat the two cases in turn: first \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(fg)\), then \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)\).</p>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">The product rule</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — set up the naive guess so the counterexample can demolish it (failure-first) -->
  <p>The cleanest guess one could make is that derivatives pass through a product untouched, \((fg)' = f'g'\). A single example shows this is false.</p>

      <p>\[ f'(x)\,g'(x) = (1)(2x) = 2x \ne 3x^{2}. \]</p>

    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Theorem</span><span class="env-num">2.6</span><span class="env-name">The product rule</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable at \(x\), then their product \(fg\) is differentiable at \(x\), and</p>
      <p>\[ (fg)'(x) = f'(x)\,g(x) + f(x)\,g'(x), \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \frac{d}{dx}(fg) = \left(\frac{d}{dx}f\right)g + f\left(\frac{d}{dx}g\right). \]</p>
    </div>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>We start from the definition and apply a useful trick: add and subtract \(f(x)\,g(x+h)\) in the numerator, which lets us isolate the change in \(f\) from the change in \(g\):</p>
      <p>\[ \begin{aligned}
        f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)
          &= \bigl[f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x+h)\bigr] + \bigl[f(x)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)\bigr] \\[2pt]
          &= \bigl[f(x+h) - f(x)\bigr]\,g(x+h) + f(x)\,\bigl[g(x+h) - g(x)\bigr].
      \end{aligned} \]</p>
      <p>Dividing by \(h\),</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h} = \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\,g(x+h) \;+\; f(x)\,\frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h}. \]</p>

Therefore</p>
      <p>\[ (fg)'(x) = f'(x)\,g(x) + f(x)\,g'(x). \]<span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>

      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Here \(h\) is a product of \(f(x) = x\) and \(g(x) = e^{x}\), with \(f'(x) = 1\) and \(g'(x) = e^{x}\) (Theorem 2.5). The product rule gives</p>
        <p>\[ h'(x) = f'(x)\,g(x) + f(x)\,g'(x) = (1)\,e^{x} + x\,e^{x} = e^{x}(1 + x). \]</p>
        <p>Notice that neither factor alone produces the answer: the term \(e^{x}\) comes from differentiating \(x\) while keeping \(e^{x}\), and the term \(x\,e^{x}\) from keeping \(x\) while differentiating \(e^{x}\) — the two strips of Figure 2.4 in algebraic form.<span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">The quotient rule</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — bridge to the quotient, reusing the same add-and-subtract idea; introduce the manuscript's Δx notation -->
  <p>The same add-and-subtract idea handles a quotient \(f/g\). Here the manuscript writes the increment as \(\Delta x\) rather than \(h\); the two symbols mean the same thing, as the next caution records.</p>

  <!-- expansion:caution — ROADMAP notation pitfall, deliberately deferred from §2.2 to here: Δx ≡ h -->
  <section class="env env-caution">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Caution</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>The increment in the variable is written either \(h\) or \(\Delta x\); in derivative computations the two are synonymous. Reading \(\Delta x\) as “a small change in \(x\),” the difference quotient \(\bigl(f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)\bigr)/\Delta x\) is identical to the \(h\)-form, and \(\Delta x \to 0\) means the same as \(h \to 0\). We switch to \(\Delta x\) below only to match the manuscript.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-theorem">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Theorem</span><span class="env-num">2.7</span><span class="env-name">The quotient rule</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable at \(x\) and \(g(x) \ne 0\), then the quotient \(f/g\) is differentiable at \(x\), and</p>
      <p>\[ \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(x) = \frac{f'(x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g'(x)}{\bigl[g(x)\bigr]^{2}}. \]</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- proof follows the manuscript: combine over a common denominator, add and subtract f(x)g(x) on the numerator, keep Δx notation; needs g continuous (so g(x+Δx)→g(x)) and g(x)≠0 (so the denominator tends to g(x)^2 ≠ 0) -->
  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Combining the two fractions over a common denominator,</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{f(x+\Delta x)}{g(x+\Delta x)} - \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f(x+\Delta x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g(x+\Delta x)}{g(x+\Delta x)\,g(x)}. \]</p>
      <p>On the numerator we play the same trick as before, now adding and subtracting \(f(x)\,g(x)\):</p>
      <p>\[ \begin{aligned}
        f(x+\Delta x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g(x+\Delta x)
          &= \bigl[f(x+\Delta x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g(x)\bigr] - \bigl[f(x)\,g(x+\Delta x) - f(x)\,g(x)\bigr] \\[2pt]
          &= \bigl[f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)\bigr]\,g(x) - f(x)\,\bigl[g(x+\Delta x) - g(x)\bigr].
      \end{aligned} \]</p>
      <p>Dividing the whole difference by \(\Delta x\), the difference quotient becomes</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{1}{g(x+\Delta x)\,g(x)}\left( \frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}\,g(x) - f(x)\,\frac{g(x+\Delta x) - g(x)}{\Delta x} \right). \]</p>
      <p>Let \(\Delta x \to 0\). The two difference quotients tend to \(f'(x)\) and \(g'(x)\); and \(g(x+\Delta x) \to g(x)\) because \(g\) is continuous at \(x\) (Theorem 2.1, §2.3), so the denominator tends to \(g(x)\,g(x) = \bigl[g(x)\bigr]^{2}\), which is nonzero since \(g(x) \ne 0\). Hence</p>
      <p>\[ \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(x) = \frac{f'(x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g'(x)}{\bigl[g(x)\bigr]^{2}}. \]<span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:caution — ROADMAP pitfall: quotient-rule asymmetry; numerator term order matters -->
  <section class="env env-caution">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Caution</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Unlike the product rule, the quotient rule is <em>not</em> symmetric in \(f\) and \(g\): the numerator is \(f'g - f g'\), with the derivative of the <em>top</em> coming first. Swapping the order to \(f g' - f' g\) flips the sign of the whole derivative, and forgetting the \(g^{2}\) in the denominator changes the answer entirely. A reliable mnemonic reads the numerator as “(derivative of top)(bottom) minus (top)(derivative of bottom), all over bottom squared.”</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:example — inline mini-application that fulfils the promise made in §2.2 (Example 2.8): recover (1/x)' = −1/x^2 in one line -->
  <p>As a first check, the quotient rule recovers a derivative we computed laboriously from first principles in §2.2. Taking the numerator to be the constant \(1\) and the denominator \(x\),</p>
  <p>\[ \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = \frac{(0)(x) - (1)(1)}{x^{2}} = -\frac{1}{x^{2}}, \]</p>
  <p>matching the derivative \(-1/x^{2}\) found in Example 2.8 — obtained here in a single line, exactly as promised there.</p>

  <!-- expansion:example — new worked example (approved 2026-06-08, decision E/F): a genuine rational quotient that exercises the full formula and the numerator order -->
  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">2.16</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Differentiate \(f(x) = \dfrac{x}{x^{2} + 1}\).</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>This is a genuine quotient — no algebra simplifies it to a power — so the quotient rule applies, with top \(x\) and bottom \(x^{2} + 1\). Their derivatives are \(1\) and \(2x\), and the bottom never vanishes, so the rule is valid for every \(x\). Taking care with the order of the numerator terms,</p>
        <p>\[ \begin{aligned}
          f'(x) &= \frac{(1)\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr) - (x)(2x)}{\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr)^{2}} \\[2pt]
                &= \frac{x^{2} + 1 - 2x^{2}}{\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr)^{2}}
                 = \frac{1 - x^{2}}{\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr)^{2}}.
        \end{aligned} \]</p>
        <p>Had we mistakenly written the numerator as \((x)(2x) - (1)(x^{2}+1)\), every sign would be wrong. As a sanity check on the result, \(f'(0) = 1 > 0\) (the curve rises through the origin) and \(f'(\pm 1) = 0\), the two points where \(x/(x^{2}+1)\) reaches its largest and smallest values.<span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Choosing the right rule</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — motivate the strategy box: with several rules now available, the first task on any problem is to read its shape and pick the rule -->
  <p>With the constant, power, sum, constant-multiple, product, and quotient rules all in hand, differentiating a given expression is now less about computing limits and more about <em>recognising</em> which rule its structure calls for. Reading that structure — and simplifying first when possible — is the skill the next box organises.</p>

  <!-- expansion:strategy — ROADMAP assigns "Selecting among the basic rules" to §2.5; 3 steps incl. the "simplify first" judgement (decision C) -->
  <section class="env env-strategy">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Strategy</span><span class="env-num">2.2</span><span class="env-name">Selecting among the basic rules</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <ol class="steps">
        <li>Read the <em>outermost</em> structure of the expression. Is it a single power or polynomial, a sum or difference, a product of two functions, or a quotient of two functions?</li>
        <li>Before reaching for the product or quotient rule, ask whether algebra simplifies the expression first: a quotient like \(\dfrac{x^{4} - x}{x}\) becomes the polynomial \(x^{3} - 1\), and a product like \(x^{2}\cdot x^{3}\) becomes \(x^{5}\). A rule you can avoid is a rule you cannot misapply.</li>
        <li>Apply the matching rule: the power, sum, and constant-multiple rules for polynomials and powers; the product rule (Theorem 2.6) for a genuine product; the quotient rule (Theorem 2.7) for a genuine quotient. For compound expressions, the rules combine — a sum of products, a product with a polynomial factor, and so on.</li>
      </ol>
      <p>The point is to spend a moment on the shape of the problem before computing: the same expression can be far easier under one rule than another, and some apparent quotients are not quotients at all once simplified.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:example — new worked example (approved 2026-06-08, decision E): rule-selection/diagnosis anchoring Strategy 2.2; (b) is the simplify-first lesson -->
  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">2.17</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>For each function, first decide which rule applies, then differentiate:
        \[ \text{(a)}\ \ y = x^{3} e^{x}, \qquad \text{(b)}\ \ y = \frac{x^{4} - x}{x}, \qquad \text{(c)}\ \ y = \frac{x^{2}}{e^{x}}. \]</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p><em>(a)</em> A genuine product of \(x^{3}\) and \(e^{x}\); use the product rule:</p>
        <p>\[ y' = (3x^{2})\,e^{x} + x^{3}\,e^{x} = x^{2} e^{x}(3 + x). \]</p>
        <p><em>(b)</em> This <em>looks</em> like a quotient, but it simplifies first — for \(x \ne 0\), \(\dfrac{x^{4} - x}{x} = x^{3} - 1\) — so the power rule finishes it with no quotient rule at all:</p>
        <p>\[ y' = 3x^{2}. \]</p>
        <p><em>(c)</em> A genuine quotient that does not simplify; use the quotient rule, with top \(x^{2}\) and bottom \(e^{x}\):</p>
        <p>\[ y' = \frac{(2x)\,e^{x} - x^{2}\,e^{x}}{\bigl(e^{x}\bigr)^{2}} = \frac{e^{x}\bigl(2x - x^{2}\bigr)}{e^{2x}} = \frac{2x - x^{2}}{e^{x}}. \]</p>
        <p>The three parts share no single rule; the work was choosing correctly, and in (b) recognising that the cleanest move was not to use the quotient rule at all.<span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <!-- expansion:summary — close the chapter's differentiation rules; one-line forward fence to Ch3 chain rule (no preview), per decision G -->
  <p>The product and quotient rules complete the basic differentiation toolkit of this chapter: together with the constant, power, sum, and constant-multiple rules and the derivative of \(e^{x}\), they let us differentiate any algebraic or exponential combination built by adding, scaling, multiplying, and dividing. One important way of combining functions remains untouched — <em>composition</em>, forming \(f\bigl(g(x)\bigr)\) — and it needs a rule of its own, the chain rule, which opens the next chapter.</p>
