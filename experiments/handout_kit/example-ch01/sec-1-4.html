1.4One-Sided and Infinite Limits

Near a point \(a\), a function can behave differently depending on which side \(x\) approaches from, and in some cases the values of the function do not approach any finite number at all. These two observations lead to one-sided limits and infinite limits.

One-sided limits

Sometimes we only want to consider one side of the approach to \(a\) — for instance because the function is defined by different formulas on the two sides, or because the graph jumps at \(a\).

Definition1.10

We write \[ \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = L \] and say that the left-hand limit of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\) is \(L\) if \(f(x)\) approaches \(L\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\) through values less than \(a\). Similarly, \[ \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) = L \] is the right-hand limit, describing the approach through values greater than \(a\).

The two one-sided limits together determine the two-sided limit.

Proposition1.5Criterion for a two-sided limit

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = L \ \text{ and } \ \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) = L. \]

If the two one-sided limits differ, then the two-sided limit does not exist.

Example1.9

Let \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} x + 1, & x < 2,\\[4pt] 4 - x, & x \ge 2. \end{cases} \] Evaluate \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2^{-}} f(x)\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2^{+}} f(x)\), and determine whether \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\) exists.

Solution

From the left we use the branch \(f(x) = x + 1\), so \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2^{-}} f(x) = 3\).

From the right we use the branch \(f(x) = 4 - x\), so \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2^{+}} f(x) = 2\).

Since the left-hand and right-hand limits differ, \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\) does not exist.

Infinite limits

In some cases, the values of \(f(x)\) do not approach any finite number as \(x \to a\). Instead, they grow arbitrarily large positive or arbitrarily large negative.

Example1.10

Consider \(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x^{2}}\). What happens to \(f(x)\) as \(x \to 0\)?

Solution

If \(x\) is close to \(0\), then \(x^{2}\) is a very small positive number, so \(\dfrac{1}{x^{2}}\) is very large. For instance, \[ \frac{1}{(0.1)^{2}} = 100, \qquad \frac{1}{(0.01)^{2}} = 10{,}000. \] The values of \(\dfrac{1}{x^{2}}\) grow without bound as \(x \to 0\), so no finite \(L\) satisfies the informal definition of the limit; the limit does not exist.

To capture this behavior, we introduce the notation \[ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x^{2}} = \infty. \] The symbol \(\infty\) does not represent a real number; it records that the function's values grow without bound.

Definition1.11

Let \(f\) be defined on both sides of \(a\), except possibly at \(a\) itself. We write \[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty \] if the values of \(f(x)\) can be made arbitrarily large by taking \(x\) sufficiently close to \(a\), with \(x \neq a\). We call such a limit an infinite limit. Similarly, \[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = -\infty \] if the values of \(f(x)\) can be made arbitrarily large negative by taking \(x\) sufficiently close to \(a\), with \(x \neq a\).

One-sided versions, \[ \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = \infty,\quad \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) = \infty,\quad \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = -\infty,\quad \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) = -\infty, \] are defined analogously. The four cases are illustrated in Figure 1.9.

Vertical asymptotes

Infinite limits are closely related to vertical asymptotes.

Definition1.12

The vertical line \(x = a\) is called a vertical asymptote of the curve \(y = f(x)\) if at least one of the following holds: \[ \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = \pm\infty \qquad \text{or} \qquad \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) = \pm\infty. \]

Example1.11

Does the curve \(y = \dfrac{2x}{x - 3}\) have a vertical asymptote?

Solution

The denominator is zero only at \(x = 3\), so \(x = 3\) is the only candidate. As \(x \to 3^{+}\), the denominator \(x - 3\) is a small positive number while the numerator \(2x\) is close to \(6\), so \[ \lim_{x \to 3^{+}} \frac{2x}{x - 3} = \infty. \] As \(x \to 3^{-}\), the denominator is a small negative number while the numerator is still close to \(6\), so \[ \lim_{x \to 3^{-}} \frac{2x}{x - 3} = -\infty. \] At least one one-sided limit is infinite, so the line \(x = 3\) is a vertical asymptote, as shown in Figure 1.10.

Example1.12

Guess the value of \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0^{+}} \ln x\).

Solution

As \(x \to 0^{+}\), the values of \(\ln x\) decrease without bound, so \[ \lim_{x \to 0^{+}} \ln x = -\infty. \] Therefore \(x = 0\) is a vertical asymptote of the graph of \(y = \ln x\).
