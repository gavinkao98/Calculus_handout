### §4.5 opener（手稿，承 §4.4 結尾）

**§ Logarithmic function.**
Since `e^x` is strictly increasing for all `x ∈ ℝ`, we can define `ln x` to be the inverse function of `e^x` for `x ∈ (a, b)` where

`a = lim_{x→−∞} e^x = 0`   and   `b = lim_{x→∞} e^x = +∞`.

> 手稿筆跡：「the **in** inverse function」有一個贅字 `in`（劃寫殘留），實為「the inverse function」。值域端點 `(a,b)=(0,+∞)` 由兩個極限給出。**[斷言不證]**：手稿直接寫 `lim_{x→−∞}e^x=0`、`lim_{x→∞}e^x=+∞`（即 `e^x` 的值域＝`(0,∞)`），**未證這兩個極限、也未證滿射**。legacy line 736 明言「we will not prove this explicitly; it follows from continuity and the limits …, which can be derived from the series」——同樣斷言不證。屬手稿刻意省略（見下「刻意省略」）。

### Def（`ln x`，手稿方框標 "Def"）

For `x ∈ (0, ∞)`, we define `ln x = a ∈ ℝ` to be the unique value that satisfies

`e^a = x`.

i.e.,   `e^{ln x} = x`.

It is not hard to see that

`ln(e^x) = x`   for all `x ∈ ℝ`.

> 手稿筆跡：「ln x = a ∈ ℝ **be** the unique value」（文法 `be`→`to be`／`is`）、「It is **no** hard to see」（`no`→`not`）——皆筆誤、數學無歧義。兩條反函數恆等式：`e^{ln x}=x`（x>0，`ln` 為右作用的反）與 `ln(e^x)=x`（x∈ℝ，左作用的反）。唯一性來自 `e^x` strict monotonicity；存在性（對每個 x>0 都有 a）來自值域＝`(0,∞)`（上面斷言不證的那條）。legacy `def:logarithm`（line 738）同：「the unique real number `a` satisfying `e^a=x`；equivalently the inverse of `e^x` on `(0,∞)`：`e^{ln x}=x` ∀x>0、`ln(e^x)=x` ∀x∈ℝ」。✓

### Property（`ln x` 連續，手稿方框標 "Property"）

**`ln x` is a continuous function.**

**證（手稿，proof by contradiction）：**
Suppose `∃ x_0 > 0` s.t. `lim_{x→x_0} ln x ≠ ln x_0`.

Either `lim_{x→x_0} ln x` does not exist, or `lim_{x→x_0} ln x = μ ≠ ln x_0`.

In either case, `∃ δ > 0` and `y_j → x_0` s.t.

`|ln y_j − ln x_0| ≥ δ`   for `j = 1, 2, 3, …`.

**Case 1.** `ln x_0 ≥ ln y_j + δ`
⟹ `e^{ln x_0} − e^{ln y_j} ≥ e^{ln x_0} − e^{ln x_0 − δ} > 0`
  (since `e^z` is a strictly increasing function).

**Case 2.** `ln x_0 + δ ≤ ln y_j`
⟹ `e^{ln y_j} − e^{ln x_0} ≥ e^{ln x_0 + δ} − e^{ln x_0} > 0`.

Let `ε = min{ e^{ln x_0} − e^{ln x_0 − δ},  e^{ln x_0 + δ} − e^{ln x_0} }`.

We see that

`|y_j − x_0| = |e^{ln y_j} − e^{ln x_0}| ≥ ε`   for all `j = 1, 2, …`.

This violates `lim_{j→∞} y_j = x_0`.

Hence, `ln x` is a continuous function.

> 邏輯：否定連續 ⟹ 存在序列 `y_j→x_0` 但 `|ln y_j − ln x_0|≥δ`（sequential continuity 的反面）。每個 `j` 落入 Case 1（`ln y_j ≤ ln x_0−δ`）或 Case 2（`ln y_j ≥ ln x_0+δ`）。對 `e^z`（strict increasing）作用，兩 case 各給一個**嚴格正**的下界；`ε`＝兩者較小。關鍵恆等式 `y_j = e^{ln y_j}`、`x_0 = e^{ln x_0}`（`e^{ln(·)}` 為恆等）⟹ `|y_j−x_0| = |e^{ln y_j}−e^{ln x_0}| ≥ ε > 0`，與 `y_j→x_0` 矛盾。legacy `thm:ln-continuous`（line 758）同骨架，但寫 **WLOG 單邊**（`ln y_j ≥ ln x_0+δ` for infinitely many j），得 `y_j ≥ e^δ·x_0 > x_0`、gap 阻止收斂。手稿顯式雙 case＋`ε=min` 更完整。math 一致。✓ **無 [請查核]。**

### § The differentiability of ln x（手稿小節標題）

For `x > 0` and `x + h > 0` with `h ≠ 0`, we have

`h = (x + h) − x = e^{ln(x+h)} − e^{ln x}`
` = [ (e^{ln(x+h)} − e^{ln x}) / (ln(x+h) − ln x) ] · (ln(x+h) − ln x)`.

**Note:** Since `h ≠ 0`, `ln(x+h) ≠ ln x`

⟹   `(ln(x+h) − ln x) / h = 1 / [ (e^{ln(x+h)} − e^{ln x}) / (ln(x+h) − ln x) ]`.

Set `y_0 = ln x`,  `y = ln(x+h)`.

Since `ln x` is a continuous and strictly increasing function,

`lim_{h→0} y = y_0`   and   `y ≠ y_0` for `h ≠ 0`.

We then see that

`lim_{h→0, h≠0} (e^{ln(x+h)} − e^{ln x}) / (ln(x+h) − ln x)`
` = lim_{y→y_0, y≠y_0} (e^y − e^{y_0}) / (y − y_0) = e^{y_0} = e^{ln x} = x`.

**Finally, we conclude**

`lim_{h→0} (ln(x+h) − ln x) / h = lim_{h→0}  1 / [ (e^{ln(x+h)} − e^{ln x}) / (ln(x+h) − ln x) ]`
` = 1 / [ lim_{h→0} (e^{ln(x+h)} − e^{ln x}) / (ln(x+h) − ln x) ] = 1/x`.

This proves that

`d/dx ln x = 1/x`   for `x > 0`.

> inverse-function 技巧：用 `e^{ln(x+h)}=x+h`、`e^{ln x}=x` 把 `h` 寫成 `e^y−e^{y_0}`（`y=ln(x+h)`, `y_0=ln x`），再把差分商倒置成「`e^y` 在 `y_0` 的差分商」的倒數。`h→0` 時 `y→y_0`（`ln` 連續，剛證），分母 → `lim_{y→y_0}(e^y−e^{y_0})/(y−y_0) = (d/dy e^y)|_{y_0} = e^{y_0} = e^{ln x} = x`（套 §4.3 Theorem 4.8）。`y≠y_0`（`ln` strict ⟹ 單射，`h≠0` ⟹ `y≠y_0`）確保分母差分商分母不為 0。倒數 ⟹ `1/x`。legacy `thm:ln-derivative-rigorous`（line 776）逐步一致（legacy 直接用 `y,y_0` 代換、手稿先寫滿 `ln(x+h)` 再 `Set y=…`，等價）。✓ **無 [請查核]。**

## 承重直覺（一節一個，領頭）
**Strict monotonicity 是同時「建造」與「驅動」反函數的那根槓桿。** 要把 `e^x` 反過來得到 `ln`，天真想法是「反函數自動存在、而且自動跟原函數一樣乖」——兩個都要打臉。**建造**面：反函數要良定義，需 (1) 單射（每個 `x>0` 對到唯一的 `a`，這來自 `e^x` **strict** monotonicity，§4.4 剛收割）＋(2) 滿射到 `(0,∞)`（每個正數都被打到，這來自連續＋極限，斷言）。**驅動**面更關鍵也更反直覺：`ln` 一旦定義，它的**連續性與可微性都不是免費的**——各需一個證明，而**兩個證明都回頭扳同一根槓桿**：`e^x` 的 strict monotonicity。連續性反證裡，若 `ln y_j` 不逼近 `ln x_0`，strict monotonicity 把它放大成 `e^{ln y_j}` 與 `e^{ln x_0}` 之間一道**撐開的 gap**，與 `y_j→x_0` 矛盾。可微性裡，inverse-function 技巧把差分商**倒置**成 `e^y` 的差分商倒數，而「能除」靠的是 `y≠y_0`——又是單射。**最該打臉的天真期待**：Ch3 §3.3 當時「假設 `ln` 可微、用 chain rule 在 `e^{ln x}=x` 上一步得 `1/x`」。§4.5 的整個重點是：**你不必預設可微**——可微性可以從差分商裡**萃取**出來，只用連續（剛證）＋`(e^x)'`（§4.3 證）。這就是本節的可攜技能：**inverse-function 技巧——把反函數的導數從一條代換 + 倒置的差分商裡逼出來，不靠 chain rule、不預設可微。**

## 強調 / takeaway
- **概念樞紐**：**反函數的良好性質不是天上掉下來的——strict monotonicity 一根槓桿同時保證 `ln` 存在（單射＋滿射）、連續、可微**。三件事（定義／連續／可微）都扳同一根槓桿。
- **可攜技能**：**inverse-function 技巧**——求反函數導數時，把差分商用 `e^{ln(x+h)}=x+h` 代換、倒置成原函數的差分商倒數，取極限（靠反函數連續）得 `1/(原函數導數)`。不預設反函數可微。學生帶得走（求任何可逆函數的反函數導數）。
- **依賴鏈 takeaway（全章收束）**：`完備性(§4.1)→級數定義 e^x→連續/指數律(§4.2)→(e^x)'=e^x(§4.3)→MVT(§4.4)→e^x 嚴格遞增→ln x 定義/連續/(ln x)'=1/x(§4.5)`。本節是全章終點，把「Ch2/Ch3 非正式用過的 `e^x`/`ln x`」全部補成定理。

  <header class="sec-head">
    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">4.5</span>Monotonicity and the Logarithmic Function</h2>
  </header>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — opener: pick up §4.4's closing fact (e^x strictly increasing ⟹ one-to-one); strict monotonicity is the engine that both builds and powers the inverse; lists the section's program and the two fences it closes (§C-11) -->
  <p>Section 4.4 closed with the fact this section is built on: \(e^{x}\) is strictly increasing on \(\mathbb{R}\), and a strictly increasing function is one-to-one. A one-to-one function can be inverted, and the inverse of the exponential is the function we are after — the <em>natural logarithm</em>. Strict monotonicity is the lever throughout. It does the obvious work of <em>building</em> the inverse, by making each value \(x &gt; 0\) come from exactly one exponent. Less obviously, it also <em>powers</em> everything we then prove about that inverse: the continuity of \(\ln\) and its differentiability are not free gifts that come with the definition. Each needs its own argument, and both arguments reach back to the strict monotonicity of \(e^{x}\). This section defines \(\ln x\), proves it is continuous, and derives \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln x = \dfrac{1}{x}\) rigorously — this time without assuming in advance that \(\ln\) is differentiable, the assumption Chapter 3 made informally. Along the way it settles two debts left earlier: the informal logarithm of §3.3 and the meaning of \(a^{r}\) for irrational \(r\), deferred back in §4.1.</p>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Defining the natural logarithm</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — §C-1: the range of e^x is (0,∞); assert with a one-line dependency note (manuscript + legacy both assert, do not prove) -->
  <p>To invert \(e^{x}\) we need to know what values it takes. As \(x\) ranges over \(\mathbb{R}\), \(e^{x}\) is continuous (Theorem 4.6) and runs from \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} e^{x} = 0\) up to \(\lim_{x \to +\infty} e^{x} = +\infty\), passing through every value in between; its range is therefore the open interval \((0, \infty)\). We take this for granted here — the two limits follow from the series and the intermediate values from continuity, but the details would detain us — and read off the consequence we need: for every \(x &gt; 0\) there is exactly one exponent producing it.</p>

  <section class="env env-definition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Definition</span><span class="env-num">4.4</span><span class="env-name">Natural logarithm</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>For \(x \in (0, \infty)\), the <em>natural logarithm</em> of \(x\), written \(\ln x\), is the unique real number \(a\) satisfying</p>
      <p>\[ e^{a} = x. \]</p>
      <p>Equivalently, \(\ln x\) is the inverse function of \(e^{x}\) on \((0, \infty)\): it is characterised by the two identities</p>
      <p>\[ e^{\ln x} = x \quad (x &gt; 0), \qquad \ln(e^{x}) = x \quad (x \in \mathbb{R}). \]</p>
      <p class="informal">In words, \(\ln x\) is the exponent you must raise \(e\) to in order to land on \(x\). The strict monotonicity of \(e^{x}\) makes that exponent unique; its range being \((0, \infty)\) makes the exponent exist for every positive \(x\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:caution — §C-2 (ROADMAP pitfall): ln defined only for x>0 -->
  <section class="env env-caution">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Caution</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>The logarithm is defined only for \(x &gt; 0\). The symbols \(\ln 0\) and \(\ln(-1)\) have no meaning here: \(\ln 0\) would require \(e^{a} = 0\) and \(\ln(-1)\) would require \(e^{a} = -1\), and neither is possible, because \(e^{a} &gt; 0\) for every real \(a\). Every formula in this section that contains \(\ln x\) silently carries the assumption \(x &gt; 0\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:figure — Figure 4.3 (§C-3, ROADMAP key figure): e^x / ln x reflection across y=x; registered in chapter4 FIGS as "exp-log-reflection" -->
  <figure class="figure" data-fig="exp-log-reflection">
    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure 4.3</span> The graphs of \(e^{x}\) (blue) and \(\ln x\) (red) are mirror images across the line \(y = x\) (dashed) — the geometric signature of the inverse relation \(\ln(e^{x}) = x\). Reflecting swaps coordinates, sending the point \((0, 1)\) on the exponential to \((1, 0)\) on the logarithm.</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Continuity of the logarithm</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — lead into the continuity proof; the indirect argument leans on strict monotonicity -->
  <p>That \(\ln\) is continuous is not automatic and takes a short argument. The idea is indirect: if some sequence of inputs approached \(x_{0}\) while their logarithms stayed away from \(\ln x_{0}\), then applying the strictly increasing \(e^{x}\) would re-open a fixed gap on the input side — contradicting the very fact that the inputs converged.</p>

  <section class="env env-theorem">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Theorem</span><span class="env-num">4.13</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>\(\ln x\) is continuous at every \(x_{0} &gt; 0\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Fix \(x_{0} &gt; 0\) and suppose, for contradiction, that \(\ln\) is <em>not</em> continuous at \(x_{0}\). Then \(\lim_{x \to x_{0}} \ln x\) either fails to exist or exists but differs from \(\ln x_{0}\). In either case the failure is witnessed by a sequence: there is a number \(\delta &gt; 0\) and points \(y_{j} \to x_{0}\) (all in \((0, \infty)\)) with</p>
      <p>\[ \lvert \ln y_{j} - \ln x_{0} \rvert \ge \delta \qquad \text{for every } j = 1, 2, 3, \dots \]</p>
      <p>For each \(j\) this means \(\ln y_{j} \le \ln x_{0} - \delta\) or \(\ln y_{j} \ge \ln x_{0} + \delta\). Apply \(e^{x}\), which is strictly increasing (Example 4.4), to each case. Writing \(y_{j} = e^{\ln y_{j}}\) and \(x_{0} = e^{\ln x_{0}}\):</p>
      <ul>
        <li>if \(\ln y_{j} \le \ln x_{0} - \delta\), then \(x_{0} - y_{j} = e^{\ln x_{0}} - e^{\ln y_{j}} \ge e^{\ln x_{0}} - e^{\ln x_{0} - \delta} &gt; 0\);</li>
        <li>if \(\ln y_{j} \ge \ln x_{0} + \delta\), then \(y_{j} - x_{0} = e^{\ln y_{j}} - e^{\ln x_{0}} \ge e^{\ln x_{0} + \delta} - e^{\ln x_{0}} &gt; 0\).</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Let</p>
      <p>\[ \varepsilon = \min\!\left\{\, e^{\ln x_{0}} - e^{\ln x_{0} - \delta}, \; e^{\ln x_{0} + \delta} - e^{\ln x_{0}} \,\right\} &gt; 0, \]</p>
      <p>a single positive number depending only on \(x_{0}\) and \(\delta\). In both cases \(\lvert y_{j} - x_{0} \rvert = \lvert e^{\ln y_{j}} - e^{\ln x_{0}} \rvert \ge \varepsilon\), so</p>
      <p>\[ \lvert y_{j} - x_{0} \rvert \ge \varepsilon \qquad \text{for every } j. \]</p>
      <p>But this contradicts \(y_{j} \to x_{0}\), which forces \(\lvert y_{j} - x_{0} \rvert\) below \(\varepsilon\) for large \(j\). The assumption of discontinuity is untenable, so \(\lim_{x \to x_{0}} \ln x = \ln x_{0}\) and \(\ln\) is continuous at \(x_{0}\). <span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">The derivative of the logarithm</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — lead: contrast with Ch3 §3.3's chain-rule pass; the inverse-function technique extracts the derivative without assuming it -->
  <p>Chapter 3 already wrote down \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln x = \dfrac{1}{x}\), but it did so by differentiating \(e^{\ln x} = x\) with the chain rule — which silently <em>assumes</em> \(\ln\) is differentiable. We can now earn the formula honestly. The inverse-function technique flips the logarithm's difference quotient into a difference quotient for \(e^{x}\), whose derivative we already control, and lets continuity carry the limit across.</p>

  <section class="env env-theorem">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Theorem</span><span class="env-num">4.14</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>For every \(x &gt; 0\),</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{d}{dx}\ln x = \frac{1}{x}. \]</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Fix \(x &gt; 0\) and take \(h \ne 0\) small enough that \(x + h &gt; 0\). Set</p>
      <p>\[ y_{0} = \ln x, \qquad y = \ln(x + h), \]</p>
      <p>so that \(e^{y_{0}} = x\) and \(e^{y} = x + h\); subtracting, \(e^{y} - e^{y_{0}} = (x + h) - x = h\). Substituting this for \(h\) and flipping the quotient,</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{\ln(x + h) - \ln x}{h} = \frac{y - y_{0}}{e^{y} - e^{y_{0}}} = \frac{1}{\dfrac{e^{y} - e^{y_{0}}}{y - y_{0}}}. \]</p>
      <p>The division is legitimate because \(y \ne y_{0}\): the logarithm is strictly increasing, being the inverse of the strictly increasing \(e^{x}\), so \(h \ne 0\) forces \(\ln(x + h) \ne \ln x\).</p>
      <p>Now let \(h \to 0\). By continuity of \(\ln\) (Theorem 4.13), \(y = \ln(x + h) \to \ln x = y_{0}\), and \(y \ne y_{0}\) all the while. The denominator is precisely the difference quotient of \(e^{y}\) at \(y_{0}\), so</p>
      <p>\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^{y} - e^{y_{0}}}{y - y_{0}} = \lim_{y \to y_{0}} \frac{e^{y} - e^{y_{0}}}{y - y_{0}} = \left.\frac{d}{dy} e^{y}\right|_{y = y_{0}} = e^{y_{0}} = e^{\ln x} = x, \]</p>
      <p>using \((e^{y})' = e^{y}\) from Theorem 4.8. Taking the reciprocal,</p>
      <p>\[ \frac{d}{dx}\ln x = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(x + h) - \ln x}{h} = \frac{1}{x}. \tag{$\ast$} \]</p>
      <p><span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — Remark 4.5 (§C-4, ROADMAP prerequisite): rigorous vs Ch3 chain-rule; closes the §3.3 forward-reference loop -->
  <section class="env env-remark">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Remark</span><span class="env-num">4.5</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>This closes a loop opened in Chapter 3. There (§3.3) the same formula \((\ln x)' = 1/x\) was obtained quickly by applying the chain rule to \(e^{\ln x} = x\) — a derivation that <em>presupposes</em> \(\ln\) is differentiable. The proof above presupposes no such thing: it extracts differentiability from the difference quotient, using only the continuity of \(\ln\) (Theorem 4.13) and the derivative of \(e^{x}\) (Theorem 4.8). The chain-rule route is faster and perfectly good once differentiability is known; the inverse-function route is the one that establishes differentiability in the first place, and so is the one the foundations rest on.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Logarithm laws and general powers</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — lead: harvest the algebraic properties; first one more MVT consequence (§C-5, HW1 → Corollary 4.4) -->
  <p>With the derivative in hand we can read off the algebra of the logarithm. The engine is a single extra consequence of the Mean Value Theorem — the companion of the monotonicity corollary, for the borderline case \(f' = 0\).</p>

  <!-- expansion:formula — Corollary 4.4 (§C-5): HW1, f'=0 ⟹ constant -->
  <section class="env env-corollary">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Corollary</span><span class="env-num">4.4</span><span class="env-name">Vanishing derivative</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Suppose \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b)\). If \(f'(c) = 0\) for every \(c \in (a, b)\), then \(f\) is constant on \([a, b]\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Take any two points \(x_{1} &lt; x_{2}\) in \([a, b]\). The Mean Value Theorem (Theorem 4.12) on \([x_{1}, x_{2}]\) supplies a \(c \in (x_{1}, x_{2})\) with</p>
      <p>\[ f(x_{2}) - f(x_{1}) = f'(c)\,(x_{2} - x_{1}) = 0 \cdot (x_{2} - x_{1}) = 0, \]</p>
      <p>so \(f(x_{2}) = f(x_{1})\). As \(x_{1}, x_{2}\) were arbitrary, \(f\) takes a single value throughout \([a, b]\). <span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:formula — Proposition 4.3 (§C-6, add-on taken): HW2 ln(ab)=ln a+ln b promoted to a named result -->
  <section class="env env-proposition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proposition</span><span class="env-num">4.3</span><span class="env-name">Logarithm product law</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>For all \(a, b &gt; 0\),</p>
      <p>\[ \ln(ab) = \ln a + \ln b. \]</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Fix \(a &gt; 0\) and define, for \(x &gt; 0\),</p>
      <p>\[ f(x) = \ln(ax) - \ln x. \]</p>
      <p>By the chain rule together with Theorem 4.14,</p>
      <p>\[ f'(x) = a \cdot \frac{1}{ax} - \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{x} = 0 \qquad \text{for every } x &gt; 0. \]</p>
      <p>So \(f'\) vanishes identically. Applying Corollary 4.4 on the closed interval with endpoints \(1\) and any \(x &gt; 0\) gives \(f(x) = f(1)\). But \(f(1) = \ln a - \ln 1 = \ln a\), since \(\ln 1 = 0\) (because \(e^{0} = 1\)). Hence \(f(x) = \ln a\) for all \(x &gt; 0\); that is, \(\ln(ax) - \ln x = \ln a\). Setting \(x = b\) gives \(\ln(ab) - \ln b = \ln a\), which rearranges to \(\ln(ab) = \ln a + \ln b\). <span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- expansion:example — Example 4.5 (§C-7, HW4): general powers a^x := e^{x ln a}; closes §4.1's deferred a^r question (D6) -->
  <p>The product law is exactly what is needed to define powers with arbitrary real exponents — the question §4.1 raised about \(a^{r}\) for irrational \(r\) and then deferred.</p>

  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">4.5</span><span class="env-name">General powers</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>For \(a &gt; 0\), <em>define</em> the power \(a^{x}\) for every real \(x\) by</p>
        <p>\[ a^{x} = e^{x \ln a}. \]</p>
        <p>This agrees with the usual meaning when \(x\) is rational and extends it to all real \(x\). Verify, for \(a, b &gt; 0\), the two exponent laws</p>
        <p>\[ \text{(i)}\quad a^{x} b^{x} = (ab)^{x}, \qquad\qquad \text{(ii)}\quad (a^{x})^{y} = a^{xy} = (a^{y})^{x}. \]</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>For <strong>(i)</strong>, multiply and collect exponents with the exponent law for \(e\) (Theorem 4.7), then use the product law just proved:</p>
        <p>\[ a^{x} b^{x} = e^{x \ln a}\, e^{x \ln b} = e^{x \ln a + x \ln b} = e^{x(\ln a + \ln b)} = e^{x \ln(ab)} = (ab)^{x}. \]</p>
        <p>For <strong>(ii)</strong>, first note \(\ln(a^{x}) = \ln\!\left(e^{x \ln a}\right) = x \ln a\), using \(\ln(e^{t}) = t\). Therefore</p>
        <p>\[ (a^{x})^{y} = e^{y \ln(a^{x})} = e^{y \,\cdot\, x \ln a} = e^{xy \ln a} = a^{xy}. \]</p>
        <p>The middle expression \(e^{xy \ln a}\) is symmetric in \(x\) and \(y\), so running the same computation with the roles swapped gives \((a^{y})^{x} = e^{xy \ln a} = a^{xy}\) as well. Hence \((a^{x})^{y} = a^{xy} = (a^{y})^{x}\). <span class="qed"></span></p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <!-- expansion:intuition — Remark 4.6 (§C-8, add-on taken): HW3 Cauchy functional equation, one sentence, proof omitted -->
  <section class="env env-remark">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Remark</span><span class="env-num">4.6</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>The exponent law has a converse worth noting: the exponential is the <em>only</em> continuous function \(g \colon \mathbb{R} \to (0, \infty)\) satisfying \(g(x)\,g(y) = g(x + y)\) for all \(x, y\) together with the normalisation \(g(1) = e\); the proof is omitted.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Why these functions are unavoidable</h3>

  <!-- expansion:application [pass: enrichment] — short capstone (§C-10, add-on taken): exponential growth/decay, half-life; ties the chapter's rigour back to why e^x and ln x matter -->
  <p>The defining feature of the exponential, \((e^{x})' = e^{x}\), is exactly what makes it the right function for any quantity whose rate of change is proportional to its current size. If \(y(t)\) obeys \(y'(t) = k\,y(t)\) for a constant \(k\), then \(y(t) = y(0)\,e^{kt}\) — the model behind population growth (\(k &gt; 0\)), radioactive decay (\(k &lt; 0\)), and continuously compounded interest. The logarithm enters the moment one inverts: for positive quantities and \(k \ne 0\), solving \(y(t) = y_{\ast}\) for the time gives \(t = (\ln y_{\ast} - \ln y(0))/k\). The radioactive half-life \(t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lvert k \rvert\) is just the case \(y_{\ast} = y(0)/2\). Everything this chapter built — the series definition, the exponent law, the rigorous derivatives, the inverse construction — is what makes these formulas genuinely well-defined rather than merely suggestive.</p>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Chapter summary</h3>

  <!-- expansion:summary — §C-9: chapter-level 4-strand summary (this is Chapter 4's final section); distillations matching CONTENT_SPEC §4 -->
  <p>Chapter 4 set out to put \(e^{x}\) and \(\ln x\) on rigorous foundations, replacing the informal handling of Chapters 2 and 3 with theorems and proofs. Four strands ran through it.</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>The exponential function.</strong> \(e^{x} := \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^{n}/n!\) is defined by a power series (§4.1), shown convergent for every real \(x\) by geometric tail bounds; it is continuous on \(\mathbb{R}\) and obeys the exponent law \(e^{x}e^{y} = e^{x+y}\) (§4.2); and its derivative is itself, \((e^{x})' = e^{x}\), proved with the explicit bound \(\lvert (e^{h}-1)/h - 1 \rvert \le \lvert h \rvert\) for \(\lvert h \rvert &lt; \tfrac{1}{2}\) (§4.3).</li>
    <li><strong>Real-analysis machinery.</strong> The completeness of \(\mathbb{R}\) — monotone bounded sequences converge — underwrites everything; from it follow the equivalence of convergence and the Cauchy condition (via Bolzano–Weierstrass) used to define \(e^{x}\), and the extreme value theorem used to launch the existence theorems.</li>
    <li><strong>Existence theorems for differentiation.</strong> Theorem A locates the vanishing of the derivative at interior extrema; Rolle's theorem follows; and the Mean Value Theorem follows from Rolle (§4.4). The MVT converts the sign of \(f'\) into monotonicity, and its borderline case \(f' = 0\) into constancy.</li>
    <li><strong>The logarithm.</strong> Because \(e^{x}\) is strictly increasing, it has an inverse \(\ln x\) on \((0, \infty)\) (§4.5); \(\ln\) is continuous, its derivative is \(1/x\), it converts products to sums, and through \(a^{x} = e^{x\ln a}\) it finally gives meaning to arbitrary real powers.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Every property of \(e^{x}\) and \(\ln x\) borrowed on faith in earlier chapters has now been turned into a theorem with a proof, and later chapters may use these functions freely.</p>
