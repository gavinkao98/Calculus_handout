# Direction brief — §6.1 Areas and Distances

Canon variant (Stewart ET 9e §5.1). Lightweight, bullet-level; the ⑤ direction-conformance contract. Written 2026-07-10 per PLAN-ch06.

- **Canon inventory (§5.1):** the *area problem* (approximate the area under \(y=f(x)\) over \([a,b]\) by rectangles — left/right/midpoint sums — then let \(n\to\infty\)); \(\Sigma\) notation for the sums; the *definition of area* as the common limit; the *distance problem* (distance from a velocity record has the same sum structure). Canon worked cases: area under a parabola via a power-sum closed form; distance from a velocity table.
- **Thinness:** canon is *enough* on the two problems + limit setup. *Thin/none*: a fully-worked evaluation of a Riemann-sum limit through the closed-form power sum (needs the App A.3 bridge); the intuition for *why* left and right sums close on the same number; the area↔distance unification. → add one exact area-as-limit example + the unification intuition.
- **Scope & depth:** §6.1 is the **motivation** section — limits of Riemann sums for area and distance. Do **not** define the definite integral or introduce the \(\int\) symbol (→ §6.2; one-line forward fence). Do **not** prove convergence/integrability (→ §6.2 statement + Proof Track). Depth: the chapter is 深理論核心, but §6.1 is the gentle on-ramp — set the sum-and-limit up honestly, state (not prove) that the limit exists for continuous \(f\). **Introduce-before-use:** \(\Sigma\) and finite arithmetic sums are A-class (assumable); **the power sum \(\sum i^2\) is B-class** → a one-line in-place bridge + cross-ref **App §A.3 Prop A.6** *before* the parabola example uses it.
- **Load-bearing intuition (one):** a curved region has no area formula, so **trap** it between rectangle sums we *can* compute and **squeeze** \(n\to\infty\). The collision: naive base×height fails for a curved top. Under- and over-estimating rectangles bracket the true area; refining the partition closes the bracket. Distance repeats the move exactly (constant-velocity legs bracket true distance).
- **Worked examples (real/concrete → diagnostic → construct):**
  - **Ex 6.1** — estimate the area under \(y=x^2\) on \([0,1]\) with \(n=4\) right rectangles (numeric, concrete).
  - **Ex 6.2** — the *exact* area under \(y=x^2\) on \([0,1]\) as \(\lim\) of right sums via \(\sum i^2=\tfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\) (cross-ref App §A.3 Prop A.6); answer \(\tfrac13\).
  - **Ex 6.3** — distance from a velocity table (discrete data) — the twin problem, real-world anchor.
- **History / application:** *application* = distance-from-velocity is the honest real anchor (canon). *history* = one line that area-by-exhaustion is ancient (Archimedes' parabola quadrature already gives the \(\tfrac13\)-type result), `expansion:history [source: standard calculus-textbook historical note]` — motivates the limit-of-sums, non-decorative. Keep light.
