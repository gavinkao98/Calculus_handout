# Direction brief — §6.3 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Canon variant (Stewart ET 9e §5.3). The ⑤ contract. 2026-07-10 per PLAN-ch06. **Chapter crux — 深理論核心, both parts proved on the spot.**

- **Canon inventory (§5.3):** the accumulation function \(g(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\); **FTC Part 1** — \(g'(x)=f(x)\) for continuous \(f\), i.e. \(\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt=f(x)\); **FTC Part 2 (evaluation)** — \(\int_a^b f=F(b)-F(a)\) for any antiderivative \(F\); the antiderivative concept; the closing statement that differentiation and integration are inverse processes.
- **Thinness:** canon is *enough* on the two statements + proofs. *Thin/none*: the accumulation-function intuition (rate of accumulation = current height); a differentiate-an-accumulation example incl. the chain-rule variant and the no-elementary-antiderivative case; evaluation examples that recover §6.1/6.2/6.6 in one line each; a Strategy box for FTC-2.
- **Scope & depth:** **PROVE BOTH PARTS** (深理論核心). FTC-1 via the Extreme Value Theorem (Thm 4.9(a), App D §D.1) + the comparison bound (Thm 6.2 part 4) + the Squeeze Theorem (Thm 1.3) + continuity — the difference quotient \([g(x+h)-g(x)]/h=\frac1h\int_x^{x+h}f\) is trapped between the min and max of \(f\) on \([x,x+h]\), both \(\to f(x)\). FTC-2 via FTC-1 + the constant-difference corollary (Cor 4.4): \(g\) and \(F\) are both antiderivatives, so \(F(b)-F(a)=g(b)-g(a)=\int_a^b f\). **Define the antiderivative (Definition 6.3)** here (Ch5 deferred it), needed for FTC-2. Do **not** develop the indefinite-integral \(\int f\,dx\) family / "\(+C\)" mechanics (→ §6.4, one-line fence), substitution (→ §6.5), or \(\int\frac1x=\ln|x|\) (→ §6.4). Avoid \(1/x\) integrands here.
- **Load-bearing intuition (one):** the accumulation function \(g(x)=\int_a^x f\) grows at the rate of the current height — pushing \(x\) right by \(h\) adds a sliver of area \(\approx f(x)\,h\), so \(g'(x)=f(x)\). "The derivative of the running total is the quantity being totalled." That single fact is FTC-1, and it is *why* antiderivatives evaluate integrals (FTC-2).
- **Worked examples (differentiate → evaluate):**
  - **Ex 6.7** — differentiate accumulation functions: \(\frac{d}{dx}\int_1^x\sqrt{1+t^2}\,dt=\sqrt{1+x^2}\); a chain-rule variant \(\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^{x^3}\cos t\,dt=3x^2\cos(x^3)\); and \(g(x)=\int_0^x e^{-t^2}dt\) with \(g'(x)=e^{-x^2}\) though \(e^{-t^2}\) has no elementary antiderivative (the payoff: FTC-1 differentiates integrals it cannot evaluate).
  - **Ex 6.8** — evaluate with FTC-2, recovering earlier work in one line: \(\int_0^1 x^2\,dx=\bigl[\tfrac{x^3}{3}\bigr]_0^1=\tfrac13\) (vs the §6.2 limit), \(\int_1^4\sqrt{x}\,dx=\bigl[\tfrac23 x^{3/2}\bigr]_1^4=\tfrac{14}{3}\) (inside the §6.6 bracket \([3,6]\)).
  - **Ex 6.9** — one fresh evaluation with a sign check, e.g. \(\int_0^\pi \sin x\,dx=[-\cos x]_0^\pi=2\) (positive, as the area of a hump over \([0,\pi]\) should be).
- **Strategy 6.1** — evaluating a definite integral by FTC-2 (find an antiderivative \(F\); compute \(F(b)-F(a)\); the constant of an antiderivative cancels).
- **history / application:** one line — the FTC is Newton's and Leibniz's central discovery, the inverse link that made calculus a single subject; `expansion:history [source: standard calculus-textbook historical note]`.

- **Emphasis / takeaway:** concept pivot = differentiation and integration are inverse processes; portable skill = differentiate an accumulation function (with the chain rule), and evaluate \(\int_a^b f\) by finding one antiderivative.
