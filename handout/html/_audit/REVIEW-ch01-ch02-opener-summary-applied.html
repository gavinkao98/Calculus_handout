opener · leadoverview
locus：fragments/ch02/sec-2-1.html（檔內第一個 <article>，§2.1 section 之前）· 章標題 Derivatives
概述（lead）

The limit machinery of Chapter 1 was developed in part to make precise the idea of a function approaching a value as its input approaches some target. The principal payoff of that machinery, and the subject of this chapter, is the derivative: an operator that takes a function and returns another function describing how fast it is changing. Geometrically the derivative gives the slope of the tangent line at each point of a curve; physically it gives the instantaneous rate of change of a quantity that varies in time; algebraically it satisfies a small set of arithmetic rules that turn differentiation into a procedure rather than a per-problem invention. We meet the derivative first at a single point, as a limit of secant slopes, then as a function in its own right. A subtler point comes next: its existence at a point implies — but is not implied by — continuity. Finally we treat it as an operator we can compute on polynomials, on the natural exponential, and on products and quotients of differentiable functions.

2 · Ch1 章末 Chapter summary（接 fragments/ch01/sec-1-6.html）

ch01 · summarynarrative recap
locus：fragments/ch01/sec-1-6.html 末（<h3 class="subsec-head">Chapter summary</h3> 後三段）

Chapter 1 laid down two foundations, and they could hardly look less alike. The first was the inverse of a function (§1.1–§1.2). A function can be run backwards exactly when it is one-to-one — distinct inputs give distinct outputs — a condition the horizontal line test reads straight off a graph. For such a function the inverse \(f^{-1}\) exists (Theorem 1.1), pinned down by \(f^{-1}(f(x)) = x\) and \(f(f^{-1}(y)) = y\). Restricting each trigonometric function to a principal interval on which it is one-to-one produced the inverse trigonometric functions \(\arcsin\), \(\arccos\), \(\arctan\) and their three companions, with fixed principal ranges and the reference-triangle method (Strategy 1.2) turning expressions such as \(\cos(\arcsin x)\) into algebra.

The second, and longer, thread was the limit (§1.3–§1.6). We began informally — \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\) means \(f(x)\) is as close to \(L\) as we please once \(x\) is close enough to, but not equal to, \(a\) — and estimated limits from tables and graphs (§1.3). One-sided limits refined the picture, with a two-sided limit existing exactly when the two one-sided limits agree (Proposition 1.5); allowing the values to grow without bound gave infinite limits and the vertical asymptotes that mark them (§1.4). The limit laws (Theorem 1.2) then made limits computable: for functions built from polynomials and roots, direct substitution works wherever the function is defined (Proposition 1.6), and the gaps left by the indeterminate form \(0/0\) yield to factoring, rationalization, or the squeeze theorem (Theorem 1.3) (§1.5).

The precise \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) definition (Definition 1.13) finally placed every preceding limit argument on a rigorous footing (§1.6): \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\) means that for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there is a \(\delta > 0\) with \(\lvert f(x) - L \rvert < \varepsilon\) whenever \(0 < \lvert x - a \rvert < \delta\), and from it we proved that a limit, when it exists, is unique (Proposition 1.7). The chapter closed by naming the property that limits make precise — \(f\) is continuous at \(a\) when \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\) — and it is on this machinery that Chapter 2 is built: the derivative is itself a limit, the limit of secant slopes.

3 · Ch2 章末 Chapter summary（接 fragments/ch02/sec-2-5.html）

ch02 · summarynarrative recap
locus：fragments/ch02/sec-2-5.html 末（<h3 class="subsec-head">Chapter summary</h3> 後四段）

Chapter 2 built the derivative in stages. At a single point it is a limit of secant slopes, \[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}, \] the slope of the tangent line \(y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a)\) and, read locally, the best linear approximation to \(f\) near \(a\) (§2.1). Letting the basepoint vary turns this number into a function \(f'\) — the derivative — recoverable from the graph of \(f\) and written interchangeably as \(f'\), \(\tfrac{df}{dx}\), or \(\tfrac{d}{dx}[f]\) (§2.2).

Not every function has a derivative everywhere. The limit can fail at a corner (\(\lvert x \rvert\) at \(0\)), at a vertical tangent (\(\sqrt[3]{x}\) at \(0\)), or at any discontinuity, so differentiability is a genuinely stronger condition than continuity: differentiable \(\Rightarrow\) continuous (Theorem 2.1), but the converse is false. Iterating the operation gives the higher derivatives \(f''\), \(f'''\), \(\dots\), \(f^{(n)}\) (§2.3).

The rest of the chapter replaced the limit, case by case, with arithmetic. The constant rule \((c)' = 0\) (Theorem 2.2), the power rule \((x^{n})' = n x^{n - 1}\) for positive integers \(n\) (Theorem 2.3), and linearity (Theorem 2.4) together differentiate every polynomial term by term (Corollary 2.1). The natural exponential earned its defining property \((e^{x})' = e^{x}\) (Theorem 2.5), proved here from the series for \(e^{x}\) (§2.4); Chapter 4 will make that step fully rigorous. Products and quotients then closed the toolkit: \[ (fg)' = f'g + fg', \qquad \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^{2}}, \] the first emphatically not \(f'g'\), the second sensitive to the order of its numerator (§2.5).

With these rules we can differentiate any function assembled from powers and the exponential by adding, scaling, multiplying, and dividing. Two things still lie outside their reach: composition, \(f\bigl(g(x)\bigr)\), which no rule here touches, and the power rule itself, so far secured only for integer exponents. Both are the business of Chapter 3, which opens with the rule for compositions — the chain rule — and uses it to extend the power rule and to differentiate the trigonometric, logarithmic, and inverse functions.
