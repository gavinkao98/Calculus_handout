6.2The Definite Integral

Section 6.1 ended on a coincidence too strong to be one: the area under a curve and the distance from a velocity were the same limit of the same kind of sum. A construction that keeps returning deserves a name and a symbol of its own. Here we give it both — the definite integral — and at the same time free it from the assumption that \(f\) is non-negative. For a function that dips below the axis the same limit still makes perfect sense; it simply measures area with a sign, and that signed quantity is what the integral records.

Fix a function \(f\) on \([a, b]\). Exactly as before, cut \([a, b]\) into \(n\) strips of equal width \(\Delta x = (b - a)/n\) by points \(a = x_{0} < x_{1} < \dots < x_{n} = b\); but now, in each strip \([x_{i-1}, x_{i}]\), allow the height to be read at any point \(x_{i}^{*}\) of that strip, not just an endpoint. The resulting sum

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x \]

is called a Riemann sum for \(f\) on \([a, b]\), after Bernhard Riemann. The left, right, and midpoint sums are the special cases \(x_{i}^{*} = x_{i-1}\), \(x_{i}^{*} = x_{i}\), and \(x_{i}^{*} = \tfrac{1}{2}(x_{i-1} + x_{i})\). The definite integral is what these sums approach.

Definition6.2Definite integral

Let \(f\) be defined on \([a, b]\). The definite integral of \(f\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x, \qquad \Delta x = \frac{b - a}{n}, \]

provided this limit exists and takes the same value for every choice of the sample points \(x_{i}^{*}\). When it does, \(f\) is called integrable on \([a, b]\).

The symbol \(\int\), an elongated \(S\) for summa, was Leibniz’s; it keeps the Riemann sum in view even after the limit has erased the individual rectangles. In \(\displaystyle\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx\), the numbers \(a\) and \(b\) are the limits of integration (lower and upper), \(f(x)\) is the integrand, and the \(dx\) marks the variable with respect to which we sum — the surviving trace of the width \(\Delta x\). The integral is a number, not a function of \(x\); the \(x\) inside is a placeholder, so

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx = \int_{a}^{b} f(t)\,dt = \int_{a}^{b} f(u)\,du. \]

Leibniz settled on the elongated \(S\) in 1675, writing the integral as a continuous analogue of a sum of infinitely many infinitely thin rectangles. The notation has outlived the shaky infinitesimals it was born with precisely because it survives translation into the honest limit of Definition 6.2.

To use the integral freely we extend it to the cases \(a = b\) and \(a > b\) by convention, so that the limits of integration may sit in either order:

\[ \int_{a}^{a} f(x)\,dx = 0, \qquad \int_{b}^{a} f(x)\,dx = -\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx. \]

Reversing the limits reverses the sign — natural, since running the strips from \(b\) back to \(a\) turns each width \(\Delta x\) into its negative.

When does the limit exist?

Definition 6.2 asks a great deal: one number approached by every choice of sample points. For a wild enough function the demand fails — recall the caution of §6.1, where samples steered onto and off the misbehaviour of \(f\) gave different answers. What rescues the functions we actually meet is continuity.

Theorem6.1Integrability of continuous functions

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\), then \(f\) is integrable on \([a, b]\): the Riemann sums approach a single limit — the same for every way of slicing \([a, b]\) into strips whose widths shrink to zero, and every choice of sample points within them. The same holds if \(f\) is bounded on \([a, b]\) and continuous there except at finitely many points.

Caution

Theorem 6.1 is the one result of this chapter we do not prove. Its proof turns on a property of a continuous function on a closed interval subtler than continuity at each point — uniform continuity, a single rate of continuity good across the whole interval — which forces the gap between the largest and smallest Riemann sums to shrink to zero as the strips narrow. We take the theorem on credit and use what it grants: for a continuous integrand the limit is always there, and the sample points may be chosen for convenience. This is the honest boundary of the chapter — everything else is earned in full.

Net signed area

When \(f\) can be negative, a strip where \(f(x_{i}^{*}) < 0\) contributes a negative term \(f(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x\) to the Riemann sum. So the integral counts area above the \(x\)-axis as positive and area below it as negative:

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx = \bigl(\text{area above the axis}\bigr) - \bigl(\text{area below the axis}\bigr), \]

the net signed area between the graph and the axis. For a non-negative \(f\) nothing lies below the axis and this reduces to the area of Definition 6.1; the definite integral is the honest generalization.

Example6.4

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_{0}^{1} x^{2}\,dx\) directly from the definition.

Solution

The integrand \(x^{2}\) is continuous, so Theorem 6.1 promises the limit exists and is blind to the choice of sample points; we may therefore use the right-endpoint sums already computed in §6.1 (Example 6.2), where they were found to converge to \(\tfrac{1}{3}\). Hence

\[ \int_{0}^{1} x^{2}\,dx = \frac{1}{3}. \]

What §6.1 called “the area under \(y = x^{2}\)” is now, with the axis label supplied, the value of a definite integral — and because \(x^{2} \ge 0\), the signed area is the plain area.

Example6.5

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_{0}^{2} (x - 1)\,dx\) using net signed area.

Solution

The graph of \(y = x - 1\) is a straight line crossing the axis at \(x = 1\). Over \([0, 1]\) it lies below the axis, bounding a triangle with legs \(1\) and \(1\), of area \(\tfrac{1}{2}\); over \([1, 2]\) it lies above the axis, bounding a congruent triangle of area \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). The integral is the area above minus the area below:

\[ \int_{0}^{2} (x - 1)\,dx = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2} = 0. \]

The two signed pieces cancel exactly. Note the contrast with the total area enclosed between the line and the axis, which is \(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} = 1\): the integral reports the net, not the total.

Example6.6

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} \sqrt{1 - x^{2}}\,dx\) by interpreting it as an area.

Solution

The graph of \(y = \sqrt{1 - x^{2}}\) is the upper half of the unit circle: squaring gives \(y^{2} = 1 - x^{2}\), that is \(x^{2} + y^{2} = 1\), and the requirement \(y \ge 0\) keeps only the top. Over \([-1, 1]\) the region between this graph and the axis is therefore a semicircular disc of radius \(1\), of area \(\tfrac{1}{2}\pi(1)^{2} = \tfrac{\pi}{2}\). Since the integrand is non-negative, the definite integral is exactly that area:

\[ \int_{-1}^{1} \sqrt{1 - x^{2}}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{2}. \]

When an integrand’s graph bounds a region whose area we already know, geometry reads the integral off at sight — no formula to hunt down, no limit of sums.

Properties of the integral

Because the integral is a limit of sums, the arithmetic of sums passes to it in the limit. The following properties hold for continuous \(f\) and \(g\); each is the limit of the corresponding statement about Riemann sums.

Theorem6.2Properties of the definite integral

Let \(f\) and \(g\) be continuous on \([a, b]\) with \(a \le b\); let \(k\) be a constant; and let \(c\) satisfy \(a \le c \le b\).

  1. Constant. \(\displaystyle\int_{a}^{b} k\,dx = k\,(b - a)\).
  2. Linearity. \(\displaystyle\int_{a}^{b}\bigl[f(x) + g(x)\bigr]dx = \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx + \int_{a}^{b} g(x)\,dx\) and \(\displaystyle\int_{a}^{b} k\,f(x)\,dx = k\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx\).
  3. Additivity over intervals. \(\displaystyle\int_{a}^{c} f(x)\,dx + \int_{c}^{b} f(x)\,dx = \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx\).
  4. Comparison. If \(f(x) \ge 0\) on \([a, b]\), then \(\displaystyle\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx \ge 0\). More generally, if \(f(x) \le g(x)\) on \([a, b]\), then \(\displaystyle\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx \le \int_{a}^{b} g(x)\,dx\); and if \(m \le f(x) \le M\) on \([a, b]\), then \(\displaystyle m\,(b - a) \le \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx \le M\,(b - a)\).

The constant and scalar rules are a line each: \(\sum k\,\Delta x = k\,(b - a)\), since the \(n\) equal widths total \(n\,\Delta x = b - a\), and \(\sum k\,f(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x = k \sum f(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x\). For the sum half of linearity, every Riemann sum splits termwise, \(\sum \bigl[f(x_{i}^{*}) + g(x_{i}^{*})\bigr]\Delta x = \sum f(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x + \sum g(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x\), and the limit of a sum is the sum of the limits (limit laws, Theorem 1.2). For comparison, if \(f \ge 0\) then every term \(f(x_{i}^{*})\,\Delta x \ge 0\), so each Riemann sum is \(\ge 0\), and a limit of non-negative numbers is non-negative. Applying this to \(g - f\), which is \(\ge 0\) exactly when \(f \le g\), and using linearity gives \(\int f \le \int g\). Squeezing \(f\) between the constants \(m\) and \(M\) then yields the two-sided bound through the constant rule. Additivity is the geometric fact that the signed area over \([a, b]\) is the signed area over \([a, c]\) together with that over \([c, b]\) — the region divides at the vertical line \(x = c\). (Being continuous on \([a, b]\), \(f\) is continuous on \([a, c]\) and \([c, b]\) as well, so both pieces are themselves integrals; a sum-based proof slices \([a, b]\) with a division point at \(c\), where the Riemann sum splits outright into a part over \([a, c]\) and a part over \([c, b]\), and Theorem 6.1 guarantees the same limit whichever way we slice. The sign convention \(\int_{b}^{a} = -\int_{a}^{b}\) then extends the identity to a \(c\) lying outside \([a, b]\), as long as \(f\) is continuous on the whole interval that \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) span.)

Example6.7

Given that \(\displaystyle\int_{0}^{1} x^{2}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{3}\) (Example 6.4), and without evaluating any new limit, find \(\displaystyle\int_{0}^{1} \bigl(3x^{2} - 4\bigr)\,dx\).

Solution

The properties turn one known integral into many. Linearity (Theorem 6.2, part 2) splits the integral and pulls out the constant factor, and the constant rule (part 1) evaluates the remaining piece directly:

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{0}^{1} \bigl(3x^{2} - 4\bigr)\,dx &= 3\int_{0}^{1} x^{2}\,dx - \int_{0}^{1} 4\,dx \\[2pt] &= 3\cdot\tfrac{1}{3} - 4\,(1 - 0) = 1 - 4 = -3. \end{aligned} \]

No new Riemann sum was evaluated: the single value \(\int_{0}^{1} x^{2} = \tfrac{1}{3}\), run through linearity, settles it. This is how the integral is used in practice — most integrals are reached not from the definition but by reducing them to ones already known.

Example6.8

Show that \(\displaystyle 3 \le \int_{1}^{4} \sqrt{x}\,dx \le 6\) without evaluating the integral.

Solution

Since \(\sqrt{x}\) is continuous on \([1, 4]\), it is integrable there by Theorem 6.1, so the properties apply. On \([1, 4]\) the integrand is increasing, running from \(\sqrt{1} = 1\) up to \(\sqrt{4} = 2\), so \(1 \le \sqrt{x} \le 2\) throughout, and the bounding property (Theorem 6.2, part 4) with \(m = 1\), \(M = 2\), and \(b - a = 3\) gives

\[ 1 \cdot 3 \le \int_{1}^{4} \sqrt{x}\,dx \le 2 \cdot 3, \qquad\text{that is}\qquad 3 \le \int_{1}^{4} \sqrt{x}\,dx \le 6. \]

A single comparison, no limit computed, pins the integral to a modest range. The exact value, \(\tfrac{14}{3} \approx 4.67\), which §6.3 will make painless to find, indeed sits inside.

We now have the definite integral on a firm footing: a number defined as a limit of Riemann sums, guaranteed to exist whenever the integrand is continuous, measuring net signed area, and obeying the linearity, additivity, and comparison rules that make it possible to reason about it. Yet every value we have produced has come the hard way — from a limit of sums, as in Example 6.4, or from a picture, as in Example 6.5. Only the smallest handful of integrals yields to either method. The next section removes the obstacle at a stroke: the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects the definite integral to the derivative, and makes the great majority of integrals painless to evaluate.
