- **Canon inventory (§8.2):** why surface area is subtler than arc length (bands, not chords); cone lateral area \(\pi r \ell\) and the frustum's \(2\pi \bar r \ell\) (\(\bar r\) = average radius); rotate each polygon chord → frustum band; band sum → \(S = \int 2\pi f(x)\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx\) (about the \(x\)-axis); the \(ds\) shorthand \(S=\int 2\pi y\,ds\) (about \(x\)-axis) and \(\int 2\pi x\,ds\) (about \(y\)-axis); worked cases: sphere (recovers \(4\pi r^2\)), standard u-sub curves.
- **Thinness:** canon is thin at exactly the point D4 targets: after MVT converts the chord, the band contributes \(2\pi\,\frac{f(x_{i-1})+f(x_i)}{2}\sqrt{1+f'(\xi_i)^2}\,\Delta x\) — with the height read at THREE different points of the strip. Stewart says "since Δx is small the values are all close" and moves on. **③-D4 final (fence DROPPED — proved on the spot):** the C¹ hypothesis closes the gap directly: \(K=\max_{[a,b]}\lvert f'\rvert\) and \(B=\max_{[a,b]}\sqrt{1+f'^2}\) exist by EVT 4.9(a); MVT 4.12 gives \(\lvert f(x_{i-1})-f(\xi_i)\rvert\le K\Delta x\) and \(\lvert f(x_i)-f(\xi_i)\rvert\le K\Delta x\), hence \(\bigl\lvert\frac{f(x_{i-1})+f(x_i)}{2}-f(\xi_i)\bigr\rvert\le K\Delta x\); the total height-replacement error across all \(n\) bands is \(\le n\cdot 2\pi B K\Delta x^2 = 2\pi BK(b-a)\,\Delta x\to 0\). What remains is an honest Riemann sum of the continuous \(2\pi f\sqrt{1+f'^2}\) → Thm 6.1 + Def 6.2. Zero new fences, zero proof debt (the originally proposed Proof-Track fence had no App D address — ③ blocking #3).
- **Scope & depth:** 標準嚴謹, fully-proved theorem (③-D4 final). Frustum lateral-area lemma: from the cone's unrolled sector get \(\pi r\ell\) (A-class solid geometry, one-line reminder — 108 課綱 S-9 covers 相似形/扇形/圓錐展開; show, don't bare-cite); frustum = big cone minus small cone → \(\pi(r_1+r_2)\ell\) — **and verify the \(r_1=r_2\) cylinder case separately by unrolling a rectangle \(2\pi r\ell\)** (the subtraction route divides by \(r_2-r_1\); the constant-height band is the core degenerate case — ③ blocking #5). Mint **Definition 7.6 (Surface area of revolution)**: \(f\) continuous, \(f\ge 0\) on \([a,b]\), \(a<b\), rotate the graph about the \(x\)-axis; \(S = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum(\text{frustum band areas over the regular partition})\), a finite limit if it exists. **Theorem 7.4 (Surface Area Formula)**: \(f\) smooth on \([a,b]\) (§7.6's C¹ term) and \(f \ge 0\) ⟹ \(S = \int_a^b 2\pi f(x)\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx\). Proof: MVT chord step cited from §7.6 (don't re-derive); the explicit K/B error bound (see Thinness above); Riemann-sum finish via Thm 6.1 + Def 6.2. Then the unified reading \(S=\int 2\pi\rho\,ds\): about the \(x\)-axis \(\rho=y=f(x)\ge0\); about the \(y\)-axis \(\rho=x\) with the curve entirely on one side, \(0\le a<b\) (③ blocking #6: bare \(\int 2\pi x\,ds\) goes negative for \(x<0\), and cross-axis curves double-generate the surface — exclude, one honest clause). Radius = distance to the rotation axis (caution; echoes §7.2/§7.3 radius discipline). **Introduce-before-use:** cone lateral area glossed in place.
- **Load-bearing intuition (one):** a curved surface cannot be flattened, but a thin BAND of it nearly can — into a frustum strip whose area we know exactly. Collision (the honest one, and the reason the definition is band-based): for arc length, chords inscribed in the curve worked; for surface area the innocent-looking analogues can go wrong — the definition must specify WHICH approximants to trust, and it picks the frustum bands generated by the arc's own chords. (Schwarz-lantern digression: NOT taken — one clause at most, per PLAN; default = the definition simply states its approximants, honesty carried by "if the limit exists".) Secondary reading: the integrand \(2\pi f\sqrt{1+f'^2} = (\text{circumference})\times(\text{arc stretch})\) — the band's two length scales (D7: the one slice with TWO scales; call it out).
- **Worked examples (exactly 2, ③-D1; sanity anchor → dual-axis-with-u-sub):**
  - **Ex 7.21 (sphere ZONE — hypothesis-clean sanity anchor)** — rotate \(y=\sqrt{r^2-x^2}\) over \([a,b]\) with \(-r < a < b < r\) about the \(x\)-axis (a <em>zone</em> of the sphere). There \(f'\) IS continuous, Theorem 7.4 applies, and \(f\sqrt{1+f'^2}\) collapses to the constant \(r\) (the algebra is the point — show it): \(S = \int_a^b 2\pi r\,dx = 2\pi r\,(b-a)\) — the zone's area depends only on its WIDTH, not where it sits (Archimedes' hat-box result; `expansion:history [source: standard calculus-textbook historical note]` one-liner). Then a follow-up remark (`expansion:formula`): letting \(a \to -r\), \(b \to r\), the zone areas approach \(2\pi r \cdot 2r = 4\pi r^2\), the classical sphere area — the full-sphere passage is a limit at the poles, where \(f'\) blows up and Theorem 7.4's hypothesis fails; a scrupulous treatment of such endpoint blow-ups arrives with improper integrals in Ch8 (honest fence, one line). RATIONALE (recorded for ⑤): the naive full-sphere computation silently violates the theorem's own hypothesis at \(\pm r\); the zone version is strictly cleaner AND carries the stronger classical punchline, with \(4\pi r^2\) still recovered in the remark.
  - **Ex 7.22 (about the \(y\)-axis + u-sub — one example, two jobs, per ③-D1)** — \(y = x^2\), \(0\le x\le 1\), rotated about the \(y\)-axis: \(S=\int 2\pi x\,ds = 2\pi\int_0^1 x\sqrt{1+4x^2}\,dx\), u-sub \(u=1+4x^2\): \(= \tfrac{\pi}{6}\bigl(5\sqrt5 - 1\bigr)\). Radius = \(x\) even though the curve is given as \(y(x)\) — the dual-axis discipline in action; curve lies in \(x\ge0\) so the one-side hypothesis holds (say so). (The separate \(y=x^3\)-about-\(x\)-axis u-sub example was CUT by ③-D1's ≤2 cap — this example carries both the u-sub and the dual-axis job.)
