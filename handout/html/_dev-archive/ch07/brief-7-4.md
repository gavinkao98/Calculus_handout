# Direction brief — §7.4 Work

Canon variant (Stewart ET 9e §6.4). Lightweight, bullet-level; the ⑤ direction-conformance contract. Written 2026-07-18 per PLAN-ch07 (D6: SI primary, exactly 3 examples).

- **Canon inventory (§6.4):** constant-force work \(W = Fd\) (the physics input); variable force along a line → slice the displacement, \(W \approx \sum F(x_i^*)\Delta x\) → \(W=\int_a^b F(x)\,dx\) (definition); units (J = N·m; ft-lb); Hooke's law \(F(x)=kx\) and spring problems (find \(k\) from data, then integrate); lifting a cable/rope (slice the CABLE, each piece travels its own distance); pumping water from a tank (slice the WATER by depth, each slab lifted its own height).
- **Thinness:** canon is *enough* on mechanics and the three classic setups. *Thin*: what changes between the three setups — in the spring the FORCE varies along one displacement; in the cable/tank each SLICE OF STUFF travels its own constant distance. These are two different slicings that both land in \(\int F\,dx\)-shaped integrals, and canon lets students pattern-match without seeing the fork. → name the fork explicitly (PLAN run-guard: each solution names WHAT is sliced before integrating; the section's transferable skill).
- **Scope & depth:** 標準嚴謹, modeling register. Mint **Definition 7.3 (Work)**: for a continuous force \(F\) along the \(x\)-axis from \(a\) to \(b\), \(W=\int_a^b F(x)\,dx\) — the constant-force product is the physics input (A-class), the integral is the slice-and-sum upgrade; existence via Thm 6.1 (one clause). Net-change echo: \(W\) accumulates like §6.4's net change — one line, not a re-derivation. Hooke's law stated as the empirical spring model with source tag (`[source: Stewart ET 9e §6.4 / standard physics]`, honest empirical status: valid for small displacements); **③ advisory #10 discipline**: \(F(x)=kx\) is the *applied* force needed to hold the spring stretched \(x\) beyond natural length (the spring's own restoring force is \(-kx\)) — the example computes work done *against* the spring, and Definition 7.3 frames \(F\) as the signed force component along the displacement direction. Units: SI primary (N, m, J); ONE parenthetical ft-lb mention at the units paragraph, no ft-lb example (D6). Water weight density: give \(\rho g \approx 9800\ \text{N/m}^3\) in the pumping example (\(\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3\), \(g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\)) — stated in-example, units tracked line by line. **Introduce-before-use:** force/energy units are new to the book but A-class physics vocabulary at 108 課綱 (physics is not assumed though — define J = N·m in place, treat "work = force × distance for constant force" as the modeling input we adopt, one gloss line).
- **Load-bearing intuition (one):** work is force × distance ONLY while the force is constant — the moment it varies, the product is meaningless and the slicing schema is forced on us. Collision: stretching a spring, the last centimetre costs more than the first (the force has grown); one product cannot price the whole stretch, but on a thin slice of displacement the force is nearly frozen and the product is honest again. Same schema, new quantity: value × width where value = force.
- **Worked examples (exactly 3, D6; the slicing-fork is the arc):**
  - **Ex 7.12 (spring)** — a spring with natural length such that a force of \(30\) N holds it stretched \(0.1\) m: find \(k = 300\) N/m from Hooke's law, then the work to stretch from \(0.1\) m to \(0.2\) m beyond natural length: \(W=\int_{0.1}^{0.2}300x\,dx = 150(0.04-0.01)=4.5\) J. (Slices displacement; force varies.)
  - **Ex 7.13 (cable)** — a \(20\) m cable weighing \(2\) N per metre hangs from a winch; work to wind it all up: slice the CABLE at depth \(x\) below the winch, slice weighs \(2\,dx\) N and rises \(x\) m, so \(W=\int_0^{20}2x\,dx=400\) J. (Slices the object; each slice's lift distance varies, its force constant.)
  - **Ex 7.14 (pumping)** — a tank shaped as an inverted circular cone (vertex down), height \(4\) m, top radius \(2\) m, filled with water to depth \(3\) m; pump all the water over the TOP rim: coordinate \(y\) from the vertex up; slab at height \(y\) is a disk of radius \(y/2\) (similar triangles — §7.2 echo), volume \(\pi y^2/4\,dy\), weight \(9800\,\pi y^2/4\,dy\) N, lifted \(4-y\) m; \(W=\int_0^3 9800\,\frac{\pi y^2}{4}(4-y)\,dy = 2450\pi\bigl[\tfrac{4y^3}{3}-\tfrac{y^4}{4}\bigr]_0^3 = 2450\pi\cdot\tfrac{63}{4}\) — evaluate exactly \(= \tfrac{154350\pi}{4} = 38587.5\pi \approx 1.21\times10^5\) J. (Slices the water by depth; combines §7.2 cross-sections with the work schema — the chapter compounding.)
