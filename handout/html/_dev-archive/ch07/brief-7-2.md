# Direction brief — §7.2 Volumes

Canon variant (Stewart ET 9e §6.2). Lightweight, bullet-level; the ⑤ direction-conformance contract. Written 2026-07-18 per PLAN-ch07.

- **Canon inventory (§6.2):** cylinder \(V = Ah\) as the base case; general solid sliced into slabs → \(\sum A(x_i^*)\Delta x\) → \(V=\int_a^b A(x)\,dx\) (definition of volume); solids of revolution: disks (\(A=\pi f(x)^2\)); washers (\(A=\pi(R^2-r^2)\)); rotation about lines other than the axis (radius = distance to the axis of rotation); non-revolution solids by cross-sections (pyramid, wedge). Canon worked cases: sphere \(\tfrac43\pi r^3\); \(y=\sqrt x\) disk; washer between two curves; pyramid \(\tfrac13 b^2 h\).
- **Thinness:** canon is *enough* on the slab derivation + instances. *Thin*: the definition-status of the formula (volume is *defined* by the integral — the modeling step deserves daylight, matching the book's honest-construction ethos); the consistency checks (constant cross-section recovers \(V=Ah\); sphere/pyramid recover classical solid geometry — the same sanity-anchor move as Ch6's semicircle Ex 6.6). → make the modeling step explicit and let two examples double as sanity anchors.
- **Scope & depth:** 標準嚴謹. Mint **Definition 7.2** (volume by cross-sections; \(A\) continuous; existence of the limit via **Thm 6.1**, cite explicitly; modeling input = the slab≈cylinder step, stated as such). Disks and washers are *instances* — compute \(A(x)\) from the geometry, then apply the definition; NO separate "disk theorem". Washer subtraction \(\pi(R^2-r^2)\) grounded in Thm 6.2 linearity at the cross-section level (one line). **Introduce-before-use:** nothing B-class (circle area, similar triangles = A-class).
- **Load-bearing intuition (one):** a solid is measured the way a loaf is sliced: each thin slab is nearly a cylinder — face area \(A(x)\), thickness \(\Delta x\) — and \(V=Ah\) for cylinders is the only volume fact geometry hands us. Collision: a curved solid is *not* a cylinder anywhere, yet every thin slab almost is; the definition turns "almost" into a limit. The 2D→3D upgrade: §7.1's strip gap becomes a slab face.
- **Worked examples (sanity anchor → basic disk → washer → construct):**
  - **Ex 7.5** — sphere of radius \(r\) via disks: \(A(x)=\pi(r^2-x^2)\), even integrand → Thm 6.8; recovers \(\tfrac43\pi r^3\) (sanity anchor #1).
  - **Ex 7.6** — region under \(y=\sqrt x\) on \([0,4]\) about the \(x\)-axis: \(V=\int_0^4 \pi x\,dx=8\pi\) (basic disk mechanics).
  - **Ex 7.7** — region between \(y=x\) and \(y=x^2\) about the \(x\)-axis: washers, \(V=\pi\int_0^1(x^2-x^4)\,dx=\tfrac{2\pi}{15}\); closing line reads off the shifted-axis variant (about \(y=2\): radii become distances \(2-x^2\), \(2-x\)) WITHOUT a full second computation — deliberate compression, recorded here; M4 may upgrade to a full example if gap-check flags it.
  - **Ex 7.8** — square pyramid, base side \(b\), height \(h\), by similar-triangle cross-sections: \(s(x)=\tfrac{b}{h}x\) (apex at origin, axis along \(x\)), \(V=\int_0^h \tfrac{b^2}{h^2}x^2\,dx=\tfrac13 b^2 h\) (sanity anchor #2; non-revolution case).
- **History / application:** *history* = one-line Cavalieri remark (equal cross-sections ⟹ equal volumes; seventeenth-century principle, `[source: standard calculus-textbook historical note]`) — earns its place as the definition's classical echo, non-decorative. No application block (the section's examples are already geometric payoff; work/physics arrives in §7.4).

- **Emphasis / takeaway:** concept pivot = *volume is the integral of cross-sectional area* — the slicing schema one dimension up; portable skill = choose a slicing axis, express \(A(x)\) from the geometry (disk, washer, or direct), integrate.
