# Direction brief — §7.5 Average Value of a Function

Canon variant (Stewart ET 9e §6.5). Lightweight, bullet-level; the ⑤ direction-conformance contract. Written 2026-07-18 per PLAN-ch07 (D2: mint the Mean Value Theorem for Integrals with full proof — the seam-ledger premise "積分均值(EVT+IVT)" closes HERE).

- **Canon inventory (§6.5):** finite average of \(n\) numbers → sample \(f\) at \(n\) points → the sampled mean IS a Riemann sum divided by \((b-a)\) → \(f_{\text{ave}} = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) (definition); worked averages (e.g. \(1+x^2\) on \([-1,2]\), temperature); the Mean Value Theorem for Integrals (\(f\) continuous ⟹ \(f(c)=f_{\text{ave}}\) for some \(c\)); geometric reading: the mean-height rectangle over \([a,b]\) has the same area as the region under \(f\).
- **Thinness:** canon is *enough* on the definition route and examples; Stewart's MVT-for-Integrals proof leans on FTC + MVT. *Thin/decision*: this book proves it the EVT+IVT way (D2) — closes the seam premise with the machinery App D built (EVT was proved FOR this); the FTC route becomes a one-line remark. Also thin in canon: the average's dependence on the interval (students read "average of \(f\)" as intrinsic to the formula) — run-guard A6: caution line + a worked example computing the same \(f\) over two intervals.
- **Scope & depth:** 標準嚴謹 with one honest proof (the chapter's first full theorem-proof). Mint **Definition 7.4** (average value; \(f\) continuous on \([a,b]\), **\(a<b\)** — no reversed-limits convention imported into averages) and **Theorem 7.2 (Mean Value Theorem for Integrals)** (same hypotheses; conclusion \(c\in[a,b]\)) with proof per ③-D2 final: EVT 4.9(a) gives attained \(m = f(x_m)\), \(M = f(x_M)\); comparison Thm 6.2(4) gives \(m(b-a) \le \int_a^b f \le M(b-a)\), i.e. \(m \le f_{\text{ave}} \le M\); **case \(m=M\)**: \(f\) is constant and any \(c\) serves; **case \(m<M\)**: set \(\alpha=\min\{x_m,x_M\}\), \(\beta=\max\{x_m,x_M\}\) and apply IVT 4.9(b) on \([\alpha,\beta]\subseteq[a,b]\) — no ordering assumption on \(x_m, x_M\). FTC-route alternative: one-line remark naming Thm 6.3 + Thm 4.12, NO second proof. **Introduce-before-use:** arithmetic mean = A-class; nothing B-class new.
- **Load-bearing intuition (one):** an average of infinitely many values cannot be "add them all and divide" — there is no \(n\) to divide by. Collision: try to average a temperature over a day from readings — every finite sampling gives a slightly different mean; refine the sampling and the means settle. The settled value is \(\frac{1}{b-a}\int f\): the integral IS the "sum of all values", and \(b-a\) IS the "how many" — width standing in for count. Geometric shadow: level the region under \(f\) into a rectangle on the same base; its height is the average.
- **Worked examples (mechanics → interval-dependence → attainment):**
  - **Ex 7.15** — \(f(x) = 1 + x^2\) on \([-1, 2]\): \(f_{\text{ave}} = \tfrac13\int_{-1}^2(1+x^2)\,dx = \tfrac13\bigl[x + \tfrac{x^3}{3}\bigr]_{-1}^2 = \tfrac13(6) = 2\) (canon's clean opener).
  - **Ex 7.16** — same \(f\) on \([0, 1]\): \(f_{\text{ave}} = \int_0^1(1+x^2)\,dx = \tfrac43\) — the average moved because the interval did (run-guard A6 made concrete; pairs with the caution).
  - **Ex 7.17** — attainment: for \(f = 1+x^2\) on \([-1,2]\), find the promised \(c\): solve \(1 + c^2 = 2\), \(c = \pm 1\), both in \([-1,2]\) — Theorem 7.2 promises at least one, here there are two (uniqueness is NOT claimed; say so). Geometric readout: the rectangle of height 2 over \([-1,2]\) has area 6 = the integral.
