# Direction brief — §6.2 The Definite Integral

Canon variant (Stewart ET 9e §5.2). The ⑤ direction-conformance contract. 2026-07-10 per PLAN-ch06.

- **Canon inventory (§5.2):** the definite integral \(\int_a^b f\,dx\) defined as the limit of Riemann sums for a general (not necessarily non-negative) continuous \(f\); the integral notation (sign, limits of integration, integrand, \(dx\)); the *integrability theorem* (continuous ⟹ integrable) stated; **net signed area** (area above the axis minus area below); the sign/empty conventions \(\int_a^a=0\), \(\int_b^a=-\int_a^b\); the properties of the integral (constant, linearity, additivity over intervals, comparison/bounds).
- **Thinness:** canon is *enough* on the definition + property list. *Thin/none*: an evaluate-from-the-definition example (reuse §6.1's \(x^2\) limit, now named an integral); a net-signed-area example done by geometry; a bound-without-evaluating example using the min/max property; short proofs of the easy properties from Riemann sums.
- **Scope & depth:** **now** introduce the \(\int\) symbol and Definition 6.2 (deferred from §6.1). - **Load-bearing intuition (one):** the definite integral is *the number the Riemann sums converge to* — and continuity is exactly what guarantees they converge to one number no matter how the sample points are chosen; for a sign-changing \(f\) that number is **net signed area**.
- **Worked examples (name → build → bound):**
  - **Ex 6.4** — evaluate \(\int_0^1 x^2\,dx\) straight from the definition, citing §6.1's right-sum limit \(=\tfrac13\), now christened an integral (ties §6.2 back to §6.1).
  - **Ex 6.5** — \(\int_0^2 (x-1)\,dx\) by geometry: a triangle of area \(\tfrac12\) *below* the axis on \([0,1]\) and one of area \(\tfrac12\) *above* on \([1,2]\), so the net signed area is \(0\). Showcases signed area.
  - **Ex 6.6** — bound \(\int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\,dx\) *without evaluating* via \(m(b-a)\le\int\le M(b-a)\): \(\sqrt{x}\in[1,2]\) on \([1,4]\), so \(3\le\int_1^4\sqrt{x}\,dx\le 6\).
- **history / application:** one line on Leibniz's integral sign (an elongated *S* for *summa*, 1675) — `expansion:history [source: standard calculus-textbook historical note]`, motivates the "sum" reading. Keep light.

- **Emphasis / takeaway:** concept pivot = the definite integral is a *number* (a limit of sums), = net signed area; portable skill = read/write integral notation, evaluate simple integrals from the definition or by area, and bound one with the comparison property.
