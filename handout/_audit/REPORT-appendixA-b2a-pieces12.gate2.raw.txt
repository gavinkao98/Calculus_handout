22:    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">A.3</span>Sigma Notation and the Geometric Series</h2>
23-  </header>
24-
25-  <p>Before the geometric series itself, a word on the notation that names it and a few moves for handling sums that the main text uses without fuss. The capital-sigma symbol \(\sum\) abbreviates a sum: \(\sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k}\) means \(a_{1} + a_{2} + \cdots + a_{n}\), the terms \(a_{k}\) added as the <em>index</em> \(k\) climbs through the whole numbers from the lower limit to the upper. The index is only a placeholder — \(\sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k}\) and \(\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{j}\) denote the very same sum — and an upper limit of \(\infty\), as in \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_{k}\), signals a sum that never stops, to be read as the limit of its running totals in the sense settled just below.</p>
26-
27-  <p>Three moves recur throughout the calculus chapters. <em>Re-indexing</em> renames the counter without disturbing the sum: setting \(j = k - 1\) rewrites \(\sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k}\) as \(\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} a_{j+1}\), the same terms relabelled. <em>Peeling</em> detaches the first or last term, as in \(\sum_{k=0}^{n} a_{k} = a_{0} + \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k}\). And <em>splitting at a cutoff</em> \(n_{0}\) breaks a sum into a leading part and a <em>tail</em>,
28-  \[ \sum_{k=0}^{n} a_{k} \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{n_{0}} a_{k} \;+\; \sum_{k=n_{0}+1}^{n} a_{k}, \]
29-  the move on which §4.1–§4.2 rest, where a series is controlled by bounding its tail while the leading partial sum is set aside.</p>
30-
31-  <p>One special sum collapses outright. When each term is the <em>difference</em> of consecutive values of a sequence \(b_{k}\), the interior pieces cancel in pairs:
32-  \[ \sum_{k=0}^{m} \bigl(b_{k} - b_{k+1}\bigr) = b_{0} - b_{m+1}, \]
33:  since every \(b_{k}\) with \(1 \le k \le m\) is added once and subtracted once. A sum that collapses this way is said to <em>telescope</em>, after the way a spyglass folds shut — and it is the exact mechanism behind the finite geometric-sum formula below.</p>
34-
35:  <p>Those moves are all finite bookkeeping. Adding up <em>infinitely</em> many terms is a genuinely new act, and most of its theory belongs to a later chapter of calculus. But one infinite sum is so simple, and so useful so early, that it deserves to be settled now: the geometric series, in which each term is a fixed multiple \(r\) of the one before. The main text leans on it in §4.1, where the <em>tails</em> — the leftover terms past a cutoff — of the series defining \(e^{x}\) are trapped under geometric tails with ratio \(r = \tfrac{1}{2}\). Everything rests on one finite identity plus one limit.</p>

40-      <p>For any number \(r \neq 1\) and any positive integer \(m\),
41-      \[ 1 + r + r^{2} + \cdots + r^{m} = \frac{1 - r^{m+1}}{1 - r}. \]</p>
42-    </div>

54-  <p>Now let the sum run on. If \(\lvert r \rvert < 1\), each multiplication by \(r\) shrinks the next term by a fixed factor, and the power \(r^{m+1}\) in the formula dies away: its size \(\lvert r \rvert^{m+1}\) drops below any positive threshold once \(m\) is large enough. In the arrow notation of §1.3 (here the whole-number index \(m\) runs off to infinity, rather than a variable approaching a finite point), \(r^{m+1} \to 0\) as \(m \to \infty\), so the running totals
55-  \[ S_{m} = \frac{1 - r^{m+1}}{1 - r} \qquad\text{settle toward}\qquad \frac{1 - 0}{1 - r} = \frac{1}{1 - r}. \]
56-  The value of an infinite sum <em>means</em> exactly this: the limit of its running totals. (That reading is made official in §4.2, where the running totals go by the name <em>partial sums</em>. The completeness facts behind general convergence arguments are the business of §4.1–§4.2; none of them are needed for the simple limit here.)</p>

17-    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">A.2</span>The Product-to-Sum and Sum-to-Product Identities</h2>
18-  </header>
19-
20:  <p>One algebraic step in this book carries more weight than any other: rewriting the difference \(\sin(x+h) - \sin x\) as a <em>product</em>, which is what makes the derivative of the sine function computable in §3.1. The identity that does it belongs to a family — the product-to-sum and sum-to-product identities — that older curricula drilled and most current precalculus courses omit. The good news is that the whole family is nothing but the angle-sum formulas, added and subtracted in pairs. If you learn one thing from this section, learn how to <em>rebuild</em> the identities; the memorizing then becomes optional.</p>
21-
22-  <p>Everything starts from the four angle-sum and angle-difference formulas, which we assume and record here for reference:
23-  \[ \begin{aligned} \sin(u + v) &= \sin u \cos v + \cos u \sin v, \\ \sin(u - v) &= \sin u \cos v - \cos u \sin v, \\ \cos(u + v) &= \cos u \cos v - \sin u \sin v, \\ \cos(u - v) &= \cos u \cos v + \sin u \sin v. \end{aligned} \]</p>
24-
25-  <p>Add or subtract these in matching pairs and half of each right-hand side cancels, leaving a single product.</p>
26-
27-  <section class="env env-proposition">
28-    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proposition</span><span class="env-num">A.2</span><span class="env-name">Product-to-sum identities</span></p>
--

34-  <section class="env env-proof">
35-    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
36-    <div class="env-body">
37-      <p>Add the two sine formulas: the \(\cos u \sin v\) terms cancel, leaving \(\sin(u+v) + \sin(u-v) = 2 \sin u \cos v\); divide by \(2\). Add the two cosine formulas: the \(\sin u \sin v\) terms cancel, giving \(\cos(u+v) + \cos(u-v) = 2 \cos u \cos v\). Subtract the cosine formulas instead — \(\cos(u-v) - \cos(u+v)\) — and the \(\cos u \cos v\) terms cancel, giving \(2 \sin u \sin v\). <span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
38-    </div>
39-  </section>
40-
41:  <p>Setting \(u = v = \theta\) in these product-to-sum identities pays an immediate dividend. The middle line becomes \(\cos^{2}\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[\cos 2\theta + \cos 0\bigr]\) and the last becomes \(\sin^{2}\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[\cos 0 - \cos 2\theta\bigr]\); since \(\cos 0 = 1\), these are the <em>power-reduction</em> formulas
42-  \[ \cos^{2}\theta = \frac{1 + \cos 2\theta}{2}, \qquad \sin^{2}\theta = \frac{1 - \cos 2\theta}{2}, \]
43-  which trade a <em>squared</em> trigonometric function for a plain one at twice the angle. (The first line, \(\sin\theta \cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}\sin 2\theta\), is the double-angle formula for sine read the same way.) These are the forms integration reaches for to handle \(\sin^{2}\) and \(\cos^{2}\) when the techniques of integration arrive.</p>
44-
45-  <p>The sum-to-product identities are the same cancellations read in the other direction. The only new idea is a change of names: given a sum like \(\sin A + \sin B\), arrange for the two angles to read \(u + v\) and \(u - v\) by choosing \(u\) and \(v\) so that \(u + v = A\) and \(u - v = B\). Adding these two equations gives \(2u = A + B\) and subtracting them gives \(2v = A - B\), so \(u\) is the <em>average</em> and \(v\) the <em>half-difference</em>:
46-  \[ u = \frac{A + B}{2}, \qquad v = \frac{A - B}{2}. \]</p>

  <p>The plan is to trap the tail of the series \(\sum x^{n}/n!\) inside a geometric series. Once the tail is controlled, the partial sums form a non-decreasing sequence that is bounded above, and Theorem 4.1 hands us the limit. The technique is worth isolating, because it returns several times in this section and the next.</p>

  <!-- expansion:strategy ??name the geometric-tail technique once; reused in the 禮4.1 convergence proof and throughout 禮4.2 -->
  <section class="env env-strategy">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Strategy</span><span class="env-num">4.1</span><span class="env-name">Bounding a series by a geometric tail</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>To show that a series of positive terms \(\sum a_{n}\) converges:</p>
      <ol class="steps">
        <li>Find an index \(n_{0}\) beyond which the ratio of consecutive terms is at most some constant \(r < 1\): that is, \(a_{n + 1}/a_{n} \le r\) for \(n \ge n_{0}\).</li>
        <li>Conclude that the terms decay geometrically: \(a_{n} \le a_{n_{0}} \, r^{\,n - n_{0}}\) for \(n \ge n_{0}\).</li>
        <li>Bound the tail by the geometric series \(\sum_{n > n_{0}} a_{n} \le a_{n_{0}} \sum_{k \ge 1} r^{k} = a_{n_{0}} \cdot \dfrac{r}{1 - r} < \infty\). (If the infinite geometric sum is new to you: the finite sum is \(r + r^{2} + \cdots + r^{m} = \frac{r(1 - r^{m})}{1 - r}\) ??multiply by \(1 - r\) and watch the telescoping ??and since \(0 < r < 1\), the power \(r^{m}\) dies away as \(m\) grows, leaving \(\frac{r}{1 - r}\). Appendix A.3 gives the full account.)</li>
        <li>The partial sums are then bounded above, and (being a sum of positive terms) non-decreasing, so Theorem 4.1 gives the limit.</li>
      </ol>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-theorem">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Theorem</span><span class="env-num">4.2</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>For every \(x > 0\), the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{n}}{n!}\) converges to a finite real number.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <p><em>Proof track: this argument earns the theorem. Later sections use the convergence itself and the tail bound \((*)\) recorded just after the proof ??on a first reading you may take both and move on.</em></p>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Fix \(x > 0\) and choose a positive integer \(n_{0}\) with \(n_{0} > 2x\). The ratio of successive terms is
      \[ \frac{x^{n + 1}/(n + 1)!}{x^{n}/n!} = \frac{x}{n + 1}, \]
      and for \(n \ge n_{0}\) this is at most \(\dfrac{x}{n_{0} + 1} < \dfrac{x}{n_{0}} < \dfrac{1}{2}\), by the choice of \(n_{0}\). Iterating from \(n_{0}\), every later term obeys
      \[ \frac{x^{n}}{n!} \le \frac{x^{n_{0}}}{n_{0}!} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{n - n_{0}} \qquad (n > n_{0}). \]
      Split a partial sum at the index \(n_{0}\):
      \[ \sum_{n = 0}^{N} \frac{x^{n}}{n!} = \underbrace{\sum_{n = 0}^{n_{0}} \frac{x^{n}}{n!}}_{\text{(I)}} + \underbrace{\sum_{n = n_{0} + 1}^{N} \frac{x^{n}}{n!}}_{\text{(II)}}. \]
      Piece (I) is a fixed finite sum. Piece (II) is bounded, using the geometric estimate above, by
      \[ \text{(II)} \le \frac{x^{n_{0}}}{n_{0}!} \sum_{n = n_{0} + 1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{n - n_{0}} = \frac{x^{n_{0}}}{n_{0}!}. \]
      Therefore
      \[ \sum_{n = 0}^{N} \frac{x^{n}}{n!} \le \sum_{n = 0}^{n_{0}} \frac{x^{n}}{n!} + \frac{x^{n_{0}}}{n_{0}!} \qquad \text{for every } N > n_{0}. \]
      The right-hand side is a fixed real number, independent of \(N\). The partial sums on the left are non-decreasing (each new term is positive) and bounded above, so by Theorem 4.1 the series converges to a finite real number. <span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

      <p>Set \(S_{n} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{k}\) and assume \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \lvert a_{k} \rvert < \infty\). By Theorem 4.5 it suffices to show \(\{S_{n}\}\) is Cauchy. Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), convergence of the absolute series gives an \(N_{0}\) with \(\sum_{k = N_{0} + 1}^{\infty} \lvert a_{k} \rvert < \varepsilon\) ??the tail is the gap between the full sum and the \(N_{0}\)-th partial sum, and partial sums close that gap. Then for any \(m > n \ge N_{0}\), the triangle inequality yields</p>
      <p>\[ \lvert S_{m} - S_{n} \rvert = \Bigl\lvert \sum_{k = n + 1}^{m} a_{k} \Bigr\rvert \le \sum_{k = n + 1}^{m} \lvert a_{k} \rvert \le \sum_{k = N_{0} + 1}^{\infty} \lvert a_{k} \rvert < \varepsilon. \]</p>
      <p>So \(\{S_{n}\}\) is Cauchy, hence convergent, which is what it means for \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{k}\) to converge. <span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>

  <h3 class="subsec-head">Extending \(e^{x}\) to all of \(\mathbb{R}\)</h3>

  <!-- expansion:intuition ??use Proposition 4.1 to define e^x for x<0; record the absolute-value tail bound (**) that upgrades (*) to all real x -->
  <p>The convergence test does exactly the job we set it. For any real \(x\), the series of absolute values is \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \lvert x \rvert^{n}/n! = e^{\lvert x \rvert}\), which is finite by Theorem 4.2 (or equals \(1\) when \(x = 0\)). Proposition 4.1 then guarantees that \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^{n}/n!\) itself converges. This defines</p>

  <!-- expansion:formula ??the definition now covers all of ?? agreeing with Definition 4.1 for x>0 -->
  <p>\[ e^{x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{n}}{n!} \qquad \text{for every } x \in \mathbb{R}, \]</p>

  <p>in agreement with Definition 4.1 when \(x > 0\), and giving \(e^{0} = 1\). Moreover the tail bound upgrades to absolute values with no extra work: for \(\lvert x \rvert \le L\) and any \(k > n_{0} > 2L\), bounding each term of the remainder by its absolute value and reusing the geometric-tail estimate of \((*)\) (Strategy 4.1) gives</p>

  <!-- expansion:formula ??absolute-value form of the 禮4.1 tail bound, valid for all |x| ??L -->
  <p>\[ \lvert e^{x} - P_{k}(x) \rvert \le \sum_{n = k + 1}^{\infty} \frac{\lvert x \rvert^{n}}{n!} \le \frac{L^{n_{0}}}{n_{0}!} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{k - n_{0}}. \tag{$**$} \]</p>

  <p>The bound \((**)\) is the two-sided workhorse for the rest of the section: it is uniform over the whole symmetric interval \(\lvert x \rvert \le L\) and shrinks geometrically in \(k\). With it, the continuity proof for \(x > 0\) extends verbatim to every real base point.</p>
