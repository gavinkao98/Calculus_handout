  <header class="sec-head">
    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">A.5</span>The Conic Sections</h2>
  </header>

  <p>Slice a cone with a flat plane and the rim of the cut is a <em>conic section</em>: a circle, an ellipse, a parabola, or a hyperbola, according to the angle of the slice. Each also has a cleaner description in terms of distance, and each has a <em>standard equation</em> ??the simplest form, obtained by placing the curve's centre or vertex at the origin. This tour records the four standard forms and the features to read straight off them. Like partial fractions, the conics are stocked here for later chapters; the main text of Chapters 1?? does not call on them.</p>

  <p><strong>The circle.</strong> A circle is the set of points a fixed distance \(r\) ??the <em>radius</em> ??from a centre. Centred at the origin, the condition \(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r\) squares to the standard equation
  \[ x^2 + y^2 = r^2. \]</p>

  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-circle">
    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.1</span> A circle of radius \(r\) centred at the origin: \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\).</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <p><strong>The parabola.</strong> Fix a point \(F\) (the <em>focus</em>) and a line (the <em>directrix</em>). A parabola is the set of points equidistant from the two. With focus \(F = (0, p)\) and directrix \(y = -p\), equating the two distances ??\(x^2 + (y - p)^2 = (y + p)^2\) ??and simplifying gives the standard equation
  \[ x^2 = 4py, \]
  a curve opening upward from its vertex at the origin. (Putting the focus on the \(x\)-axis instead gives the sideways form \(y^2 = 4px\).)</p>

  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-parabola">
    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.2</span> A parabola \(x^2 = 4py\), with focus \(F = (0, p)\) and directrix \(y = -p\).</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <p><strong>The ellipse.</strong> Fix two points \(F_1, F_2\) (the <em>foci</em>). An ellipse is the set of points whose distances to the two foci add to a fixed total. Placing the foci at \((\pm c, 0)\) and writing the total as \(2a\) leads to the standard equation
  \[ \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad b^2 = a^2 - c^2, \]
  with \(a > b > 0\). The curve crosses the axes at \((\pm a, 0)\) and \((0, \pm b)\); the longer semi-axis \(a\) runs along the line through the foci, and \(c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}\) locates them.</p>

  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-ellipse">
    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.3</span> An ellipse \(\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1\) with foci \(F_1, F_2\) at \((\pm c, 0)\), where \(c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}\).</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <p><strong>The hyperbola.</strong> Keep two foci, but now ask for a fixed <em>difference</em> of distances rather than a sum. With foci at \((\pm c, 0)\) and the difference written \(2a\), the standard equation is
  \[ \frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad b^2 = c^2 - a^2. \]
  The curve has two branches with vertices at \((\pm a, 0)\), and far from the centre it draws arbitrarily close to the two <em>asymptotes</em> \(y = \pm\frac{b}{a}x\), the crossed lines that govern its opening. Here \(c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\), so the foci lie beyond the vertices.</p>

  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-hyperbola">
    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.4</span> A hyperbola \(\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1\), with vertices \((\pm a, 0)\), foci \((\pm c, 0)\), and asymptotes \(y = \pm\frac{b}{a}x\).</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">A.6</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Identify the curve \(4x^2 + 9y^2 = 36\) and locate its foci.</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Divide through by \(36\) to reach standard form:
        \[ \frac{x^2}{9} + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1. \]
        Both squared terms are added, so the curve is an ellipse, with \(a^2 = 9\) and \(b^2 = 4\) ??that is \(a = 3\) and \(b = 2\). Since \(a > b\), the major axis is horizontal, and \(c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2} = \sqrt{9 - 4} = \sqrt{5}\). The foci therefore sit at \((\pm\sqrt{5},\, 0)\), about \((\pm 2.24,\, 0)\). Had the sign between the terms been a minus, the same reading would have named a hyperbola instead, with \(c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\).</p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <p>Those are the four curves, their standard equations, and the features ??centre, radius, focus, directrix, foci, axes, and asymptotes ??that each equation puts on display. Moving a centre or vertex off the origin only replaces \(x\) and \(y\) with \((x - h)\) and \((y - k)\); the shapes, and everything this tour named, are unchanged.</p>

  <header class="sec-head">
    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">A.4</span>Partial Fractions</h2>
  </header>

  <p>Adding two fractions over a common denominator is routine; the reverse ??breaking one complicated fraction back into simple pieces ??is <em>partial fractions</em>. A rational function is a quotient of polynomials \(P(x)/Q(x)\), and partial fractions rewrites it as a sum of fragments simple enough to handle one at a time. The smallest instance is worth seeing first:
  \[ \frac{1}{x(x + 1)} = \frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{x + 1}, \]
  as recombining the right-hand side over a common denominator confirms. (Read left to right, this is exactly the split that makes \(\sum \tfrac{1}{k(k + 1)}\) telescope ??зҰ®A.3's collapsing sum in disguise.) The main text does not yet call on this tool; it is stocked here for the integration chapters, where each fragment turns out to have a known antiderivative.</p>

  <section class="env env-proposition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proposition</span><span class="env-num">A.6</span><span class="env-name">Partial-fraction decomposition</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Let \(P(x)/Q(x)\) be a <em>proper</em> rational function ??one whose numerator has lower degree than its denominator ??and factor \(Q\) completely into linear and irreducible quadratic factors. Then \(P/Q\) equals a sum of partial fractions, contributed factor by factor:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>each distinct linear factor \((x - a)\) contributes one term \(\dfrac{A}{x - a}\);</li>
        <li>each repeated linear factor \((x - a)^{m}\) contributes \(\dfrac{A_{1}}{x - a} + \dfrac{A_{2}}{(x - a)^{2}} + \cdots + \dfrac{A_{m}}{(x - a)^{m}}\);</li>
        <li>each irreducible quadratic factor \((x^{2} + px + q)\) ??one with no real root, i.e. \(p^{2} < 4q\), so it cannot be split into real linear factors ??contributes \(\dfrac{Bx + C}{x^{2} + px + q}\); a repeated one \((x^{2} + px + q)^{m}\) contributes one such term over each power up to \(m\).</li>
      </ul>
      <p>In every case the fragment's numerator sits one degree below its denominator ??a constant over a linear factor, a linear \(Bx + C\) over a quadratic ??and a factor raised to a power gets one fragment for each power up to that exponent, exactly the freedom needed to match an arbitrary proper numerator. The constants are then uniquely determined by \(P\) and \(Q\).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <p>That such a decomposition always exists, and with only one choice of constants, is a fact of linear algebra: matching numerators forces a system of linear equations in the unknowns that has exactly one solution. We take the <em>shape</em> as given and spend the effort where the work really is ??pinning down the constants.</p>

  <section class="env env-strategy">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Strategy</span><span class="env-num">A.2</span><span class="env-name">Finding the constants</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <ol class="steps">
        <li>If the fraction is <em>improper</em> (numerator degree \(\ge\) denominator degree), divide first: \(P/Q = (\text{a polynomial}) + (\text{proper remainder})/Q\), and decompose only the remainder.</li>
        <li>Factor the denominator completely and write the template from Proposition A.6 with the constants left as unknowns.</li>
        <li>Multiply through by \(Q(x)\) to clear denominators, then solve for the constants ??either by matching coefficients of like powers of \(x\), or by substituting convenient values of \(x\) (a factor's own root kills every other term at once).</li>
      </ol>
      <p class="informal">The substitution trick in step 3 is the <em>cover-up</em> shortcut: to get the constant over a <em>distinct</em> linear factor \((x - a)\), set \(x = a\) everywhere except in that one factor. It reaches only the top constant of a repeated factor, and an irreducible quadratic has no real root to substitute at all ??those remaining constants come from matching coefficients.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">A.4</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Decompose \(\dfrac{5x - 1}{(x - 1)(x + 2)}\).</p>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Two distinct linear factors call for the template \(\dfrac{5x - 1}{(x - 1)(x + 2)} = \dfrac{A}{x - 1} + \dfrac{B}{x + 2}\). Multiplying through by \((x - 1)(x + 2)\) gives
        \[ 5x - 1 = A(x + 2) + B(x - 1). \]
        Setting \(x = 1\) leaves \(4 = 3A\), so \(A = \tfrac{4}{3}\); setting \(x = -2\) leaves \(-11 = -3B\), so \(B = \tfrac{11}{3}\). Hence
        \[ \frac{5x - 1}{(x - 1)(x + 2)} = \frac{4/3}{x - 1} + \frac{11/3}{x + 2}. \]
        Each substitution was a root of one factor, which zeroed the other term and handed back a single constant ??the cover-up shortcut in action.</p>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">A.5</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Decompose each.</p>
        <ol class="prompt-list">
          <li>\(\dfrac{x}{(x - 1)^{2}}\), with a repeated linear factor.</li>
          <li>\(\dfrac{x + 1}{x(x^{2} + 1)}\), with an irreducible quadratic factor.</li>
        </ol>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <ol class="sol-list">
          <li>The repeated factor \((x - 1)^{2}\) needs both powers: \(\dfrac{x}{(x - 1)^{2}} = \dfrac{A}{x - 1} + \dfrac{B}{(x - 1)^{2}}\). Clearing denominators, \(x = A(x - 1) + B\). Setting \(x = 1\) gives \(B = 1\); matching the coefficient of \(x\) gives \(A = 1\). So \(\dfrac{x}{(x - 1)^{2}} = \dfrac{1}{x - 1} + \dfrac{1}{(x - 1)^{2}}\).</li>
          <li>The quadratic \(x^{2} + 1\) does not factor over the reals ??there is no real root to cover up ??so it carries a linear numerator, and its constants come from matching coefficients: \(\dfrac{x + 1}{x(x^{2} + 1)} = \dfrac{A}{x} + \dfrac{Bx + C}{x^{2} + 1}\). Clearing denominators, \(x + 1 = A(x^{2} + 1) + (Bx + C)x = (A + B)x^{2} + Cx + A\). Matching powers: the constant term gives \(A = 1\), the coefficient of \(x\) gives \(C = 1\), and the coefficient of \(x^{2}\) gives \(A + B = 0\), so \(B = -1\). Hence \(\dfrac{x + 1}{x(x^{2} + 1)} = \dfrac{1}{x} + \dfrac{1 - x}{x^{2} + 1}\).</li>
        </ol>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

  <section class="env env-caution">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Caution</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Partial fractions applies only to a <em>proper</em> fraction. Faced with \(\dfrac{x^{2}}{x - 1}\), divide first: \(x^{2} = (x - 1)(x + 1) + 1\), so \(\dfrac{x^{2}}{x - 1} = x + 1 + \dfrac{1}{x - 1}\). No combination of the form \(\dfrac{A}{x - 1}\) alone can reproduce a numerator that outgrows its denominator ??the polynomial part must be divided out before the templates apply.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <p>Every fragment this method produces ??\(\dfrac{A}{x - a}\), \(\dfrac{A}{(x - a)^{k}}\), and \(\dfrac{Bx + C}{(x^{2} + px + q)^{k}}\) ??has a standard antiderivative, which is why partial fractions is the opening move for integrating a rational function. That payoff belongs to the integration chapters; the algebra it rests on is complete here.</p>

</article>

<article class="sec" lang="en">

  <header class="sec-head">
    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">A.1</span>The Reciprocal Trigonometric Functions</h2>
  </header>

  <p>The three primary trigonometric functions \(\sin\), \(\cos\), and \(\tan\) have three companions ??two honest reciprocals and one inverted ratio. They carry no new information ??every fact about them is a fact about \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) in disguise. Yet calculus speaks them fluently: the derivative of \(\tan x\) turns out to be \(\sec^{2} x\) (зҰ®3.1), the inverse secant needs its own principal range (зҰ®1.2), and integral tables are full of secants and cosecants. This section gives them an honest introduction.</p>

  <section class="env env-definition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Definition</span><span class="env-num">A.1</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>For any angle \(\theta\) at which the denominator is nonzero, the <em>secant</em>, <em>cosecant</em>, and <em>cotangent</em> functions are defined by
      \[ \sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta}, \qquad \csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}, \qquad \cot\theta = \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}. \]</p>
      <p class="informal">Informally: secant is the reciprocal of cosine, cosecant is the reciprocal of sine, and cotangent flips the ratio that defines tangent. The names cross over historically ??<em>se</em>cant with <em>co</em>sine, <em>co</em>secant with sine ??and the pairing simply has to be memorized.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <p>In a right triangle, the definitions invert the familiar ratios. Since \(\cos\theta\) is adjacent over hypotenuse, \(\sec\theta\) is hypotenuse over adjacent; since \(\sin\theta\) is opposite over hypotenuse, \(\csc\theta\) is hypotenuse over opposite; and \(\cot\theta\) is adjacent over opposite. Special-angle values follow instantly from the primary ones: for instance \(\sec\tfrac{\pi}{3} = 1/\cos\tfrac{\pi}{3} = 2\), and \(\cot\tfrac{\pi}{4} = 1\). Each function is undefined exactly where its denominator vanishes ??\(\sec\) and \(\tan\) at odd multiples of \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\), \(\csc\) and \(\cot\) at multiples of \(\pi\) ??and near those angles its values blow up, producing the vertical asymptotes on their graphs.</p>

  <section class="env env-caution">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Caution</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Cotangent is defined as \(\cos\theta/\sin\theta\), <em>not</em> as \(1/\tan\theta\). On most of the domain the two agree, but not everywhere: at \(\theta = \tfrac{\pi}{2}\) we have \(\cot\tfrac{\pi}{2} = \cos\tfrac{\pi}{2}/\sin\tfrac{\pi}{2} = 0/1 = 0\), a perfectly good value, while \(1/\tan\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) is meaningless ??\(\tan\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) is undefined. The quotient \(\cos/\sin\) is the robust definition; \(1/\tan\) is a shortcut valid only where \(\tan\theta\) is defined and nonzero.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <p>Dividing the Pythagorean identity \(\sin^{2}\theta + \cos^{2}\theta = 1\) by \(\cos^{2}\theta\) or by \(\sin^{2}\theta\) produces the two companion identities that calculus uses constantly.</p>

  <section class="env env-proposition">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proposition</span><span class="env-num">A.1</span><span class="env-name">Pythagorean companions</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>\[ \begin{aligned} 1 + \tan^{2}\theta &= \sec^{2}\theta \qquad (\cos\theta \neq 0), \\ 1 + \cot^{2}\theta &= \csc^{2}\theta \qquad (\sin\theta \neq 0). \end{aligned} \]</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="env env-proof">
    <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Proof</span></p>
    <div class="env-body">
      <p>Divide \(\sin^{2}\theta + \cos^{2}\theta = 1\) through by \(\cos^{2}\theta\):
      \[ \frac{\sin^{2}\theta}{\cos^{2}\theta} + 1 = \frac{1}{\cos^{2}\theta}, \qquad\text{that is,}\qquad \tan^{2}\theta + 1 = \sec^{2}\theta. \]
      Dividing instead by \(\sin^{2}\theta\) gives \(1 + \cot^{2}\theta = \csc^{2}\theta\). <span class="qed qed-proof"></span></p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <div class="workedexample">
    <section class="env env-example">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Example</span><span class="env-num">A.1</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <p>Evaluate the following.</p>
        <ol class="prompt-list">
          <li>\(\sec\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi}{3}\right)\) and \(\cot\!\left(-\tfrac{\pi}{4}\right)\).</li>
          <li>Given that \(\tan\theta = 3\) and \(\theta\) lies in the third quadrant, find \(\sec\theta\) and \(\csc\theta\).</li>
        </ol>
      </div>
    </section>
    <section class="env env-solution">
      <p class="env-head"><span class="env-kicker">Solution</span></p>
      <div class="env-body">
        <ol class="sol-list">
          <li>Since \(\cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\), we get \(\sec\tfrac{2\pi}{3} = 1/(-\tfrac{1}{2}) = -2\). Since \(\cos(-\tfrac{\pi}{4}) = \tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\) and \(\sin(-\tfrac{\pi}{4}) = -\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\), we get \(\cot(-\tfrac{\pi}{4}) = \frac{\sqrt{2}/2}{-\sqrt{2}/2} = -1\).</li>
          <li>By Proposition A.1, \(\sec^{2}\theta = 1 + \tan^{2}\theta = 1 + 9 = 10\), so \(\sec\theta = \pm\sqrt{10}\). In the third quadrant \(\cos\theta < 0\), hence \(\sec\theta < 0\), and therefore \(\sec\theta = -\sqrt{10}\). For the cosecant, \(\cot\theta = 1/\tan\theta = \tfrac{1}{3}\) (legitimate here, since \(\tan\theta = 3\) is defined and nonzero), so \(\csc^{2}\theta = 1 + \cot^{2}\theta = 1 + \tfrac{1}{9} = \tfrac{10}{9}\) and \(\csc\theta = \pm\tfrac{\sqrt{10}}{3}\). In the third quadrant \(\sin\theta < 0\), so \(\csc\theta = -\tfrac{\sqrt{10}}{3}\). The squaring in Proposition A.1 forgets all signs; the quadrant restores them.</li>
        </ol>
      </div>
    </section>
  </div>

857-    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">A.5</span>The Conic Sections</h2>
858-  </header>
859-
860-  <p>Slice a cone with a flat plane and the rim of the cut is a <em>conic section</em>: a circle, an ellipse, a parabola, or a hyperbola, according to the angle of the slice. Each also has a cleaner description in terms of distance, and each has a <em>standard equation</em> вҖ” the simplest form, obtained by placing the curve's centre or vertex at the origin. This tour records the four standard forms and the features to read straight off them. Like partial fractions, the conics are stocked here for later chapters; the main text of Chapters 1вҖ“4 does not call on them.</p>
861-
862-  <p><strong>The circle.</strong> A circle is the set of points a fixed distance \(r\) вҖ” the <em>radius</em> вҖ” from a centre. Centred at the origin, the condition \(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r\) squares to the standard equation
863-  \[ x^2 + y^2 = r^2. \]</p>
864-
865-  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-circle">
866-    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.1</span> A circle of radius \(r\) centred at the origin: \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\).</figcaption>
867-  </figure>
868-
869-  <p><strong>The parabola.</strong> Fix a point \(F\) (the <em>focus</em>) and a line (the <em>directrix</em>). A parabola is the set of points equidistant from the two. With focus \(F = (0, p)\) and directrix \(y = -p\), equating the two distances вҖ” \(x^2 + (y - p)^2 = (y + p)^2\) вҖ” and simplifying gives the standard equation
870-  \[ x^2 = 4py, \]
871-  a curve opening upward from its vertex at the origin. (Putting the focus on the \(x\)-axis instead gives the sideways form \(y^2 = 4px\).)</p>
872-
873-  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-parabola">
874-    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.2</span> A parabola \(x^2 = 4py\), with focus \(F = (0, p)\) and directrix \(y = -p\).</figcaption>
875-  </figure>
876-
877-  <p><strong>The ellipse.</strong> Fix two points \(F_1, F_2\) (the <em>foci</em>). An ellipse is the set of points whose distances to the two foci add to a fixed total. Placing the foci at \((\pm c, 0)\) and writing the total as \(2a\) leads to the standard equation
878-  \[ \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad b^2 = a^2 - c^2, \]
879-  with \(a > b > 0\). The curve crosses the axes at \((\pm a, 0)\) and \((0, \pm b)\); the longer semi-axis \(a\) runs along the line through the foci, and \(c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}\) locates them.</p>
880-
881-  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-ellipse">
882-    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.3</span> An ellipse \(\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1\) with foci \(F_1, F_2\) at \((\pm c, 0)\), where \(c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}\).</figcaption>
883-  </figure>
884-
885-  <p><strong>The hyperbola.</strong> Keep two foci, but now ask for a fixed <em>difference</em> of distances rather than a sum. With foci at \((\pm c, 0)\) and the difference written \(2a\), the standard equation is
886-  \[ \frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad b^2 = c^2 - a^2. \]
887-  The curve has two branches with vertices at \((\pm a, 0)\), and far from the centre it draws arbitrarily close to the two <em>asymptotes</em> \(y = \pm\frac{b}{a}x\), the crossed lines that govern its opening. Here \(c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\), so the foci lie beyond the vertices.</p>

857-    <h2 class="sec-title"><span class="sec-no">A.5</span>The Conic Sections</h2>
858-  </header>
859-
860-  <p>Slice a cone with a flat plane and the rim of the cut is a <em>conic section</em>: a circle, an ellipse, a parabola, or a hyperbola, according to the angle of the slice. Each also has a cleaner description in terms of distance, and each has a <em>standard equation</em> вҖ” the simplest form, obtained by placing the curve's centre or vertex at the origin. This tour records the four standard forms and the features to read straight off them. Like partial fractions, the conics are stocked here for later chapters; the main text of Chapters 1вҖ“4 does not call on them.</p>
861-
862-  <p><strong>The circle.</strong> A circle is the set of points a fixed distance \(r\) вҖ” the <em>radius</em> вҖ” from a centre. Centred at the origin, the condition \(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r\) squares to the standard equation
863-  \[ x^2 + y^2 = r^2. \]</p>
864-
865-  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-circle">
866-    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.1</span> A circle of radius \(r\) centred at the origin: \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\).</figcaption>
867-  </figure>
868-
869-  <p><strong>The parabola.</strong> Fix a point \(F\) (the <em>focus</em>) and a line (the <em>directrix</em>). A parabola is the set of points equidistant from the two. With focus \(F = (0, p)\) and directrix \(y = -p\), equating the two distances вҖ” \(x^2 + (y - p)^2 = (y + p)^2\) вҖ” and simplifying gives the standard equation
870-  \[ x^2 = 4py, \]
871-  a curve opening upward from its vertex at the origin. (Putting the focus on the \(x\)-axis instead gives the sideways form \(y^2 = 4px\).)</p>
872-
873-  <figure class="figure" data-fig="conic-parabola">
874-    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.2</span> A parabola \(x^2 = 4py\), with focus \(F = (0, p)\) and directrix \(y = -p\).</figcaption>
875-  </figure>
876-
877-  <p><strong>The ellipse.</strong> Fix two points \(F_1, F_2\) (the <em>foci</em>). An ellipse is the set of points whose distances to the two foci add to a fixed total. Placing the foci at \((\pm c, 0)\) and writing the total as \(2a\) leads to the standard equation
878-  \[ \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad b^2 = a^2 - c^2, \]
879-  with \(a > b > 0\). The curve crosses the axes at \((\pm a, 0)\) and \((0, \pm b)\); the longer semi-axis \(a\) runs along the line through the foci, and \(c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}\) locates them.</p>
880-

882-    <figcaption><span class="fig-no">Figure A.3</span> An ellipse \(\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1\) with foci \(F_1, F_2\) at \((\pm c, 0)\), where \(c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}\).</figcaption>
883-  </figure>
884-
885-  <p><strong>The hyperbola.</strong> Keep two foci, but now ask for a fixed <em>difference</em> of distances rather than a sum. With foci at \((\pm c, 0)\) and the difference written \(2a\), the standard equation is
886-  \[ \frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad b^2 = c^2 - a^2. \]
887-  The curve has two branches with vertices at \((\pm a, 0)\), and far from the centre it draws arbitrarily close to the two <em>asymptotes</em> \(y = \pm\frac{b}{a}x\), the crossed lines that govern its opening. Here \(c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\), so the foci lie beyond the vertices.</p>
