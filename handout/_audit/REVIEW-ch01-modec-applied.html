§1.1 Inverse Functions

1.1-2intuition
locus：Def 1.2（反函數定義）之前的動機散文

The idea is simply that the inverse trades the roles of input and output. Where \(f\) reads as a question — “given the input \(x\), what is the output?” — the inverse reads the same correspondence in reverse: “given that output, which input produced it?” This is why the domain and range change places: the outputs of \(f\) are exactly the inputs of \(f^{-1}\), and the inputs of \(f\) are its outputs.

source：authored
1.1-1caution
locus：Def 1.2 之後、Remark 1.2 之前
Caution 1.1

The superscript \(-1\) in \(f^{-1}\) denotes the inverse function, not a reciprocal. That is, \(f^{-1}(x)\) is the inverse evaluated at \(x\), whereas \(\bigl(f(x)\bigr)^{-1} = 1 / f(x)\) is the reciprocal of the value \(f(x)\); these are generally different. The same warning applies to the inverse trigonometric notation \(\sin^{-1} x\) in the next section.

source：[source: Mooculus digInInversesOfFunctions L95-101]

§1.2 Inverse Trigonometric Functions

1.2-1intuition
locus：「The inverse cosine function」子節開頭

As with sine, we first choose an interval on which the function is one-to-one and still runs through all of its values. Cosine decreases steadily from \(1\) to \(-1\) as the angle goes from \(0\) to \(\pi\), so the interval \([0, \pi]\) sweeps the entire range \([-1, 1]\) exactly once — the natural analogue of the choice \(\left[-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right]\) made for sine.

source：authored
1.2-2intuition
locus：「The inverse tangent function」子節開頭

On the open interval \(\left(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\), the tangent function climbs continuously from \(-\infty\) to \(+\infty\), taking each real number as a value exactly once. This is why the inverse tangent, unlike \(\arcsin\) and \(\arccos\), accepts every real number as input: its domain is all of \(\mathbb{R}\).

source：authored
1.2-3caution
locus：Proposition 1.4（arctan 恆等式）之後
Caution 1.4

As with the sine case, the “cancellation” identities hold only on each principal range. That is, \(\arccos(\cos x) = x\) only when \(x \in [0, \pi]\), and \(\arctan(\tan x) = x\) only when \(x \in \left(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\). Outside these intervals the left-hand side returns the equivalent angle that does lie in the principal range; for instance, \[ \arccos(\cos 2\pi) = \arccos(1) = 0 \neq 2\pi, \qquad \arctan(\tan \pi) = \arctan(0) = 0 \neq \pi. \]

§1.3 The Limit of a Function

1.3-1caution
locus：Example 1.18（表格猜極限）之後
Caution 1.5

A table of values can only ever suggest a limit; it can never confirm one, and it can actively mislead. Consider \(f(x) = \sin(\pi / x)\) as \(x \to 0\). Sampling \(x = 0.1, 0.01, 0.001, \dots\) gives \(\sin(10\pi), \sin(100\pi), \dots = 0\) every time, suggesting the limit is \(0\). But sampling \(x = 0.3, 0.03, 0.003, \dots\) gives \(\sin\!\left(\tfrac{10\pi}{3}\right), \sin\!\left(\tfrac{100\pi}{3}\right), \dots = -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) every time, suggesting \(-\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\). In truth \(f\) runs through every value in \([-1, 1]\) infinitely often as \(x \to 0\), so the limit does not exist. The definition requires \(f(x)\) to be close to a single \(L\) for all \(x\) sufficiently near \(a\) — not merely for a convenient list of samples.

source：[source: Mooculus digInWhatIsALimit, sin(π/x)]
1.3-2application
locus：節末收尾段之前（閉合開場的速度線）

We can now close the loop opened at the start of the section. Suppose a ball thrown upward has height \(s(t) = 40t - 16t^{2}\) feet at time \(t\) seconds. Its average velocity over the interval from \(t\) to \(2\) is \[ \frac{s(2) - s(t)}{2 - t} = \frac{16 - (40t - 16t^{2})}{2 - t} = \frac{8(2t - 1)(t - 2)}{2 - t} = 8 - 16t, \qquad t \neq 2. \] The instantaneous velocity at \(t = 2\) is the value this average approaches as the interval shrinks to nothing: \[ v(2) = \lim_{t \to 2} \frac{s(2) - s(t)}{2 - t} = \lim_{t \to 2} (8 - 16t) = -24 \text{ ft/s}. \] Notice that the original ratio has the indeterminate form \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) at \(t = 2\) — both \(s(2) - s(t)\) and \(2 - t\) tend to \(0\) — so the cancellation that produced \(8 - 16t\) was what made the limit accessible.

§1.4 One-Sided and Infinite Limits

1.4-2caution
locus：Definition 1.11（infinite limit）之後
Caution 1.6

Writing \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty\) does not mean that the limit exists in the ordinary sense. It is shorthand recording a particular way the limit fails to exist: the values of \(f\) grow without bound. The symbol \(\infty\) is not a real number, and it cannot be added, multiplied, or otherwise manipulated like one. Reading “\(= \infty\)” as though it were an ordinary limit value is a common slip.

source：[source: Mooculus digInLimitsOfTheFormNonZeroOverZero]
1.4-4strategy
locus：Definition 1.12 之後、Example 1.23 之前
Strategy 1.2 — Locating vertical asymptotes
  1. Find the candidate points: the places not in the domain that can still be approached from within it. For a rational function, these are the zeros of the denominator.
  2. At each candidate \(x = a\), examine the one-sided limits. When the expression has the form \(\frac{\text{nonzero}}{0}\), determine the sign of the small denominator on each side to decide between \(+\infty\) and \(-\infty\).
  3. If at least one one-sided limit is \(+\infty\) or \(-\infty\), the line \(x = a\) is a vertical asymptote. If instead the denominator's zero is cancelled by the numerator and no infinity appears, there is no asymptote there.
source：[source: Mooculus digInVerticalAsymptotes]

§1.5 Limit Laws and Computational Techniques

1.5-1intuition
locus：Theorem 1.2（limit laws）之前

Each of these laws says something we already expect. If \(f(x)\) is settling toward \(L\) and \(g(x)\) toward \(M\) as \(x\) nears \(a\), then \(f(x) + g(x)\) ought to settle toward \(L + M\), the product \(f(x)\,g(x)\) toward \(LM\), and so on. In short, the laws assert that combining the functions first and then taking the limit gives the same result as taking the limits first and then combining.

source：authored
1.5-2caution
locus：商定律「In words…」段之後
Caution 1.7

The quotient law applies only when the denominator's limit is nonzero. If \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = 0\), the law says nothing, and you may not conclude that the quotient's limit fails to exist or that it takes any particular value. A resulting \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) is a signal to do more work — usually algebraic simplification, as in the examples below — not a verdict about the limit.

§1.6 The Precise (ε–δ) Definition of a Limit

1.6-2application
locus：開場段之後、Definition 1.13 之前

It helps to read \(\varepsilon\) and \(\delta\) as tolerances. In engineering and manufacturing one specifies how far a finished quantity may stray from its target: an output tolerance \(\varepsilon\) around the desired value \(L\). One then asks how tightly the input must be controlled near \(a\) to keep the result within that tolerance — an input tolerance \(\delta\). The definition below is the mathematical backbone of the everyday promise “control the input closely enough, and the output stays within specification.”

source：[source: APEX §1.2, x-tolerance = δ, y-tolerance = ε]
advisory 已修：原為單一長句含兩段破折號插語，拆為兩句（output tolerance 一句、input tolerance 一句）。
1.6-1intuition
locus：Figure 1.21 後那段之後、Example 1.38 之前

It is useful to picture the definition as a challenge-and-response game. A skeptic names a tolerance \(\varepsilon > 0\) — how close to \(L\) they insist \(f(x)\) must be — and our job is to respond with a \(\delta > 0\) that meets the demand: every \(x\) within \(\delta\) of \(a\) (but not equal to \(a\)) is sent to within \(\varepsilon\) of \(L\). The limit equals \(L\) precisely when we can answer every challenge, no matter how small the \(\varepsilon\). This is why \(\varepsilon\) comes first and \(\delta\) second: \(\delta\) is chosen in reply to \(\varepsilon\).
