2.5The Product and Quotient Rules

In §2.4 we learned that differentiation behaves beautifully with respect to addition: the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives, and the same holds for differences, \((f \pm g)' = f' \pm g'\) (Theorem 2.4). It is tempting to hope that products and quotients are just as docile — that the derivative of a product is the product of the derivatives. This section shows that they are not, finds the correct rules, and so completes the toolkit of basic differentiation rules begun in §2.4. We treat the two cases in turn: first \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(fg)\), then \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)\).

The product rule

The cleanest guess one could make is that derivatives pass through a product untouched, \((fg)' = f'g'\). A single example shows this is false.

Caution

In general \((fg)' \ne f'g'\). Take \(f(x) = x\) and \(g(x) = x^{2}\), so that \(fg = x^{3}\). Then \((fg)' = 3x^{2}\), whereas

\[ f'(x)\,g'(x) = (1)(2x) = 2x \ne 3x^{2}. \]

Differentiating a product is not a term-by-term operation. This is the single most common error with the rules of this chapter; the correct rule is below.

To see where the correct rule comes from, picture \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) as the two side lengths of a rectangle, so that the product \(f(x)\,g(x)\) is its area. Increase \(x\) by a small amount \(h\): the width grows by \(\Delta f = f(x+h) - f(x)\) and the height by \(\Delta g = g(x+h) - g(x)\). The extra area is an L-shaped border made of three pieces (Figure 2.4): a strip \(g\,\Delta f\) along one side, a strip \(f\,\Delta g\) along the other, and a small corner \(\Delta f\,\Delta g\).

Dividing the added area by \(h\) and letting \(h \to 0\), the two strips contribute \(g\,\dfrac{\Delta f}{h} \to g\,f'\) and \(f\,\dfrac{\Delta g}{h} \to f\,g'\), while the corner contributes \(\dfrac{\Delta f\,\Delta g}{h} = \dfrac{\Delta f}{h}\,\Delta g \to f' \cdot 0 = 0\), since \(\Delta f/h\) stays finite (tending to \(f'\)) while \(\Delta g \to 0\). The surviving pieces are exactly the two terms of the product rule.

Theorem2.6The product rule

If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable at \(x\), then their product \(fg\) is differentiable at \(x\), and

\[ (fg)'(x) = f'(x)\,g(x) + f(x)\,g'(x), \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \frac{d}{dx}(fg) = \left(\frac{d}{dx}f\right)g + f\left(\frac{d}{dx}g\right). \]

Proof

We start from the definition and apply a useful trick: add and subtract \(f(x)\,g(x+h)\) in the numerator, which lets us isolate the change in \(f\) from the change in \(g\):

\[ \begin{aligned} f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x) &= \bigl[f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x+h)\bigr] + \bigl[f(x)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)\bigr] \\[2pt] &= \bigl[f(x+h) - f(x)\bigr]\,g(x+h) + f(x)\,\bigl[g(x+h) - g(x)\bigr]. \end{aligned} \]

Dividing by \(h\),

\[ \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h} = \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\,g(x+h) \;+\; f(x)\,\frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h}. \]

Now let \(h \to 0\) and evaluate each piece with the limit laws of §1.5. The two difference quotients tend to \(f'(x)\) and \(g'(x)\). For the remaining factor, \(g(x+h) \to g(x)\): this is exactly the statement that \(g\) is continuous at \(x\), which holds because \(g\), being differentiable at \(x\), is continuous there (Theorem 2.1, §2.3). Therefore

\[ (fg)'(x) = f'(x)\,g(x) + f(x)\,g'(x). \]

The rule is quick to apply once it is in hand. For instance,

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(x^{2} e^{x}\bigr) = (2x)\,e^{x} + x^{2}\,e^{x} = x(x + 2)\,e^{x}, \]

where we used the power rule (Theorem 2.3) for \(x^{2}\) and \((e^{x})' = e^{x}\) (Theorem 2.5) for the exponential factor. The manuscript records the following companion example.

Example2.19

Differentiate \(h(x) = x\,e^{x}\).

Solution

Here \(h\) is a product of \(f(x) = x\) and \(g(x) = e^{x}\), with \(f'(x) = 1\) and \(g'(x) = e^{x}\) (Theorem 2.5). The product rule gives

\[ h'(x) = f'(x)\,g(x) + f(x)\,g'(x) = (1)\,e^{x} + x\,e^{x} = e^{x}(1 + x). \]

Notice that neither factor alone produces the answer: the term \(e^{x}\) comes from differentiating \(x\) while keeping \(e^{x}\), and the term \(x\,e^{x}\) from keeping \(x\) while differentiating \(e^{x}\) — the two strips of Figure 2.4 in algebraic form.

The product rule has a clean reading in economics. Suppose a firm sells \(q\) units at a price \(p(q)\) that depends on how many are sold; its revenue is the product \(R(q) = p(q)\,q\). The product rule gives the marginal revenue \(R'(q) = p'(q)\,q + p(q)\): selling one more unit changes revenue in two ways at once — through the price change \(p'(q)\) spread over all \(q\) units, and through the price \(p(q)\) fetched by the extra unit itself. The two strips of Figure 2.4 return here as two competing economic effects.

The quotient rule

The same add-and-subtract idea handles a quotient \(f/g\). Here the manuscript writes the increment as \(\Delta x\) rather than \(h\); the two symbols mean the same thing, as the next caution records.

Caution

The increment in the variable is written either \(h\) or \(\Delta x\); in derivative computations the two are synonymous. Reading \(\Delta x\) as “a small change in \(x\),” the difference quotient \(\bigl(f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)\bigr)/\Delta x\) is identical to the \(h\)-form, and \(\Delta x \to 0\) means the same as \(h \to 0\). We switch to \(\Delta x\) below only to match the manuscript.

Theorem2.7The quotient rule

If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable at \(x\) and \(g(x) \ne 0\), then the quotient \(f/g\) is differentiable at \(x\), and

\[ \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(x) = \frac{f'(x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g'(x)}{\bigl[g(x)\bigr]^{2}}. \]

Before the proof, it helps to see why the rule has this shape. Like the product rule, its numerator pairs each function with the derivative of the other — but joined by a minus sign instead of a plus, and the whole expression is divided by \(g^{2}\). The minus sign reflects that \(g\) sits in the denominator: making \(g\) larger makes the quotient \(f/g\) smaller, so the effect of \(g'\) is subtracted rather than added. The \(g^{2}\) is the footprint of differentiating \(1/g\): already in the simplest case \(f = 1\), the derivative of \(1/x\) was \(-1/x^{2}\) in §2.2 — the same minus sign and the same square that the quotient rule carries in general.

Proof

Combining the two fractions over a common denominator,

\[ \frac{f(x+\Delta x)}{g(x+\Delta x)} - \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f(x+\Delta x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g(x+\Delta x)}{g(x+\Delta x)\,g(x)}. \]

On the numerator we play the same trick as before, now adding and subtracting \(f(x)\,g(x)\):

\[ \begin{aligned} f(x+\Delta x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g(x+\Delta x) &= \bigl[f(x+\Delta x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g(x)\bigr] - \bigl[f(x)\,g(x+\Delta x) - f(x)\,g(x)\bigr] \\[2pt] &= \bigl[f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)\bigr]\,g(x) - f(x)\,\bigl[g(x+\Delta x) - g(x)\bigr]. \end{aligned} \]

Dividing the whole difference by \(\Delta x\), the difference quotient becomes

\[ \frac{1}{g(x+\Delta x)\,g(x)}\left( \frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}\,g(x) - f(x)\,\frac{g(x+\Delta x) - g(x)}{\Delta x} \right). \]

Let \(\Delta x \to 0\). The two difference quotients tend to \(f'(x)\) and \(g'(x)\); and \(g(x+\Delta x) \to g(x)\) because \(g\) is continuous at \(x\) (Theorem 2.1, §2.3), so the denominator tends to \(g(x)\,g(x) = \bigl[g(x)\bigr]^{2}\), which is nonzero since \(g(x) \ne 0\). Hence

\[ \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(x) = \frac{f'(x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g'(x)}{\bigl[g(x)\bigr]^{2}}. \]

Caution

Unlike the product rule, the quotient rule is not symmetric in \(f\) and \(g\): the numerator is \(f'g - f g'\), with the derivative of the top coming first. Swapping the order to \(f g' - f' g\) flips the sign of the whole derivative, and forgetting the \(g^{2}\) in the denominator changes the answer entirely. A reliable mnemonic reads the numerator as “(derivative of top)(bottom) minus (top)(derivative of bottom), all over bottom squared.”

As a first check, the quotient rule recovers a derivative we computed laboriously from first principles in §2.2. Taking the numerator to be the constant \(1\) and the denominator \(x\),

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = \frac{(0)(x) - (1)(1)}{x^{2}} = -\frac{1}{x^{2}}, \]

matching the derivative \(-1/x^{2}\) found in Example 2.8 — obtained here in a single line, exactly as promised there.

Example2.20

Differentiate \(f(x) = \dfrac{x}{x^{2} + 1}\).

Solution

This is a genuine quotient — no algebra simplifies it to a power — so the quotient rule applies, with top \(x\) and bottom \(x^{2} + 1\). Their derivatives are \(1\) and \(2x\), and the bottom never vanishes, so the rule is valid for every \(x\). Taking care with the order of the numerator terms,

\[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= \frac{(1)\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr) - (x)(2x)}{\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr)^{2}} \\[2pt] &= \frac{x^{2} + 1 - 2x^{2}}{\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr)^{2}} = \frac{1 - x^{2}}{\bigl(x^{2} + 1\bigr)^{2}}. \end{aligned} \]

Had we mistakenly written the numerator as \((x)(2x) - (1)(x^{2}+1)\), every sign would be wrong. As a sanity check on the result, \(f'(0) = 1 > 0\) (the curve rises through the origin) and \(f'(\pm 1) = 0\), the two points where \(x/(x^{2}+1)\) reaches its largest and smallest values.

Example2.21

A student differentiates \(f(x) = \dfrac{2x}{x + 1}\) as follows:

\[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= \frac{2(x + 1) + 2x}{(x + 1)^{2}} \\[4pt] &= \frac{2(x + 1)}{(x + 1)^{2}} = \frac{2}{x + 1}. \end{aligned} \]

Spot all the errors in this work, and give the correct derivative.

Solution

There are two errors.

Error 1 — wrong sign in the quotient rule. The quotient rule carries a minus sign in the numerator,

\[ \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^{2}}, \]

but the student wrote a plus. With top \(2x\) and bottom \(x + 1\), the correct numerator is

\[ (2)(x + 1) - (2x)(1) = 2x + 2 - 2x = 2, \]

not \(2(x + 1) + 2x\).

Error 2 — incorrect simplification. Even within the student's own (wrong) numerator, \(2(x + 1) + 2x = 4x + 2\), not \(2(x + 1)\); the \(2x\) term was simply dropped.

Carrying out the rule correctly,

\[ f'(x) = \frac{(2)(x + 1) - (2x)(1)}{(x + 1)^{2}} = \frac{2}{(x + 1)^{2}}. \]

Note that the denominator is \((x + 1)^{2}\), not \((x + 1)\) — a third common slip the student was heading toward.

Choosing the right rule

With the constant, power, sum, constant-multiple, product, and quotient rules all in hand, differentiating a given expression is now less about computing limits and more about recognizing which rule its structure calls for. Reading that structure — and simplifying first when possible — is the skill the next box organizes.

Strategy2.2Selecting among the basic rules

  1. Read the outermost structure of the expression. Is it a single power or polynomial, a sum or difference, a product of two functions, or a quotient of two functions?
  2. Before reaching for the product or quotient rule, ask whether algebra simplifies the expression first: a quotient like \(\dfrac{x^{4} - x}{x}\) becomes the polynomial \(x^{3} - 1\), and a product like \(x^{2}\cdot x^{3}\) becomes \(x^{5}\). A rule you can avoid is a rule you cannot misapply.
  3. Apply the matching rule: the power, sum, and constant-multiple rules for polynomials and powers; the product rule (Theorem 2.6) for a genuine product; the quotient rule (Theorem 2.7) for a genuine quotient. For compound expressions, the rules combine — a sum of products, a product with a polynomial factor, and so on.

The point is to spend a moment on the shape of the problem before computing: the same expression can be far easier under one rule than another, and some apparent quotients are not quotients at all once simplified.

Caution

Not every product or quotient on the page calls for the product or quotient rule. A constant factor is handled by the constant-multiple rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(5x^{3}) = 5 \cdot 3x^{2} = 15x^{2}\), with no need to "differentiate" the \(5\). A constant denominator is likewise just a constant multiple, since \(\dfrac{x^{2}}{3} = \tfrac{1}{3}x^{2}\) gives \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\dfrac{x^{2}}{3} = \tfrac{2}{3}x\). The product and quotient rules are needed only when both parts genuinely depend on \(x\); using them when one part is constant still reaches the answer, but by a longer route with more chances to slip.

Example2.22

For each function, first decide which rule applies, then differentiate: \[ \text{(a)}\ \ y = x^{3} e^{x}, \qquad \text{(b)}\ \ y = \frac{x^{4} - x}{x}, \qquad \text{(c)}\ \ y = \frac{x^{2}}{e^{x}}. \]

Solution

(a) A genuine product of \(x^{3}\) and \(e^{x}\); use the product rule:

\[ y' = (3x^{2})\,e^{x} + x^{3}\,e^{x} = x^{2} e^{x}(3 + x). \]

(b) This looks like a quotient, but it simplifies first — for \(x \ne 0\), \(\dfrac{x^{4} - x}{x} = x^{3} - 1\) — so the power rule finishes it with no quotient rule at all:

\[ y' = 3x^{2}. \]

(c) A genuine quotient that does not simplify; use the quotient rule, with top \(x^{2}\) and bottom \(e^{x}\):

\[ y' = \frac{(2x)\,e^{x} - x^{2}\,e^{x}}{\bigl(e^{x}\bigr)^{2}} = \frac{e^{x}\bigl(2x - x^{2}\bigr)}{e^{2x}} = \frac{2x - x^{2}}{e^{x}}. \]

The three parts share no single rule; the work was choosing correctly, and in (b) recognizing that the cleanest move was not to use the quotient rule at all.

Example2.23

Differentiate \(f(x) = \dfrac{e^{x}}{2x}\).

Solution

The quotient rule with top \(e^{x}\) and bottom \(2x\) gives

\[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= \frac{(e^{x})(2x) - (e^{x})(2)}{(2x)^{2}} \\[2pt] &= \frac{2e^{x}(x - 1)}{4x^{2}} = \frac{(x - 1)\,e^{x}}{2x^{2}}. \end{aligned} \]

The derivative of the top is again \(e^{x}\) (Theorem 2.5), and the derivative of the bottom is just \(2\). The sign of \(f'(x)\) changes at \(x = 1\): for \(0 < x < 1\) we have \(f'(x) < 0\) (the function is decreasing), while for \(x > 1\) we have \(f'(x) > 0\) (the function is increasing).

The product and quotient rules complete the basic differentiation toolkit of this chapter: together with the constant, power, sum, and constant-multiple rules and the derivative of \(e^{x}\), they let us differentiate any algebraic or exponential combination built by adding, scaling, multiplying, and dividing. One important way of combining functions remains untouched — composition, forming \(f\bigl(g(x)\bigr)\) — and it needs a rule of its own, the chain rule, which opens the next chapter.
