2.2The Derivative as a Function

Imagine a car moving along a straight road, its position at time \(t\) recorded as \(s(t)\). Over the short interval from \(t\) to \(t + h\), the car's average velocity is the change in position divided by the elapsed time, \(\bigl(s(t+h) - s(t)\bigr)/h\) — exactly the difference quotient of §2.1. Letting \(h \to 0\) gives the instantaneous velocity at the single instant \(t\). But we rarely care about one instant in isolation; we want to know the velocity at every instant — that is, we want velocity as a function of time. Making this shift, from the derivative at one point to the derivative as a function, is the work of this section.

From a number to a function

In §2.1 we fixed a point \(a\) and computed the derivative \(f'(a)\), a single number recording the slope of the tangent line — equivalently, the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) — at that one point. To find the slope at a different point we would have to run the entire limit again, and to know the slope at every point we would have to repeat the computation infinitely many times. Worse, a table of separate values \(f'(2),\, f'(3),\, f'(\tfrac{7}{2}),\, \ldots\) never reveals the underlying pattern.

There is a far better way. Instead of substituting a specific number for the point, we leave it symbolic — call it \(x\) — and carry out the limit just once. The outcome is no longer a single number but a formula in \(x\): a brand-new function that returns the slope at every point at once. This function is called the derivative of \(f\).

Definition2.2The derivative as a function

Let \(f\) be a function. The derivative of \(f\) is the function \(f'\) defined by \[ f'(x) \;=\; \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}, \] whose domain is the set of all \(x\) for which this limit exists. The value \(f'(x)\) is the slope of the tangent line to \(y = f(x)\) at \(\bigl(x,\, f(x)\bigr)\) — equivalently, the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) at \(x\).

Informally, \(f'\) is the “slope machine” of \(f\): feed it any input \(x\), and it returns the slope of \(f\) there.

This is the same limit we met in §2.1: the substitution \(x = a + h\) turns it into \(\lim_{x \to a}\bigl(f(x) - f(a)\bigr)/(x - a)\) with the fixed point renamed. The \(h\)-form above is usually the more convenient one to compute with, and the recipe is the same one we used at a single point — only now the point stays symbolic.

Strategy2.1Computing a derivative from the definition

  1. Form the difference quotient \(\dfrac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}\), expanding \(f(x + h)\).
  2. Simplify the quotient algebraically — by expanding, rationalising, or combining fractions — until the \(h\) that makes the denominator vanish cancels.
  3. Evaluate the limit as \(h \to 0\) in the simplified expression.

These are the same three steps we used at a fixed point in §2.1; the only change is that \(x\) now stays symbolic, so the answer emerges as a function rather than a number. The algebra in step 2 takes a different form for each function — expansion, conjugates, common denominators — but its job is always to clear the \(0/0\); for the polynomial, root, and rational functions of this chapter a factor of \(h\) always cancels and leaves a limit we can evaluate.

Example2.6

Find the derivative of \(f(x) = x^{2} + 4x - 2\).

Solution

Following the three steps,

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} &= \frac{\bigl[(x + h)^{2} + 4(x + h) - 2\bigr] - \bigl[x^{2} + 4x - 2\bigr]}{h} \\[2pt] &= \frac{\bigl(x^{2} + 2xh + h^{2} + 4x + 4h - 2\bigr) - \bigl(x^{2} + 4x - 2\bigr)}{h} \\[2pt] &= \frac{2xh + h^{2} + 4h}{h} \;=\; 2x + h + 4. \end{aligned} \]

Hence \[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h + 4) = 2x + 4. \] Nothing in this computation relied on \(x\) being any particular number, so the single formula \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\) delivers the slope at every point: for instance \(f'(0) = 4\), while \(f'(-2) = 0\), signalling a horizontal tangent at \(x = -2\).

Example2.7

Find the derivative of \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\), \(x > 0\).

Solution

The difference quotient contains a difference of square roots, which we clear by multiplying by the conjugate \(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} &= \frac{\sqrt{x + h} - \sqrt{x}}{h} = \frac{\bigl(\sqrt{x + h} - \sqrt{x}\bigr)\bigl(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}\bigr)}{h\bigl(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}\bigr)} \\[4pt] &= \frac{(x + h) - x}{h\bigl(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}\bigr)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}}. \end{aligned} \]

The denominator no longer vanishes as \(h \to 0\), so \[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}. \] As a check, at \(x = 4\) this gives \(f'(4) = 1/(2 \cdot 2) = \tfrac{1}{4}\) — precisely the slope we found for \(\sqrt{x}\) at \((4, 2)\) in §2.1 (Remark 2.2). The function \(f'(x) = 1/(2\sqrt{x})\) packages that slope, and the slope at every other positive \(x\), into one formula.

Example2.8

Find the derivative of \(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}\), \(x \ne 0\).

Solution

This time the difference quotient is a difference of fractions, which we combine over a common denominator:

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} &= \frac{1}{h}\left( \frac{1}{x + h} - \frac{1}{x} \right) = \frac{1}{h} \cdot \frac{x - (x + h)}{x(x + h)} \\[4pt] &= \frac{1}{h} \cdot \frac{-h}{x(x + h)} = \frac{-1}{x(x + h)}. \end{aligned} \]

Letting \(h \to 0\), \[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{-1}{x(x + h)} = -\frac{1}{x^{2}}. \] The manipulation is new — clear the fractions, then cancel the \(h\) — yet the three-step pattern is unchanged, and the result \(f'(x) = -1/x^{2}\) is once more a function, defined for all \(x \ne 0\). We will recover it in one line from the power and quotient rules of §2.4–§2.5.

Remark2.3The domain of \(f'\)

Being a function in its own right, \(f'\) has its own domain, which may be smaller than that of \(f\). Although Example 2.7 worked with \(x > 0\), the square-root function is defined for all \(x \ge 0\), whereas its derivative \(f'(x) = 1/(2\sqrt{x})\) is defined only for \(x > 0\) — so the domain of \(f'\) can be a proper subset of the domain of \(f\). Precisely when a derivative fails to exist is taken up in §2.3.

Example2.9

For \(f(x) = x^{2} + 4x - 2\), find the point on the curve at which the tangent line has slope \(2\).

Solution

The slope of the tangent at \(x\) is the value of the derivative there, and from Example 2.6 we already have the derivative function \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\). We therefore want the input \(x\) at which

\[ f'(x) = 2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 2x + 4 = 2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x = -1. \]

The point on the curve is \(\bigl(-1,\, f(-1)\bigr) = (-1,\, -5)\). Note the change of viewpoint: we did not compute a derivative here — we already had \(f'\) as a function and solved it for the input producing a prescribed slope. Being able to run \(f'\) forward (input \(\to\) slope) and backward (slope \(\to\) input) is precisely what having the derivative as a function buys us.

Notation

So far we have written the derivative as \(f'(x)\), the compact prime notation. When \(y = f(x)\), the same derivative is also written

\[ f'(x) \;=\; y' \;=\; \frac{dy}{dx} \;=\; \frac{df}{dx} \;=\; \frac{d}{dx}\,f(x), \]

the last three being Leibniz notation. Here \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\) is an operator — it acts on a function and returns its derivative — and the symbol deliberately echoes the difference quotient \(\Delta y / \Delta x\), which becomes \(dy/dx\) as the increment \(\Delta x\) (our \(h\)) shrinks to zero. Leibniz notation has the merit of displaying the variable we differentiate with respect to, which becomes valuable once a problem involves more than one variable; prime notation is lighter when the variable is understood. We will use whichever is clearer, and we state the rules of the coming sections most often with \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\). Thus the two results above may equally be written \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^{2} + 4x - 2) = 2x + 4\) and \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\sqrt{x} = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\).

Reading \(f'\) from the graph of \(f\)

Drawing a derivative as a function in its own right exposes a vivid link between the graphs of \(f\) and \(f'\). Figure 2.2 places the parabola \(f(x) = x^{2} + 4x - 2\) of Example 2.6 directly above its derivative \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\). Since \(f'(x)\) is by definition the slope of the tangent line at \(x\), we can read its sign straight off the graph of \(f\):

  • where the tangent line slopes upward, \(f' > 0\);
  • where the tangent line slopes downward, \(f' < 0\);
  • where the tangent line is horizontal, \(f' = 0\) — as at the lowest point of the parabola, \(x = -2\).

The link runs the other way too: where \(f' > 0\) the graph of \(f\) rises, and where \(f' < 0\) it falls. So we can sketch the shape of \(f'\) straight from the graph of \(f\), with no formula at all.

Example2.10

Cover the lower panel of Figure 2.2 and look only at the graph of \(f\) on top. Using its shape alone — not the formula — say where \(f'\) is positive, where it is negative, and where it is zero. Then uncover the lower panel and check.

Solution

Reading the top graph: \(f\) falls until \(x = -2\), so its tangents slope downward and \(f' < 0\) there; the curve is momentarily flat at its lowest point \(x = -2\), so \(f'(-2) = 0\); and \(f\) rises for \(x > -2\), so \(f' > 0\). Uncovering the lower panel confirms it exactly — the line \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\) lies below the axis for \(x < -2\), crosses zero at \(x = -2\), and lies above it for \(x > -2\). We recovered the sign of \(f'\) everywhere from the shape of \(f\) alone.

Remark2.4Derivative as rate of change

Return to the moving car of the opening. If \(s(t)\) is its position, the derivative function \(s'(t)\) is its velocity \(v(t)\) at every time \(t\). The slope reading and the rate-of-change reading are two faces of the same limit: \(f'(x)\) measures how fast the output of \(f\) changes per unit change in the input — whether that output is read as a height (slope) or a position (velocity). This is why the derivative function is one of the central objects of calculus: it turns any varying quantity into a record of how fast it varies.

Throughout this section we assumed the defining limit exists. It need not: at some points a function may have no well-defined slope at all. In the next section we ask precisely when \(f'\) exists — the question of differentiability — uncover its close tie to continuity, and form the derivative of a derivative.
