2.3Differentiability, Continuity, and Higher Derivatives

Throughout §2.2 we freely computed \(f'(x)\) by taking the limit of the difference quotient — but we never stopped to ask whether that limit exists. For the polynomial, root, and rational functions we worked with, it always did, and we might be tempted to believe that every function has a derivative at every point of its domain. This section shows that the temptation is wrong: at certain points the limit can fail, and recognizing those failures is an essential part of understanding what the derivative means. Along the way we prove the first theorem of the chapter — differentiability guarantees continuity — and extend the derivative idea to second, third, and higher orders.

When does the derivative exist?

Definition2.3Differentiable

A function \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\) if the limit \[ f'(a) \;=\; \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} \] exists (as a finite number). We say \(f\) is differentiable on an open interval \((a, b)\) if it is differentiable at every point of that interval.

Informally, differentiability at a point means the graph has a single, well-defined tangent direction there — no sharp corner, no vertical spike, and no break.

The definition asks exactly what we have been computing: can the difference quotient settle on a single value as \(h \to 0\)? When it can, the function is differentiable and the derivative equals that value. When it cannot — because the left-hand and right-hand limits disagree, or because the quotient blows up — the function is not differentiable at that point. The simplest failure mode is a corner.

Example2.11A corner: \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) at \(x = 0\)

Show that \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) is not differentiable at \(x = 0\).

Solution

We form the difference quotient at \(a = 0\):

\[ \frac{f(0 + h) - f(0)}{h} \;=\; \frac{\lvert h \rvert - 0}{h} \;=\; \frac{\lvert h \rvert}{h}. \]

Now examine the one-sided limits separately. For \(h > 0\) we have \(\lvert h \rvert = h\), so

\[ \lim_{h \to 0^{+}} \frac{\lvert h \rvert}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^{+}} \frac{h}{h} = 1. \]

For \(h < 0\) we have \(\lvert h \rvert = -h\), so

\[ \lim_{h \to 0^{-}} \frac{\lvert h \rvert}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^{-}} \frac{-h}{h} = -1. \]

Since the right-hand limit (\(1\)) and the left-hand limit (\(-1\)) are not equal, the two-sided limit \(\lim_{h \to 0} \lvert h \rvert / h\) does not exist. By Definition 2.3, \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) is not differentiable at \(x = 0\).

Geometrically, the graph of \(\lvert x \rvert\) forms a V-shape at the origin (Figure 2.3). Approaching from the right, the secant lines all have slope \(+1\); approaching from the left, they all have slope \(-1\). The two families converge to different directions, so there is no single tangent line — exactly the geometric meaning of non-differentiability.

Example2.12A vertical tangent: \(f(x) = \sqrt[3]{x}\) at \(x = 0\)

Show that \(f(x) = \sqrt[3]{x} = x^{1/3}\) is not differentiable at \(x = 0\), even though its graph has no corner there.

Solution

We form the difference quotient at \(a = 0\):

\[ \frac{f(0 + h) - f(0)}{h} \;=\; \frac{h^{1/3} - 0}{h} \;=\; \frac{1}{h^{2/3}}. \]

Since \(h^{2/3} > 0\) for all \(h \ne 0\), the quotient is always positive. As \(h \to 0\) the denominator \(h^{2/3} \to 0\), so

\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h^{2/3}} = +\infty. \]

The limit does not exist as a finite number, so by Definition 2.3 the function is not differentiable at \(x = 0\).

Notice the contrast with Example 2.11. There the two one-sided limits existed but disagreed (\(+1\) versus \(-1\)); here both one-sided limits agree — they both tend to \(+\infty\) — so there is no corner. Instead the graph of \(\sqrt[3]{x}\) has a vertical tangent at the origin: the secant lines all steepen without bound as \(h \to 0\). A vertical line has no finite slope, and the derivative demands a finite limit, so differentiability fails here for a different reason than at a corner.

Differentiability and continuity

We now ask: what does differentiability give us beyond just having a tangent line? The answer is a clean logical relationship with continuity.

Theorem2.1Differentiable implies continuous

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\), then \(f\) is continuous at \(a\).

Proof

We must show \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\), which is equivalent to showing \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \bigl[f(x) - f(a)\bigr] = 0\).

For \(x \ne a\), write

\[ f(x) - f(a) \;=\; \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \;\cdot\; (x - a). \]

As \(x \to a\), both factors on the right have limits that exist:

  • \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} = f'(a)\) (by hypothesis, since \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\));
  • \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} (x - a) = 0\).

By the product rule for limits,

\[ \lim_{x \to a} \bigl[f(x) - f(a)\bigr] = f'(a) \cdot 0 = 0. \]

Hence \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\), so \(f\) is continuous at \(a\).

The proof is short, but the picture behind it is worth keeping. To be differentiable at \(a\) is to have one definite tangent direction there, and a curve smooth enough to settle on a single tangent has no room to jump — so differentiability forces continuity. The converse fails because continuity asks far less: it only forbids breaks, and an unbroken curve is still free to have a sharp corner, where no single tangent direction exists.

Caution

The converse of Theorem 2.1 is false: continuity does not imply differentiability. The function \(f(x) = \lvert x \rvert\) is continuous at \(x = 0\) (the left and right limits both equal \(f(0) = 0\)), yet Example 2.11 shows it is not differentiable there. Differentiability is a strictly stronger condition than continuity.

Example2.13

Find the values of the constants \(a\) and \(b\) for which the function \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} x^{2} & x \le 2, \\ ax + b & x > 2 \end{cases} \] is differentiable everywhere.

Solution

Away from \(x = 2\) each piece is a polynomial and therefore differentiable, so the only point in question is \(x = 2\).

Step 1 — Continuity. By Theorem 2.1, differentiability at \(x = 2\) requires continuity there, which forces the two pieces to meet:

\[ \lim_{x \to 2^{-}} f(x) = 2^{2} = 4 \quad\text{and}\quad \lim_{x \to 2^{+}} f(x) = 2a + b, \]

so we need \(2a + b = 4\).

Step 2 — Matching derivatives. The left-hand derivative at \(x = 2\) uses the \(x^{2}\) branch, whose derivative \(2x\) follows in one line from the difference quotient (the same technique used in §2.2 Example 2.6); it gives slope \(4\) at \(x = 2\). The right-hand derivative uses the \(ax + b\) branch, whose derivative is the constant \(a\) (the slope of that line). For the two-sided limit of the difference quotient to exist, these must agree:

\[ a = 4. \]

Substituting \(a = 4\) into \(2a + b = 4\) gives \(b = -4\). With \(a = 4\) and \(b = -4\) the two pieces meet smoothly, and the difference quotient at \(x = 2\) has a well-defined limit equal to \(4\).

Higher derivatives

If \(f\) is differentiable, then its derivative \(f'\) is itself a function — and we can ask whether \(f'\) is differentiable. When it is, the derivative of \(f'\) is called the second derivative of \(f\), written \(f''\). Repeating the process gives the third derivative \(f'''\), the fourth derivative \(f^{(4)}\), and in general the \(n\)-th derivative \(f^{(n)}\):

\[ f,\quad f',\quad f'',\quad f''',\quad f^{(4)},\quad \ldots,\quad f^{(n)},\quad \ldots \]

Each is obtained by differentiating the previous one. By convention, \(f^{(0)} = f\) and \(f^{(1)} = f'\).

Higher derivatives are more than a formal exercise in differentiating again; the low-order ones carry concrete meaning. If \(s(t)\) is the position of a moving object, the first derivative \(s'(t)\) is its velocity and the second derivative \(s''(t)\) is its acceleration — the rate at which the velocity changes, felt as the push that presses you back when a car speeds up. The third derivative \(s'''(t)\), the rate of change of acceleration, is called the jerk; engineers keep it small when designing elevators and trains so that changes in acceleration come on smoothly rather than as an abrupt lurch.

Remark2.5Higher-derivative notation

In Leibniz notation the second derivative of \(y = f(x)\) is written

\[ f''(x) \;=\; \frac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}} \;=\; \frac{d^{2}f}{dx^{2}} \;=\; \frac{d^{2}}{dx^{2}}\,f(x), \]

and in general the \(n\)-th derivative is

\[ f^{(n)}(x) \;=\; \frac{d^{n}y}{dx^{n}}. \]

The superscript in \(d^{n}y/dx^{n}\) is not an exponent — it records how many times the differentiation operator \(d/dx\) has been applied. Prime notation \(f'', f'''\) is convenient for low orders; for \(n \ge 4\) the parenthesized form \(f^{(n)}\) avoids a forest of primes.

Caution

Read the second-derivative symbol as a single unit, not as a fraction to pull apart. The whole expression \(\dfrac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}\) stands for the operator \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\) applied twice to \(y\), and — as Remark 2.5 noted — the small \(2\)’s only count how many times we differentiate; they are not exponents. So \(d^{2}y\) means “\(d\) applied to \(y\) twice,” not \(d\) times \(2y\); and the \(dx^{2}\) is part of the symbol for differentiating twice with respect to \(x\) — not \(d(x^{2})\), where the \(x\) itself would be squared, and not an algebraic denominator to cancel or split.

Example2.14

Find \(f''(x)\) and \(f'''(x)\) for \(f(x) = x^{2} + 4x - 2\).

Solution

In Example 2.6 we found \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\). To get the second derivative, we differentiate \(f'\) from the definition:

\[ \begin{aligned} f''(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f'(x + h) - f'(x)}{h} \\[2pt] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\bigl[2(x + h) + 4\bigr] - \bigl[2x + 4\bigr]}{h} \\[2pt] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2h}{h} = 2. \end{aligned} \]

The second derivative is the constant \(2\) — the slope of the linear function \(f'(x) = 2x + 4\). Differentiating once more,

\[ f'''(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f''(x + h) - f''(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2 - 2}{h} = 0. \]

Every subsequent derivative is likewise \(0\): once we reach a constant function, further differentiation produces zero. In Leibniz notation, \(\dfrac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}} = 2\) and \(\dfrac{d^{3}y}{dx^{3}} = 0\).

With the concepts of differentiability, its tie to continuity, and higher-order derivatives in hand, we turn in the next sections to the rules that make differentiation efficient — systematic shortcuts that bypass the limit computation entirely.
