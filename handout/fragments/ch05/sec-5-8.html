5.8Curve Sketching

Everything this chapter built now converges on a single task: drawing the graph of a function from its formula alone, without plotting a cloud of points. Each tool contributes one layer. The domain says where the curve lives; limits at the edges and at infinity give its asymptotes; the first derivative marks where it rises and falls and locates its peaks and valleys; the second derivative says which way it bends and where it changes. Laid over one another, these layers determine the shape completely. What remains is to run them in a fixed order, so that nothing is missed.

Asymptotes

An asymptote is a straight line the curve approaches arbitrarily closely as \(x\) or \(y\) runs off to infinity. Three kinds arise: vertical asymptotes are detected by the infinite limits of Chapter 1, horizontal and slant asymptotes by limits as \(x \to \pm\infty\).

Definition5.7Asymptotes

The line \(x = a\) is a vertical asymptote of \(f\) if \(f(x) \to +\infty\) or \(-\infty\) as \(x \to a\) from one side or both.

The line \(y = L\) is a horizontal asymptote if \(f(x) \to L\) as \(x \to +\infty\) or \(x \to -\infty\).

The line \(y = mx + b\) (with \(m \ne 0\)) is a slant asymptote if \(f(x) - (mx + b) \to 0\) as \(x \to +\infty\) or \(x \to -\infty\): the curve draws ever closer to the line without leveling off.

Vertical-asymptote candidates come from zeros of a denominator or a logarithm’s argument approaching \(0\), confirmed by a one-sided infinite limit — a factor that cancels leaves a hole, not an asymptote; horizontal ones are read from the limit at infinity; a slant asymptote appears when a function grows linearly but is not itself a line — for a rational function, when the numerator’s degree is exactly one more than the denominator’s, so that dividing out leaves a linear part plus a remainder that decays.

Strategy5.6Sketching a curve

To sketch \(y = f(x)\), gather the following in order and then draw what they dictate:

  1. Domain. Where is \(f\) defined?
  2. Intercepts. The \(y\)-intercept \(f(0)\), and the \(x\)-intercepts where \(f(x) = 0\), when they are easy to find.
  3. Symmetry. Is \(f\) even (\(f(-x) = f(x)\), symmetric across the \(y\)-axis), odd (\(f(-x) = -f(x)\), symmetric through the origin), or periodic? Any symmetry halves the work.
  4. Asymptotes. Vertical (where \(f \to \pm\infty\)), horizontal (\(\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} f\)), and slant.
  5. Increase, decrease, and local extrema. From the sign of \(f'\) (§5.4).
  6. Concavity and inflection. From the sign of \(f''\) (§5.6).
  7. Assemble. Plot the intercepts, extrema, and inflection points, draw the asymptotes as guide lines, and connect the pieces so the rise-fall and concavity data are honoured.

Example5.24

Sketch the curve \(y = \dfrac{x^{2}}{x^{2} - 1}\).

Solution

Run the checklist.

Domain. The denominator vanishes at \(x = \pm 1\), so the domain is all \(x\) with \(x \ne \pm 1\). Intercepts. Both are at the origin: \(f(0) = 0\), and \(x^{2} = 0\) only at \(x = 0\). Symmetry. Replacing \(x\) by \(-x\) leaves the function unchanged, so it is even — symmetric across the \(y\)-axis.

Asymptotes. Near \(x = 1\) and \(x = -1\) the denominator \(\to 0\) while the numerator \(\to 1\), so \(f \to \pm\infty\): vertical asymptotes \(x = \pm 1\). At infinity,

\[ \lim_{x \to \pm\infty} \frac{x^{2}}{x^{2} - 1} = 1, \]

so \(y = 1\) is a horizontal asymptote. First derivative. By the quotient rule,

\[ \begin{aligned} f’(x) &= \frac{2x(x^{2} - 1) - x^{2}(2x)}{(x^{2} - 1)^{2}} = \frac{-2x}{(x^{2} - 1)^{2}}. \end{aligned} \]

The denominator is positive wherever it is defined, so \(f'\) has the sign of \(-2x\): positive for \(x < 0\), negative for \(x > 0\). Thus \(f\) increases on \((-\infty, -1)\) and \((-1, 0)\), decreases on \((0, 1)\) and \((1, \infty)\), with a local maximum at \((0, 0)\). Second derivative. Differentiating again and simplifying,

\[ f''(x) = \frac{2(3x^{2} + 1)}{(x^{2} - 1)^{3}}. \]

The numerator is always positive, so \(f''\) has the sign of \((x^{2} - 1)^{3}\), that is, of \(x^{2} - 1\): concave up on \((-\infty, -1)\) and \((1, \infty)\), concave down on \((-1, 1)\). There is no inflection point, since \(x = \pm 1\) are outside the domain.

Assemble. Between the asymptotes lies an arch peaking at the origin and falling toward \(-\infty\) at both \(x = \pm 1\); outside them, two branches descend from \(+\infty\) at the asymptotes toward the horizontal line \(y = 1\), staying above it. The even symmetry mirrors the left half onto the right.

Example5.25

Sketch the curve \(y = x + \dfrac{1}{x}\).

Solution

Domain. All \(x \ne 0\). Intercepts. None: \(f(0)\) is undefined, and \(x + 1/x = 0\) would need \(x^{2} = -1\). Symmetry. \(f(-x) = -x - 1/x = -f(x)\), so \(f\) is odd — symmetric through the origin.

Asymptotes. As \(x \to 0\), the term \(1/x \to \pm\infty\), so \(x = 0\) is a vertical asymptote. There is no horizontal asymptote, but the function is already split into a linear part and a decaying remainder: \(f(x) - x = 1/x \to 0\) as \(x \to \pm\infty\), so \(y = x\) is a slant asymptote. First derivative.

\[ f'(x) = 1 - \frac{1}{x^{2}}, \]

which is zero at \(x = \pm 1\), positive for \(|x| > 1\), and negative for \(0 < |x| < 1\). So \(f\) has a local maximum at \((-1, -2)\) and a local minimum at \((1, 2)\) — the maximum value lying below the minimum value, which is possible here because the two sit on branches separated by the asymptote. Second derivative.

\[ f''(x) = \frac{2}{x^{3}}, \]

positive for \(x > 0\) and negative for \(x < 0\): concave up on the right, concave down on the left, with no inflection (the sign change is across the excluded point \(x = 0\)).

Assemble. For \(x > 0\) the curve falls from \(+\infty\) along the vertical asymptote to the minimum \((1, 2)\), then rises toward the slant line \(y = x\); the odd symmetry produces the mirror-image branch for \(x < 0\), rising to the maximum \((-1, -2)\) and dropping toward \(y = x\) from below.

Caution

An asymptote describes where a curve is heading, not territory it may never touch. Both graphs in this section happen to stay on one side of their horizontal and slant asymptotes, which can plant a false rule: nothing prevents a curve from crossing such an asymptote — even infinitely often. The graph of \(y = (\sin x)/x\) has the horizontal asymptote \(y = 0\), yet it crosses that line at every nonzero multiple of \(\pi\). Vertical asymptotes are different in kind: they mark places where the nearby values blow up, not lines the graph runs alongside — usually the function is not even defined there, and an isolated value assigned at that point changes nothing about the blow-up. Approach is a statement about the far end of the graph; en route, anything is allowed.

With the checklist, a formula unfolds into a shape: no plotting, just the systematic reading of domain, limits, and two derivatives. Every question this chapter posed about a function’s behaviour — its rates, its extremes, its bends, its ends — now has a computable answer, and they combine into a single picture. The final section turns the machinery around. Instead of describing a known function, it asks how to find something a function conceals — a solution of \(f(x) = 0\) that no formula surrenders — by putting the tangent line to work as an engine of approximation.
