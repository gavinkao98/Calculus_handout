5.4Maximum and Minimum Values

Of all the questions calculus answers, the most insistent in practice is: where is a quantity largest or smallest? Maximum profit, minimum cost, least material, greatest range — each asks for an extreme value of a function, and the derivative is built to find them. The strategy rests on a simple observation made rigorous in Chapter 4: at a smooth peak or valley in the interior of a curve, the tangent is horizontal, so the derivative vanishes. Extremes therefore hide at a short list of places, and the work is to enumerate the list and test it.

Two kinds of extreme must be kept apart. Chapter 4 defined the absolute (or global) maximum and minimum of a function on a domain (Definition 4.3): the single largest and smallest values it attains anywhere on that domain. Alongside these sits a local notion — largest or smallest only in comparison with nearby points.

Definition5.3Local maximum and minimum

A function \(f\) has a local maximum (or relative maximum) at \(c\) if \(f(c) \ge f(x)\) for every \(x\) in the domain of \(f\) lying in some open interval around \(c\); it has a local minimum at \(c\) if \(f(c) \le f(x)\) for every such \(x\).

A local maximum is a point that is highest in its own neighbourhood — the top of one hill, not necessarily the highest hill. An absolute maximum is highest everywhere; a local one need only win locally.

Where extrema can occur: critical numbers

The engine that narrows the search is Fermat's theorem from Chapter 4 (Theorem 4.10): if \(f\) has a local extremum at an interior point \(c\) and \(f'(c)\) exists, then \(f'(c) = 0\). Contrapositively, a local extremum in the interior can occur only where the derivative is zero or fails to exist. Those places deserve a name.

Definition5.4Critical number

A critical number of \(f\) is a number \(c\) in the domain of \(f\) at which either \(f'(c) = 0\) or \(f'(c)\) does not exist.

Fermat's theorem, recast, reads: every interior local extremum occurs at a critical number. This is the workhorse — it shrinks the hunt for extrema from a whole interval to a finite list of suspects.

Caution

A critical number is a candidate, not a guarantee. The converse of Fermat's theorem is false: \(f'(c) = 0\) does not force an extremum at \(c\). The function \(f(x) = x^{3}\) has \(f'(0) = 0\), yet \(0\) is neither a maximum nor a minimum — the curve passes straight through with a momentary flat spot. Critical numbers must always be tested, never assumed to be extrema.

Example5.11

Find the critical numbers of \(f(x) = x^{3/5}(4 - x)\).

Solution

Expand and differentiate: \(f(x) = 4x^{3/5} - x^{8/5}\), so

\[ f'(x) = \tfrac{12}{5}x^{-2/5} - \tfrac{8}{5}x^{3/5} = \frac{4}{5}x^{-2/5}(3 - 2x) = \frac{4(3 - 2x)}{5\,x^{2/5}}. \]

Two kinds of critical number appear. Setting the numerator to zero, \(f'(x) = 0\) when \(3 - 2x = 0\), that is \(x = \tfrac{3}{2}\). And \(f'(x)\) does not exist where the denominator vanishes, at \(x = 0\) — yet \(x = 0\) is in the domain of \(f\) (indeed \(f(0) = 0\)), so it too is a critical number. The critical numbers are

\[ x = 0 \quad\text{and}\quad x = \tfrac{3}{2}. \]

Both must be kept: the first is where the tangent is vertical, the second where it is horizontal. Which, if either, is an extremum is a separate question, settled below.

Absolute extrema on a closed interval

On a closed interval the search closes completely. The Extreme Value Theorem (Theorem 4.9(a)) guarantees that a function continuous on \([a, b]\) attains an absolute maximum and minimum there; Fermat's theorem says each is either an interior critical number or an endpoint. So the absolute extremes are found by checking a finite list.

Strategy5.3The Closed Interval Method

To find the absolute maximum and minimum of a function \(f\) continuous on a closed interval \([a, b]\):

  1. Find the critical numbers of \(f\) that lie in the open interval \((a, b)\), and evaluate \(f\) at each.
  2. Evaluate \(f\) at the two endpoints \(a\) and \(b\).
  3. The largest of these values is the absolute maximum; the smallest is the absolute minimum.

Example5.12

Find the absolute maximum and minimum of \(f(x) = x^{3} - 3x^{2} + 1\) on \([-\tfrac{1}{2}, 4]\).

Solution

The function is a polynomial, hence continuous on the closed interval, so the method applies. Its derivative is

\[ f'(x) = 3x^{2} - 6x = 3x(x - 2), \]

which is defined everywhere and zero at \(x = 0\) and \(x = 2\); both lie in \((-\tfrac{1}{2}, 4)\). Evaluate \(f\) at these critical numbers and at the endpoints:

\[ f(-\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{8}, \qquad f(0) = 1, \qquad f(2) = -3, \qquad f(4) = 17. \]

The largest value is \(17\) and the smallest is \(-3\). So the absolute maximum is \(17\), attained at the endpoint \(x = 4\), and the absolute minimum is \(-3\), attained at the interior critical number \(x = 2\). Notice that the maximum sat at an endpoint, not a critical number — which is exactly why the endpoints must be checked.

Classifying local extrema: the First Derivative Test

The Closed Interval Method finds absolute extremes by brute evaluation, but says nothing about the character of an interior critical number — whether it is a local max, a local min, or neither. The sign of the derivative on either side settles it, and the Mean Value Theorem's monotonicity corollary (Corollary 4.3) is what makes the reasoning rigorous.

Theorem5.1First Derivative Test

Suppose \(f\) is continuous at a critical number \(c\), and differentiable on an open interval around \(c\) except possibly at \(c\) itself.

  • If \(f'\) changes from positive to negative at \(c\) — positive just left, negative just right — then \(f\) has a local maximum at \(c\).
  • If \(f'\) changes from negative to positive at \(c\), then \(f\) has a local minimum at \(c\).
  • If \(f'\) does not change sign at \(c\) (positive on both sides, or negative on both), then \(f\) has neither.

Proof

Fix a point \(u\) just left of \(c\) and a point \(v\) just right, both close enough that the stated sign conditions hold, so \(u < c < v\). On each side, \(f\) is continuous on the closed piece — \([u, c]\) or \([c, v]\) — and differentiable on its interior, so the monotonicity corollary (Corollary 4.3) governs each: applied to \(f\) directly wherever \(f' > 0\), and to \(-f\) wherever \(f' < 0\), since a negative derivative for \(f\) is a positive one for \(-f\), making \(-f\) strictly increase and \(f\) strictly decrease. Continuity of \(f\) at \(c\) lets each comparison reach the value \(f(c)\). The four sign patterns read off directly:

  • positive then negative: \(f\) strictly increases up to \(c\) and strictly decreases after, so \(f(u) < f(c)\) and \(f(v) < f(c)\) — a local maximum;
  • negative then positive: the reverse, \(f(u) > f(c)\) and \(f(v) > f(c)\) — a local minimum;
  • positive on both sides: \(f(u) < f(c) < f(v)\), so \(f\) climbs straight through \(c\) — neither;
  • negative on both sides: \(f(u) > f(c) > f(v)\), so \(f\) falls straight through — neither.

In the two changing cases \(f(c)\) beats its neighbours on both sides (or is beaten on both), delivering the local extremum; in the two unchanging cases \(f\) passes monotonically through \(c\), which is therefore neither.

Example5.13

Classify the local extrema of \(f(x) = x^{3} - 3x^{2} + 1\).

Solution

From \(f'(x) = 3x(x - 2)\), the critical numbers are \(x = 0\) and \(x = 2\). Track the sign of \(f'\) across them. The factors \(3x\) and \(x - 2\) give

\[ f'(x) > 0 \text{ on } (-\infty, 0), \qquad f'(x) < 0 \text{ on } (0, 2), \qquad f'(x) > 0 \text{ on } (2, \infty). \]

At \(x = 0\), \(f'\) changes from positive to negative: a local maximum, with value \(f(0) = 1\). At \(x = 2\), \(f'\) changes from negative to positive: a local minimum, with value \(f(2) = -3\). These are the same two interior values the Closed Interval Method turned up in Example 5.12; the First Derivative Test now explains their roles — the hill at \(x = 0\) and the valley at \(x = 2\) — independently of any interval.

The derivative has done its defining work: it flags where a function can turn, and its sign says which way. That is enough to solve genuine optimization problems, which the next section takes up — real quantities to maximize or minimize, with the modelling that turns a word problem into a function on an interval. A second, sometimes faster classifier — the sign of the second derivative — waits until §5.6, once concavity is in hand.
